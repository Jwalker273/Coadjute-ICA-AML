Why the Law Matters ‚Äî and Why You Are in It

AML is not just best practice. It is law. People go to prison for it. Understanding the legal and regulatory framework tells you exactly what you are obliged to do, what happens if you don't, and where those obligations come from.

Most people at Coadjute think of AML in practical terms ‚Äî checking IDs, screening names, filing reports. All of that is grounded in a legal framework that operates at three levels: international standards, national legislation, and sector-specific regulation. This module explains all three and shows how they connect.

The Three-Layer Framework

1

International ‚Äî FATF Standards

The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) sets the global standards. These are not law, but governments that sign up to them are expected to implement them through national legislation. FATF then evaluates whether they have.

2

National ‚Äî Primary Legislation (e.g. POCA 2002)

Parliament creates Acts of Parliament (primary legislation) that criminalise money laundering and create reporting obligations. These apply to everyone ‚Äî individual, firm, or organisation.

3

Sector ‚Äî Regulations & Guidance (e.g. MLR 2017)

Secondary legislation (Regulations) and regulator guidance flesh out the practical obligations for specific sectors ‚Äî banks, estate agents, conveyancers, and others. This is the layer that says how firms must comply.

üè† Coadjute Context

Coadjute operates at all three levels simultaneously. FATF sets the standard that flows into UK law (POCA 2002), which is implemented through the Money Laundering Regulations 2017. Estate agents and conveyancers are specifically named as regulated sectors under MLR 2017. When Coadjute carries out AML on their behalf, everything we do is rooted in this legal chain.

International Bodies: FATF, UNODC & the Global Network

The global fight against money laundering is coordinated by a network of international bodies. FATF is the most important ‚Äî but it is not alone. Knowing who does what is an ICA exam staple.

1. UNODC ‚Äî Where It All Started

The UN Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC), headquartered in Vienna, was the UN's original response to drug money laundering. Before FATF existed, it was the UNODC that led international efforts to stop criminals cleaning proceeds of the global drugs trade.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî UNODC
  • Headquarters: Vienna. 22 field offices globally plus a New York representative office.
  • Funded 90% by voluntary contributions, mainly from governments.
  • Until 1 October 2002, it was called the Office for Drug Control and Crime Prevention.
  • Its Global Programme Against Money Laundering (GPML) helps member states: draft legislation, develop strategies, raise public awareness, and coordinate AML initiatives with other international organisations.
  • Three main activities: research & analysis; helping countries implement treaties and draft domestic legislation; field-based/on-the-ground assistance including training judicial officials.

2. FATF ‚Äî The Global Standard Setter

The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) is the worldwide leading standard-setter for AML and counter-financing of terrorism. It was established in 1989 ‚Äî originally as a G7 initiative ‚Äî with the objective of setting standards and promoting their implementation globally.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî FATF Key Facts
  • Inter-governmental body established in 1989.
  • Currently 40 member jurisdictions (Russia's membership was suspended on 24 February 2023).
  • Nine FATF-Style Regional Bodies (FSRBs) extend its network ‚Äî meaning over 200 jurisdictions worldwide have committed to the FATF Recommendations.
  • Known globally for its 40 Recommendations ‚Äî last updated November 2023.
  • FATF conducts mutual evaluations of member countries ‚Äî peer reviews assessing whether they have implemented the Recommendations in both law and practice.
  • Forthcoming assessments evaluate not just legislative structure but how well it is being implemented ‚Äî moving from technical compliance to effectiveness.

FATF's High-Risk & Monitored Jurisdictions

Three times per year, FATF publishes two lists identifying countries with weak AML/CFT measures. These are critical for your risk assessments:

DocumentFormer NameWhat It Means
High-Risk Jurisdictions subject to a Call for Action"Public Statement" / "Black List"Serious AML weaknesses. Enhanced due diligence required for any connection to these jurisdictions. Transactions may need to be declined.
Jurisdictions under Increased Monitoring"Grey List" / "Improving Global Compliance"Working with FATF to address weaknesses. Higher scrutiny required but not necessarily a bar on transactions.

3. Other Key International Bodies

Egmont Group

A network of Financial Intelligence Units (FIUs) from over 160 countries. Enables cross-border information sharing ‚Äî so that FIUs can share intelligence on suspected money laundering and terrorist financing with their counterparts in other jurisdictions. The UK's FIU is the UKFIU within the NCA.

Wolfsberg Group

13 major international banks (including Barclays, HSBC, Deutsche Bank, Goldman Sachs) who develop private-sector AML guidance and standards. Their Correspondent Banking Due Diligence Questionnaire (CBDDQ) is widely used in correspondent banking relationships. No regulatory authority ‚Äî but highly influential in banking practice.

Basel Committee on Banking Supervision

Part of the Bank for International Settlements (BIS). Issues guidance on AML risk management for banks. Its Customer Due Diligence for Banks guidelines are globally referenced.

IMF and World Bank

Both provide technical assistance to help developing countries build AML frameworks. The IMF in particular assesses countries' AML frameworks as part of its broader financial stability evaluations.

‚ÑπÔ∏è Key Distinction: Hard Law vs Soft Law

FATF Recommendations, Wolfsberg guidance, and Basel Committee papers are all "soft law" ‚Äî they are not directly enforceable but set the standards that inform hard law (like POCA 2002 and MLR 2017). In practice, regulators evaluate firms against what these bodies say best practice looks like, so soft law has real teeth.

The EU Money Laundering Directives

Although the UK has left the EU, the EU Directives shaped UK AML law ‚Äî and they remain important for understanding the global regulatory direction of travel. Many Coadjute clients will have counterparts in EU jurisdictions.

The History of EU Directives ‚Äî A Quick Timeline

1

1st Money Laundering Directive ‚Äî June 1991

Focused on drug money. Required banks and financial institutions to identify customers and report suspicious transactions.

2

2nd Directive ‚Äî December 2001

Extended scope to lawyers, accountants, estate agents, notaries, and casinos. Brought the legal/property professions into the regulated sector for the first time.

3

3rd Directive ‚Äî May 2005

Implemented the revised FATF 2003 Recommendations. Introduced the risk-based approach (RBA) ‚Äî the principle that firms should focus scrutiny where risk is highest.

4

4th Directive (4MLD) ‚Äî June 2015 / Transposed June 2017

Significantly strengthened requirements. Introduced beneficial ownership registers. The UK implemented this as the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 (MLR 2017).

5

5th Directive (5MLD) ‚Äî June 2018 / Applied January 2020

Aimed at greater transparency. Key changes: publicly accessible UBO registers; brought cryptocurrency exchanges under scope; reduced prepaid card limits; central bank account registers.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Key 5MLD Changes
  • UBO Registers: Centralised Ultimate Beneficial Owner (UBO) registers were made publicly accessible. EU member states required to connect their UBO lists for cross-border verification.
  • Cryptocurrency: A legal definition of cryptocurrency was introduced. Cryptocurrency exchanges and wallet providers were brought within the scope of AML regulations for the first time.
  • Prepaid Cards: Transaction limits reduced to ‚Ç¨150 (‚Ç¨50 for online). Anonymous electronic money cannot fund a prepaid instrument. Transactions from prepaid cards outside the EU are prohibited unless issued in an AML-equivalent territory.
  • Central Registers: All EU member states required to maintain centralised national bank and payment account registers, accessible by FIUs.

The UK After Brexit

When the UK left the EU on 31 December 2020, existing EU sanctions and Directive-based requirements were converted into UK law through various legislative processes. The key framework ‚Äî MLR 2017 (implementing 4MLD) ‚Äî remained in force and has since been amended to incorporate elements of 5MLD.

‚ö†Ô∏è Brexit Divergence Risk

The EU is developing a 6th Money Laundering Directive and a new pan-EU AML Authority (AMLA). The UK will no longer automatically align with these changes. Over time, the UK and EU frameworks may diverge ‚Äî particularly around beneficial ownership transparency and crypto-asset regulation. Firms with operations in both jurisdictions need to track both.

üè† Property Sector ‚Äî Why Estate Agents Were First Regulated in 2001

The 2nd EU Directive in 2001 brought estate agents within the regulated sector. The reason? Property is uniquely attractive for money laundering ‚Äî high value, tangible asset, internationally tradeable. At Coadjute we work with estate agents who are under this regulatory umbrella ‚Äî the roots of which trace directly to that 2001 Directive. Understanding this helps explain why estate agents face regulatory obligations that might initially seem surprising for a sales business.

What the MLR 2017 Requires of Regulated Firms

MLR 2017 is the framework we operate within day-to-day. It is worth knowing its key requirements precisely because they describe what Coadjute must do on behalf of estate agents and conveyancers.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî MLR 2017 Core Requirements
  • Appointment of a Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO) / Nominated Officer at senior management level
  • Conduct a firm-wide risk assessment and implement a risk-based approach
  • Customer Due Diligence (CDD) ‚Äî identify and verify clients and beneficial owners; understand the nature of the business relationship
  • Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) for high-risk situations (PEPs, high-risk jurisdictions, complex structures)
  • Ongoing monitoring of business relationships and transaction scrutiny
  • Record-keeping ‚Äî minimum five years retention for CDD records and supporting evidence
  • Written policies and procedures, kept up to date
  • Staff training ‚Äî regular, effective, appropriate to role
  • Implementation of an internal SAR reporting system
  • Regular AML audit/effectiveness review (e.g. every two years)

Important: Failure to implement these measures is itself a criminal offence ‚Äî even if no actual money laundering has occurred. A firm can be prosecuted simply for failing to have adequate procedures in place.

The Money Laundering Offences Under POCA 2002

POCA 2002 creates four criminal offences that are directly relevant to people working in the property sector. These are not abstract legal concepts ‚Äî they describe acts that estate agents, conveyancers, and technology providers could commit in the course of ordinary commercial activity.

The Four POCA Offences

s.327 ‚Äî POCA 2002
Concealing

Concealing, disguising, converting, transferring, or removing criminal property from the UK. This is the classic laundering act ‚Äî turning dirty money into clean assets.

s.328 ‚Äî POCA 2002
Arranging

Entering into or becoming concerned in an arrangement that facilitates the acquisition, retention, use or control of criminal property by another person. The key offence for property professionals ‚Äî arranging transactions.

s.329 ‚Äî POCA 2002
Acquisition, Use & Possession

Acquiring, using, or having possession of criminal property. For example, a conveyancer who takes a fee payment from funds that are the proceeds of crime could commit this offence.

s.330 ‚Äî POCA 2002
Failure to Report

Failure to disclose knowledge or suspicion (or reasonable grounds to suspect) that another person is engaged in money laundering, where the knowledge or suspicion came through a business in the regulated sector. Maximum 5 years imprisonment.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The Mental Element (Mens Rea)

All three primary offences (s.327, s.328, s.329) require a particular state of mind. The person must have been:

  • Knowing ‚Äî they definitively knew the property was criminal; OR
  • Suspecting ‚Äî they personally suspected the property was criminal; OR
  • Having reasonable grounds to suspect ‚Äî a reasonable professional in their position ought to have suspected, whether or not they personally did.

The third element ‚Äî reasonable grounds to suspect ‚Äî is the most important for regulated professionals. It means ignorance is not automatically a defence. If the red flags were obvious and you ignored them, you may have committed an offence.

Why s.328 (Arranging) Is Critical for Coadjute

üìã Scenario ‚Äî The s.328 Risk in Property

An estate agent instructs Coadjute to process AML checks on a buyer. The buyer's funds pass initial scrutiny and the transaction proceeds to completion. Six months later it emerges that the buyer was using proceeds of a large-scale fraud and that, with hindsight, there were significant red flags in the CDD that should have triggered further investigation.

The question the authorities will ask: did the individuals involved ‚Äî the estate agent, the conveyancer, and potentially those processing the AML checks ‚Äî know, suspect, or have reasonable grounds to suspect that the transaction involved criminal property? If yes, they may have committed the s.328 arranging offence.

This is not hypothetical. Property professionals have been prosecuted under s.328 in the UK.

What Is "Criminal Property"?

Criminal property is defined broadly under POCA 2002. It is property that:

  • constitutes a person's benefit from criminal conduct, OR
  • represents such a benefit (whether directly or indirectly)

And crucially ‚Äî the defendant knows or suspects it constitutes such a benefit. The definition covers the proceeds of any criminal conduct ‚Äî not just serious organised crime. Tax evasion, benefit fraud, even a minor criminal offence could generate criminal property.

üî¥ Common Misconceptions About Criminal Property
  • "The crime happened abroad" ‚Äî Irrelevant in many cases. Dual criminality (conduct criminal both in the UK and the foreign jurisdiction) can still trigger POCA offences.
  • "The crime was years ago" ‚Äî No limitation period for money laundering. Historical criminal proceeds can still be criminal property today.
  • "This is a long-standing client" ‚Äî Existing client relationships present as great a ML risk as new ones ‚Äî often greater because complacency sets in.
  • "The amount is small" ‚Äî POCA has no minimum threshold. Any amount of criminal property triggers the offences.

The Tipping Off Offence (s.333A POCA)

A separate but related offence under POCA ‚Äî covered in more detail in Module 6 ‚Äî is tipping off. It is committed when a person who knows or suspects a SAR has been made discloses information to another person in a way that is likely to prejudice any investigation.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Tipping Off s.333A
  • The offence can be committed before or after a SAR is made ‚Äî as long as the offender knows or suspects that a SAR exists or an investigation is underway.
  • The disclosure can be to anyone ‚Äî not just the suspect. This includes family members, lawyers, and journalists.
  • The obligation not to tip off overrides the normal duty of client confidentiality.
  • Important nuance: Making routine due diligence enquiries of a client does not constitute tipping off, even if it alerts them to scrutiny. Enquiries are often key to uncovering criminal activity.

Knowledge, Suspicion & Wilful Blindness

The entire structure of ML criminal liability turns on a question of knowledge and suspicion. The law has developed a sophisticated framework for assessing what a person knew, should have known, or deliberately chose not to know. This is one of the most tested areas in the ICA exam.

The Three Types of Knowledge

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Forms of Knowledge Under POCA
  • Actual subjective knowledge ‚Äî You definitively know a fact. For example, a client has told you directly that the money comes from drug dealing. This is the clearest form ‚Äî but rarest in practice.
  • Wilfully shutting your eyes to the obvious (wilful blindness) ‚Äî You suspect something is wrong but deliberately avoid asking questions or making enquiries that would confirm it. The classic example: you don't ask the client about the source of funds because you think the answer might mean you have to file a report. Courts treat this as equivalent to actual knowledge.
  • Wilfully and recklessly failing to make enquiries ‚Äî An honest and reasonable person in your position would have made enquiries. You failed to make them. This also falls within the concept of knowledge in many jurisdictions.

The Legal Definition of "Suspicion"

The courts have grappled with defining suspicion ‚Äî because it is, by nature, subjective and personal. Two key formulations from case law:

üìñ Court Definitions of Suspicion

"A degree of satisfaction and not necessarily amounting to belief but at least extending beyond speculation as to whether an event has occurred or not."

"Although the creation of suspicion requires a lesser factual basis than the creation of a belief, it must nonetheless be built upon some foundation."

‚Äî JMLSG, Prevention of money laundering/combating terrorist financing

In plain English: suspicion is more than a vague feeling, but less than certainty. You don't need to know something is criminal ‚Äî you need to believe it's possible enough that you would be comfortable saying "I suspected this." And that suspicion must be based on something ‚Äî a combination of red flags, inconsistencies, or contextual factors.

The Suspicion Spectrum

The ICA places knowledge and suspicion on a spectrum from comfort through concern to suspicion ‚Äî and describes the process as gradual, rarely arising from a single event:

‚úÖ Comfort

Normal client activity. CDD is consistent with business profile. No red flags. Proceed as normal.

‚ö†Ô∏è Concern

Something unusual has arisen. Investigate further ‚Äî review CDD, discuss with colleagues, consider making discreet enquiries of the client. This is not yet a reporting obligation.

üî¥ Suspicion

Concern has crystallised into a belief that ML may be occurring, built on some factual foundation. Report to the MLRO immediately.

Objective vs Subjective Suspicion

TestWhat It AsksWho It Protects / Risks
SubjectiveDid this person actually form a suspicion that ML was occurring?Protects the genuinely innocent ‚Äî but not the wilfully blind.
ObjectiveShould a reasonable professional in this position, with this information, have suspected ML?The higher standard. Prosecutors use this. Ignorance of obvious red flags is not a defence.
üî¥ The Objective Test ‚Äî Why It Changes Everything

The objective test means the following scenario is possible: an employee genuinely claims they didn't suspect anything. But a court looks at the information available to them ‚Äî the client's unexplained wealth, the offshore funds, the contradictions in their story ‚Äî and concludes that a reasonable professional in that position ought to have suspected ML. The employee can be convicted on the objective test even if they honestly say they had no suspicion.

This is why training, awareness, and documented red-flag assessment matter: they demonstrate that a professional engaged with the information available to them in good faith.

Does Seniority Matter?

A common assumption is that only senior staff or compliance professionals have meaningful reporting obligations. The ICA is explicit in rejecting this:

‚úÖ Key ICA Principle ‚Äî Junior Staff

The reporting obligation applies universally ‚Äî to every employee in the regulated sector, regardless of seniority or role. A junior administrator who notices an inconsistency has the same obligation to report to the MLRO as a senior relationship manager. Do not assume that more senior colleagues are handling it ‚Äî if you have a suspicion, it is your obligation to report.

The ICA explicitly notes: "Do not assume that, because there is somebody more senior than you involved in the client relationship or sign-off process, that this means that your concern/suspicion does not count ‚Äî it does."

The Duty to Report, Client Confidentiality & the DAML

Three of the most practically important legal concepts for property professionals: the legal obligation to report, how it interacts with client confidentiality, and the Defence Against Money Laundering that allows transactions to proceed safely.

The Duty to Report ‚Äî s.330 POCA 2002

Section 330 of POCA 2002 creates a criminal offence of failure to report. It applies when a person in the regulated sector knows, suspects, or has reasonable grounds to suspect that another person is engaged in money laundering ‚Äî and fails to disclose this to the MLRO (or directly to the NCA) as soon as practicable.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî s.330 Failure to Report
  • Maximum sentence: 5 years imprisonment plus a fine.
  • Applies to all employees in regulated businesses ‚Äî not just the MLRO.
  • The threshold is knowledge, suspicion, or reasonable grounds to suspect ‚Äî proof is not required.
  • The obligation is to report as soon as practicable ‚Äî delay itself can constitute the offence.
  • The obligation arises from information obtained in the course of a business in the regulated sector.
  • Defence: a person is not guilty if they had a reasonable excuse for not disclosing, or if they are a legal professional and the information came to them in privileged circumstances.

The Conflict with Client Confidentiality

Most professions ‚Äî solicitors, accountants, advisers ‚Äî have a strong duty of client confidentiality. Filing a SAR about a client appears to conflict directly with this duty. The law resolves this conflict explicitly:

‚úÖ Safe Harbour ‚Äî Reporting Overrides Confidentiality

The law provides complete protection against any action for breach of client confidentiality when you report knowledge or suspicion of criminal conduct in good faith. This is known as the safe harbour provision. You cannot be sued for breach of confidentiality as a result of making a good-faith SAR.

The obligation to report overrides the duty of confidentiality ‚Äî it does not just create an exception to it. Where the two conflict, reporting wins.

Client Confidentiality vs Tipping Off

While confidentiality is overridden by the duty to report, there is an equal and opposite obligation not to tip off. This creates a practical tension in property transactions where clients inevitably ask questions about delays:

üìã Scenario ‚Äî Handling Client Questions After a SAR

A SAR has been filed mid-transaction. The client calls asking why completion has been delayed. You cannot:

  • Tell the client a report has been filed
  • Hint that the transaction is "under investigation"
  • Say "we have concerns about the source of your funds"

You can:

  • Explain there are routine internal compliance checks underway
  • Indicate there is an administrative delay without further detail

The ICA is clear: a carefully worded explanation about internal compliance checks is appropriate. Never indicate that a SAR exists.

The DAML ‚Äî Defence Against Money Laundering

The DAML is one of the most important practical tools for property professionals. It is the mechanism by which a firm can legally proceed with a transaction even where it suspects criminal property is involved.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The DAML Process
  • A DAML is a request for consent from the NCA before proceeding with a transaction suspected of involving criminal property.
  • Without DAML consent, proceeding with such a transaction could constitute the s.328 (arranging) or s.329 (acquisition/possession) offence.
  • The NCA has 7 working days to grant or refuse consent from the date the SAR is received.
  • If the NCA refuses consent, a further moratorium period of 31 days follows ‚Äî during which the NCA can seek a restraint order. After 31 days, if no order is made, the firm may proceed.
  • If the NCA does not respond within 7 days, the firm has what is called the "limited intelligence value" consent and may proceed.
  • Filing a DAML is a defence against prosecution ‚Äî it demonstrates good faith engagement with the authorities.
üè† Coadjute Context ‚Äî DAML in Property Transactions

Property transactions are uniquely problematic for DAMLs because they are time-sensitive. Exchange dates and completion dates are contractually fixed. A SAR filed mid-transaction can create a 7‚Äì38 working day pause that collapses the chain or results in claims for breach of contract.

The key implication: robust upfront CDD is far preferable to a mid-transaction DAML. If the red flags are identified and addressed early ‚Äî before exchange ‚Äî the disruption is manageable. A post-exchange DAML, conversely, puts everyone in an extremely difficult position.

This is one of the most compelling commercial arguments for proper AML: it protects the transaction, not just the firm's legal compliance.

Protecting the Identity of the Reporter

Employees sometimes fear retaliation or legal consequences from clients if they file SARs. The law and case law both address this concern directly:

‚öñÔ∏è HSBC v Shah (Court of Appeal, 2012)

Shah filed a complaint against HSBC for delaying payments and sought to discover who had filed the SAR against him. The UK Court of Appeal ruled that a bank does not have to disclose the identity of employees who make internal SARs. The name of the SAR reporter should not be disclosed ‚Äî even to regulatory or law enforcement authorities.

This case provides strong protection for employees who report in good faith. Staff should be confident that their identity is protected when they raise concerns.
