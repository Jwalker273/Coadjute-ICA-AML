Why the Law Matters: The Three-Layer Framework

AML law doesn't come from one place. It flows through three distinct layers ‚Äî international standards, national legislation, and regulatory rules. Understanding how they connect is the foundation of everything else in this module.

The Three Layers

When we talk about AML law, we're actually talking about a system of overlapping obligations. Think of it as three concentric rings, each one becoming more specific and enforceable as you move inward.

LayerWhat it isExampleEnforceability
1. International StandardsGlobal benchmarks set by bodies like FATF. Countries agree to comply, but they are not directly enforceable law.FATF's 40 RecommendationsSoft law ‚Äî enforced through peer review and reputational pressure
2. National Legislation (Primary)Acts of Parliament that create criminal offences and legal obligations for individuals and companies.Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 (POCA)Hard law ‚Äî criminal prosecution, imprisonment, unlimited fines
3. Secondary Legislation / RegulationStatutory instruments and regulations that set practical obligations for regulated firms.Money Laundering Regulations 2017 (MLR 2017)Hard law ‚Äî regulatory sanctions, civil penalties, criminal liability
üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî FATF Recommendations Are Not Law

This is a commonly tested distinction. FATF Recommendations are soft law ‚Äî they do not directly create enforceable legal obligations in the UK. They only become law when Parliament implements them through legislation. The Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 is the primary legislation that criminalises money laundering. The Money Laundering Regulations 2017 implement FATF standards into UK regulatory requirements for supervised firms.

How the Layers Connect at Coadjute

Coadjute operates at all three levels simultaneously. FATF sets the standard that flows into UK law (POCA 2002), which is further implemented through the Money Laundering Regulations 2017. Estate agents and conveyancers are specifically named as regulated sectors under MLR 2017. When Coadjute carries out AML on their behalf, everything we do is rooted in this legal chain.

üè† Coadjute Context

When HMRC inspects an estate agency's AML compliance, it is assessing compliance with MLR 2017 (Layer 3). But the reason MLR 2017 looks the way it does is because it implements the EU's 4th Money Laundering Directive (which itself reflects FATF standards). You can't fully understand any one layer without knowing where it came from.

International Bodies: FATF, UNODC & the Global Network

The global fight against money laundering is coordinated by a network of international bodies. FATF is the most important ‚Äî but it is not alone. Knowing who does what is an ICA exam staple.

1. UNODC ‚Äî Where It All Started

The UN Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC), headquartered in Vienna, was the UN's original response to drug money laundering. Before FATF existed, it was the UNODC that led international efforts to stop criminals cleaning the proceeds of the global drugs trade.

The UNODC administers the UN Convention Against Illicit Traffic in Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances (the Vienna Convention) 1988 ‚Äî the first international treaty to require countries to criminalise money laundering. It also administers the Palermo Convention 2000, which extended money laundering offences beyond drug proceeds to all serious crimes.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî UNODC Key Facts
  • Headquarters: Vienna
  • Administered the Vienna Convention (1988) ‚Äî first ML criminalisation requirement
  • Administered the Palermo Convention (2000) ‚Äî extended ML to all serious crime proceeds
  • The UNODC estimates that global ML is equivalent to 2‚Äì5% of global GDP annually

2. FATF ‚Äî The Global Standard-Setter

The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) is the most important international body in AML. Established in 1989 by the G7 summit in Paris, it sets the international standards that all countries are expected to implement.

FATF's primary output is its 40 Recommendations ‚Äî a framework covering everything from how countries should criminalise ML and TF, to how they should regulate financial institutions and pursue international cooperation. These were most recently updated in November 2023.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî FATF Key Facts
  • Established: 1989 (G7 Paris Summit)
  • 40 Recommendations last updated: November 2023
  • Operates a Mutual Evaluation programme ‚Äî peer reviews of member countries' AML frameworks
  • High-Risk countries (Blacklist) ‚Äî "call for action"; subject to enhanced due diligence or counter-measures
  • Increased Monitoring list (Greylist) ‚Äî countries working to fix identified AML deficiencies
  • FATF Recommendations are soft law ‚Äî not directly enforceable until Parliament implements them

3. FATF-Style Regional Bodies (FSRBs)

FATF cannot monitor every country directly. Instead, it works through a network of regional bodies ‚Äî FATF-Style Regional Bodies (FSRBs) ‚Äî that apply the same standards in their geographic areas. Examples include:

  • MONEYVAL ‚Äî Council of Europe member states not in FATF
  • MENAFATF ‚Äî Middle East and North Africa
  • GIABA ‚Äî West Africa
  • APG ‚Äî Asia-Pacific

4. The Egmont Group

The Egmont Group is an international network of Financial Intelligence Units (FIUs) ‚Äî the bodies in each country that receive and analyse suspicious activity reports. In the UK, that is the National Crime Agency (NCA).

The Egmont Group provides a secure channel for FIUs to share financial intelligence across borders. This is critical when ML schemes operate across multiple jurisdictions ‚Äî which they almost always do.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Egmont Group
  • A network of Financial Intelligence Units (FIUs)
  • Enables cross-border intelligence sharing between FIUs
  • UK FIU: the National Crime Agency (NCA)
  • Named after the Egmont-Arenberg Palace in Brussels, where the first meeting was held in 1995

5. The Wolfsberg Group

The Wolfsberg Group is a private sector body ‚Äî an association of 13 major global banks. It produces guidance and principles on AML best practice for financial institutions. While Wolfsberg guidance is not legally binding, it is influential and widely followed across the industry.

Named after the Wolfsberg Castle in Switzerland where the founding banks first met in 2000.

EU Money Laundering Directives: A Progressive Framework

The EU has driven much of UK AML law through a series of Money Laundering Directives. Each one went further than the last. Knowing what each Directive introduced ‚Äî and what they mean post-Brexit ‚Äî is regularly tested in the ICA exam.

The Five EU Directives

1

1st Directive ‚Äî June 1991

Required banks and financial institutions to identify customers and report suspicious transactions. Applied only to drug money laundering at this stage.

2

2nd Directive ‚Äî December 2001

Extended scope to lawyers, accountants, estate agents, notaries, and casinos. Brought the legal and property professions into the regulated sector for the first time. Also extended the predicate offences beyond drug trafficking.

3

3rd Directive ‚Äî May 2005

Implemented the revised FATF 2003 Recommendations. Introduced the risk-based approach (RBA) ‚Äî the principle that firms should focus scrutiny where risk is highest rather than applying identical checks to everyone.

4

4th Directive (4MLD) ‚Äî June 2015 / Transposed June 2017

Significantly strengthened requirements. Introduced beneficial ownership registers. Placed strong emphasis on the risk-sensitive approach to CDD. The UK implemented this as the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 (MLR 2017).

5

5th Directive (5MLD) ‚Äî June 2018 / Applied January 2020

Aimed at greater transparency. Publicly accessible UBO registers; brought cryptocurrency exchanges under AML scope; reduced prepaid card transaction limits; required central bank account registers in all member states.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Key 5MLD Changes
  • UBO Registers: Ultimate Beneficial Owner (UBO) registers made publicly accessible. EU member states required to connect their lists for cross-border verification.
  • Cryptocurrency: A legal definition of cryptocurrency introduced. Cryptocurrency exchanges and wallet providers brought within AML regulations for the first time.
  • Prepaid Cards: Transaction limits reduced to ‚Ç¨150 (‚Ç¨50 for online). Anonymous electronic money cannot fund a prepaid instrument.
  • Central Registers: All EU member states required to maintain centralised national bank and payment account registers, accessible by FIUs.
üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî 2nd Directive & Property Professionals

The 2nd Money Laundering Directive (2001) was the first to bring estate agents and legal professionals (conveyancers, notaries, solicitors) within the scope of AML regulation. Before 2001, property professionals had no formal AML obligations under EU law. This is specifically tested ‚Äî if asked when estate agents first came under AML regulation, the answer is 2nd Directive, 2001.

The UK After Brexit

When the UK left the EU on 31 December 2020, existing EU sanctions and Directive-based requirements were converted into UK law through the European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018. The MLR 2017 ‚Äî which implemented 4MLD ‚Äî remains in force. The UK has also incorporated the substantive measures of 5MLD.

However, the UK is no longer bound to follow future EU Directives. Any changes in UK AML law will now come through domestic legislative processes rather than EU implementation.

üè† Coadjute Context ‚Äî Post-Brexit Divergence

The EU is developing a 6th Money Laundering Directive and an Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA) to centralise EU AML supervision. The UK will not be part of this. Over time, there may be meaningful divergence between UK and EU AML requirements ‚Äî something firms operating across both markets will need to monitor.

The UK National Framework: POCA, MLR 2017 & the Terrorism Act

Three pieces of UK legislation sit at the heart of AML compliance. Each does a different job. The ICA exam tests not just that you know these exist ‚Äî but that you understand what each one does, who it applies to, and why it matters.

The Core Legislation

ActWhat it doesWhy it matters to us
Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 (POCA) Creates the principal money laundering criminal offences (s.327, 328, 329). Sets the duty to report (s.330). Establishes the consent/DAML regime. Applies to everyone ‚Äî not just regulated firms. The foundation of everything. Non-compliance is a criminal offence, not just a regulatory failure. Maximum sentence: 14 years imprisonment.
Terrorism Act 2000 (TACT) Creates terrorist financing offences. Creates a separate but parallel duty to report TF suspicions. Parallel to POCA for TF. The duty to report is the same ‚Äî different legislation, same consequence.
Money Laundering Regulations 2017 (MLR 2017) Implements 4MLD. Sets practical AML obligations for regulated sectors ‚Äî CDD, record keeping, training, internal controls, MLRO appointment. Estate agents and conveyancers are named regulated sectors. This is the framework Coadjute implements CDD against. Breach is a criminal offence.

Other Supporting Legislation

  • Serious Organised Crime and Police Act 2005 (SOCPA) ‚Äî Contains certain defences to ML offences for overseas conduct and streamlined certain POCA provisions.
  • Policing and Crime Act 2017 ‚Äî Strengthened OFSI's powers; increased maximum custodial sentence for sanctions breaches to seven years.
  • Economic Crime (Transparency and Enforcement) Act 2022 ‚Äî Introduced the Register of Overseas Entities (ROE), requiring overseas entities owning UK property to declare beneficial owners to Companies House.
  • Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023 ‚Äî Further reforms to Companies House, new failure-to-prevent fraud offence, and enhanced powers for investigating economic crime.

What the MLR 2017 Requires of Regulated Firms

MLR 2017 is the framework we operate within day-to-day. It requires regulated firms to:

  • Carry out Customer Due Diligence (CDD) on clients (Regulations 27‚Äì38)
  • Conduct ongoing monitoring of business relationships (Regulation 28)
  • Appoint a nominated officer (MLRO) to receive and consider internal disclosures
  • Implement written AML policies, controls, and procedures
  • Train staff on ML risks, legal obligations, and how to recognise and report suspicions
  • Keep adequate records of CDD and transaction data
  • Conduct a documented firm-wide risk assessment

Supervisory Authorities

Under MLR 2017, each regulated sector is overseen by a designated supervisor:

SectorSupervisor
Banks, insurers, investment firmsFinancial Conduct Authority (FCA)
Estate agentsHM Revenue & Customs (HMRC)
Solicitors (England & Wales)Solicitors Regulation Authority (SRA)
AccountantsICAEW / ACCA / HMRC (depending on membership)
CasinosGambling Commission
Crypto asset businessesFinancial Conduct Authority (FCA)
üè† Coadjute Context ‚Äî HMRC Supervision of Estate Agents

Estate agents are supervised by HMRC ‚Äî not the FCA. HMRC has the power to conduct inspections, issue civil penalties, and in serious cases refer matters for criminal prosecution. When Coadjute assists estate agents with their AML obligations, we are helping them meet the requirements that HMRC will evaluate them against. An HMRC inspection that finds inadequate CDD can result in significant fines.

The Money Laundering Offences Under POCA 2002

POCA 2002 creates four criminal offences that are directly relevant to people working in the property sector. These are not abstract legal concepts ‚Äî they describe acts that estate agents, conveyancers, and technology providers could commit in the course of ordinary commercial activity.

The Four POCA Offences

s.327 ‚Äî POCA 2002
Concealing

Concealing, disguising, converting, transferring, or removing criminal property from the UK. This is the classic laundering act ‚Äî turning dirty money into clean assets.

s.328 ‚Äî POCA 2002
Arranging

Entering into or becoming concerned in an arrangement that facilitates the acquisition, retention, use or control of criminal property by another person. The key offence for property professionals ‚Äî arranging transactions.

s.329 ‚Äî POCA 2002
Acquisition, Use & Possession

Acquiring, using, or having possession of criminal property. For example, a conveyancer who takes a fee payment from funds that are the proceeds of crime could commit this offence.

s.330 ‚Äî POCA 2002
Failure to Report

Failure to disclose knowledge or suspicion (or reasonable grounds to suspect) that another person is engaged in money laundering, where the knowledge or suspicion came through work in the regulated sector. Maximum 5 years imprisonment.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The Mental Element (Mens Rea)

All three primary offences (s.327, s.328, s.329) require a particular state of mind. The person must have been:

  • Knowing ‚Äî they definitively knew the property was criminal; OR
  • Suspecting ‚Äî they personally suspected the property was criminal; OR
  • Having reasonable grounds to suspect ‚Äî a reasonable professional in their position ought to have suspected, whether or not they personally did.

The third element ‚Äî reasonable grounds to suspect ‚Äî is the most important for regulated professionals. It means that claiming "I didn't personally think anything was wrong" is not automatically a complete defence.

What Is "Criminal Property"?

A key element of all three primary offences is that the property involved must constitute or represent a person's benefit from criminal conduct. The accused must know or suspect this.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Misconceptions
  • "The crime happened years ago" ‚Äî Historical criminal proceeds are still criminal property today. There is no time limit.
  • "This is a long-standing client" ‚Äî Existing client relationships present as great a ML risk as new ones ‚Äî often greater, because complacency sets in.
  • "The amount is small" ‚Äî POCA has no minimum threshold. Any amount of criminal property triggers the offences.

The Tipping Off Offence (s.333A POCA)

A separate but related offence under POCA ‚Äî covered in depth in Module 6 ‚Äî is tipping off. It is committed when a person who knows or suspects a SAR has been made discloses information to another person in a way that is likely to prejudice any investigation.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Tipping Off s.333A
  • The offence can be committed before or after a SAR is made ‚Äî as long as the offender knows or suspects a SAR exists or an investigation is underway.
  • The disclosure can be to anyone ‚Äî not just the suspect. This includes family members, lawyers, and journalists.
  • The obligation not to tip off overrides the normal duty of client confidentiality.
  • Important nuance: Making routine due diligence enquiries of a client does not constitute tipping off, even if it alerts them to scrutiny.

Knowledge, Suspicion & Wilful Blindness

The entire structure of ML criminal liability turns on a question of knowledge and suspicion. The law has developed a sophisticated framework for assessing what a person knew, should have known, or deliberately chose not to know. This is one of the most tested areas in the ICA exam.

The Three Types of Knowledge

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Forms of Knowledge Under POCA
  • Actual subjective knowledge ‚Äî You definitively know a fact. For example, a client has told you directly that the money comes from crime. This is the clearest form ‚Äî but rarest in practice.
  • Wilfully shutting your eyes to the obvious (wilful blindness) ‚Äî You suspect something is wrong but deliberately avoid asking questions that would confirm it. Classic example: you don't ask about source of funds because you think the answer might mean you have to file a report. Courts treat wilful blindness similarly to actual knowledge.
  • Recklessness ‚Äî You recognise a risk that the property is criminal but proceed anyway without making enquiries. Enquiries are often key to uncovering criminal activity.

The Objective Test

The concept of "reasonable grounds to suspect" introduces an objective test ‚Äî not just "what did you actually think?" but "what would a reasonable professional in your position have thought?" This is the most practically important standard for property sector professionals.

‚ÑπÔ∏è The Reasonable Professional Standard

A court assessing whether a regulated professional had "reasonable grounds to suspect" will look at:

  • What information was available to the professional at the time
  • What industry red flags existed that a trained professional should have recognised
  • Whether the professional made sufficient enquiries given what they knew
  • What a competent colleague in the same role would have done

This standard is not about perfection ‚Äî it is about professional competence and reasonable diligence.

What Is "Suspicion"?

The ICA notes that suspicion is a lower threshold than belief or knowledge. A suspicion is a thought or feeling, however vague, that something might be wrong. You do not need to be certain; you do not need to know the specific predicate offence. If you have a feeling something might not be right, the safe course is always to report to your MLRO.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Suspicion Threshold

The courts have interpreted "suspicion" as going beyond a mere vague feeling of unease. In R v Da Silva [2006], the Court of Appeal held that suspicion requires a "settled" state of mind ‚Äî a possibility that has been thought about and not dismissed. However, it falls well short of belief or certainty. You do not need to prove criminality ‚Äî you need only a genuine possibility that funds are from crime.

Junior Staff and the Reporting Obligation

A common misconception is that more junior staff can defer to seniors when it comes to suspicion. The ICA is explicit in rejecting this.

‚úÖ Key ICA Principle ‚Äî Junior Staff

The reporting obligation applies universally ‚Äî to every employee in the regulated sector, regardless of seniority or role. A junior administrator who notices an inconsistency has the same obligation to report to the MLRO as a senior relationship manager. Do not assume that, because there is somebody more senior than you involved in the client relationship, your concern doesn't count ‚Äî it does.

The Duty to Report, Client Confidentiality & the DAML

Three of the most practically important legal concepts for property professionals: the legal obligation to report, how it interacts with client confidentiality, and the Defence Against Money Laundering that allows transactions to proceed safely.

The Duty to Report ‚Äî s.330 POCA 2002

Section 330 of POCA 2002 creates a criminal offence of failure to report. A person in the regulated sector commits this offence if they:

  • Know, suspect, or have reasonable grounds to suspect that another person is engaged in money laundering; and
  • The information came to them in the course of their activities in the regulated sector; and
  • They fail to make a disclosure to the MLRO (or directly to the NCA) as soon as practicable.

Maximum sentence: 5 years imprisonment.

Client Confidentiality vs. The Reporting Obligation

A question that property professionals often ask is: "Do I have to report even if it breaches client confidentiality?" The answer is unambiguous: yes. The statutory duty to report overrides client confidentiality.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Confidentiality & Disclosure

The duty to report under s.330 POCA is a statutory obligation that takes precedence over the common law duty of confidentiality owed to clients. This means a solicitor, estate agent, or conveyancer must file a SAR even if doing so would disclose information that the client provided in confidence. Not filing because of confidentiality concerns is not a defence.

The Safe Harbour ‚Äî Authorised Disclosure

A person who suspects they may have already committed a primary ML offence (s.327, 328, or 329) faces a dilemma: how can they continue, freeze, or exit the transaction without committing the offence? The law provides a safe harbour through authorised disclosure.

Under s.338 POCA, a person can make an authorised disclosure to the NCA before undertaking the act they suspect constitutes an offence. This creates a full defence ‚Äî even if the property later turns out to be criminal.

The DAML ‚Äî Defence Against Money Laundering

An Appropriate Consent (commonly called a DAML ‚Äî Defence Against Money Laundering) is the mechanism through which a firm asks the NCA for permission to proceed with a transaction that they suspect may involve criminal property.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The DAML Process & Timings
  • The firm submits a SAR to the NCA requesting consent to proceed.
  • The NCA has 7 working days to respond. If no response is received within 7 working days, consent is deemed granted and the firm may proceed.
  • If the NCA refuses consent within 7 days, a moratorium period of 31 calendar days begins. During this period, the firm must not proceed with the transaction.
  • If the 31-day moratorium expires without further action from the NCA, the firm may then proceed.
  • The 7-day and 31-day periods are specifically tested in the ICA exam.

Tipping Off and Prejudicing an Investigation

Once a SAR has been filed ‚Äî or even once you suspect one has been filed ‚Äî you must not tell the subject of the SAR, or anyone else, in a way that could prejudice the investigation. This is the tipping off offence under s.333A POCA.

In practice, this means freezing a transaction without explanation can be difficult. The standard approach is to cite internal compliance review requirements, administrative delays, or incomplete documentation ‚Äî without disclosing that a SAR has been filed.

üè† Case Note ‚Äî Backed by Case Law

In Barclays Bank plc v Various Claimants [2018], the court confirmed that a bank (or by extension, any regulated firm) has a right to freeze a transaction or account where they have a genuine suspicion of ML, without breaching contractual duties to the customer. The name of the SAR reporter should not be disclosed ‚Äî even to regulatory or law enforcement authorities. This provides strong protection for employees who report in good faith.
