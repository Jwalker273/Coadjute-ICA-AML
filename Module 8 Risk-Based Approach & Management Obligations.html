The Risk-Based Approach

The Risk-Based Approach (RBA) is the cornerstone of modern AML regulation. Rather than applying identical controls to every customer and transaction, the RBA requires firms to understand their own risk landscape and concentrate resources where money laundering risk is greatest.

Why the RBA Exists

Regulators including FATF and the EU have increasingly promoted risk-based controls for AML because a one-size-fits-all approach is both inefficient and ineffective. The logic is straightforward: all firms have finite resources, and those resources will achieve maximum effect if targeted at the highest-risk areas.

The 4th Money Laundering Directive (4MLD) places particular emphasis on the concept that all firms must adopt a "risk-sensitive" approach to CDD. This means that senior management must actively identify and assess money laundering risks, then take steps to mitigate and monitor those risks on an ongoing basis.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî RBA Definition

"A risk-based approach means that firms ensure that their systems and controls are proportionate to their particular risks of money laundering."

The aim is to move away from a 'one-size-fits-all' approach. Firms facing lower risk may apply simplified due diligence; firms facing higher risk must apply enhanced due diligence. Critical caveat: the RBA is not an opportunity to avoid appropriate identification, verification and monitoring ‚Äî all required standards must still be met.

üè† Coadjute Context ‚Äî RBA in Property AML

What does risk-sensitivity mean for estate agents and conveyancers?

At Coadjute, we apply the RBA on behalf of our estate agent and conveyancer clients. In practice, this means we don't treat a first-time buyer purchasing a ¬£180,000 flat with a mortgage the same way we treat a cash buyer purchasing a ¬£3.5 million property through a corporate structure registered in a non-EEA jurisdiction. The former may qualify for simplified CDD; the latter demands enhanced due diligence and potentially a Defence Against Money Laundering (DAML) SAR.

The RBA is what justifies that distinction ‚Äî and it's what protects our clients from being prosecuted for applying CDD proportionately.

The Four Components of a Risk-Based Approach

The ICA identifies four key components that together constitute a proper RBA framework. All four must be present ‚Äî missing any one of them undermines the entire structure.

üí° The Four RBA Components (Exam Essential)
  1. Risk Assessment ‚Äî A detailed, documented understanding of the money laundering and terrorist financing risks your firm faces, and the level of risk you consider acceptable (your risk appetite).
  2. Risk Mitigation ‚Äî A comprehensive set of policies and procedures designed to mitigate identified risks as far as possible. Note: it is unlikely you can remove all risk entirely.
  3. Effective Implementation ‚Äî The policies and procedures must actually be put into practice. A policy that sits in a drawer serves no purpose.
  4. Monitoring & Review ‚Äî A comprehensive ongoing monitoring process that identifies further improvements, tests whether controls are working, and adjusts the framework as the risk landscape evolves.

Risk Assessment: What Factors Must You Consider?

Before you can mitigate risk, you must understand it. The ICA sets out the key risk factors that should inform a firm's risk assessment:

Risk FactorQuestions to Ask
Product typesWhat services do we offer? Which carry higher ML risk?
Distribution channelsDo we deal with clients face-to-face or remotely? Via intermediaries?
JurisdictionsWhere are our clients based? Where do funds originate? FATF blacklist/greylist?
Customer typesAre customers individuals, companies, trusts, PEPs, charities?
Transaction volumes/sizesLarge cash transactions? High volumes that make individual scrutiny harder?
Risk appetiteWhat level of residual risk is the firm willing to accept?
üè† Property Sector Risk Factors in Practice

In residential property, Coadjute's risk assessment considers: whether the purchase is cash or mortgage-funded (cash is higher risk); whether the buyer is a UK resident individual or an overseas corporate entity; whether the property price is significantly above or below market value; whether there are multiple parties in the chain with complex ownership structures; and whether any solicitor or estate agent in the chain has previously been flagged for AML concerns.

Risk Mitigation

Once risks are identified, controls must be designed to mitigate them. The ICA is clear: you cannot eliminate risk entirely, but you must reduce it as far as possible. Controls must be proportionate ‚Äî applying maximum scrutiny to every transaction would make business unworkable, but applying no scrutiny creates criminal exposure.

Mitigation in practice means applying SDD to lower-risk business, standard CDD to the majority of customers, and EDD to higher-risk categories. It also means setting up internal reporting lines, training staff, maintaining records and screening against sanctions lists.

Risk Monitoring: A Continuous Process

The RBA is not a one-off exercise. Risk profiles change ‚Äî a customer who was standard risk when onboarded may become high risk following a change in their business activities, a media report, or a change in the political situation in their home country. Controls that were adequate last year may be inadequate today as criminal typologies evolve.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistake

Firms sometimes conduct an excellent initial risk assessment and then leave it untouched for years. This is dangerous. The ICA is explicit that monitoring must be continuous ‚Äî the risk profile must be regularly reviewed to ensure it remains accurate, and controls must be updated accordingly.

Record-Keeping: The Audit Trail

The fourth element of the RBA ‚Äî record-keeping ‚Äî is often underestimated. A firm that has conducted a thorough risk assessment, implemented appropriate controls and monitored them effectively is in a strong position with regulators, but only if it can prove it. Without documentation, none of the above happened as far as the regulator is concerned.

Records of risk assessments, control decisions, monitoring activity and the management information produced from that monitoring must all be retained. Effective record-keeping provides assurance to regulators and gives senior management a powerful tool for developing and improving systems and controls.

Module 08 ‚Äî Section 2

Formulating an AML Policy

Every organisation subject to AML regulation must have a formal AML and CFT policy, approved at the most senior level. This isn't a box-ticking exercise ‚Äî a well-crafted policy is a public statement of an organisation's ethics, its risk appetite, and its commitment to preventing financial crime.

Why the Policy Matters

The ICA makes a striking point: an organisation's attitude towards combating money laundering is increasingly viewed as indicative of the ethics with which it conducts all of its business. In other words, how seriously you take AML says something about how you run your company generally ‚Äî and regulators, law enforcement, clients and counterparties are all watching.

A formal AML policy serves as a clear statement of corporate intent, communicated to four audiences:

  • Employees ‚Äî so they understand the firm's obligations and their own role
  • Clients ‚Äî so they know what to expect during onboarding and throughout the relationship
  • Regulatory authorities ‚Äî demonstrating that the firm takes compliance seriously
  • Law enforcement ‚Äî signalling willingness to cooperate where required
üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Policy Approval Level

The ICA is explicit: the AML policy must be approved at the most senior level within the organisation ‚Äî typically the board of directors. This is not a matter for the compliance function alone. Senior-level approval signals genuine corporate intent rather than a delegated compliance exercise.

The "Window Dressing" Warning

The ICA includes a specific and pointed warning about a common failure mode: firms that simply pay external consultants to draft an AML policy, which is then adopted at board level without any internal consideration or genuine discussion.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Window Dressing Problem

A policy produced without meaningful internal input risks being irrelevant to the firm's actual operations and risks. Staff won't recognise themselves in it; procedures won't map to it; and when a regulator examines it, the gap between the policy and practice will be immediately apparent. The ICA's phrase for this: such policies "do little more than window dress."

The danger is compounded when a regulator or law enforcement authority discovers that an organisation has failed to comply with its own policy. That is far more damaging than having no policy at all, because it shows the firm's stated standards existed but were simply ignored.

What Must an AML Policy Contain?

The ICA sets out a minimum framework that management must address when formulating a policy. It begins with four analytical questions about the organisation itself:

  • The nature, scale and complexity of its business
  • The diversity of its operations, including geographical diversity
  • The volumes and sizes of its transactions
  • The degree of ML/TF risk associated with each area of operation

Having answered those questions, the policy should then set out at least the following ten commitments:

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Ten Minimum Policy Elements
  1. Compliance with all applicable legislation, secondary legislation and best-practice guidance
  2. Cooperation with law enforcement and investigating authorities where required
  3. Respect for client confidentiality ‚Äî breached only where legally required
  4. Clearly defined responsibilities and accountabilities for AML prevention within the business
  5. A statement of the firm's willingness to provide sufficient resources for AML work
  6. Acceptance of new business only subject to appropriate CDD and risk assessment
  7. Continuation of existing business only where CDD, risk assessment and monitoring requirements are met
  8. A clear approach to staff education, training and awareness maintenance
  9. A statement of the firm's attitude towards persistent non-compliance with AML procedures
  10. An indication of the prevailing cultural attitude the firm wishes to create around AML prevention

Going Beyond the Minimum

The ICA notes that it is always open to a firm to exceed these minimum requirements. In fact, for firms operating across multiple jurisdictions, a sensible approach is to adopt "global standards" that meet the requirements of the most stringent national framework with which the firm must comply. Attempting to play jurisdictions off against each other ‚Äî sometimes called "jurisdiction shopping" or "regulatory arbitrage" ‚Äî is described by the ICA as short-sighted, and from an organisational perspective makes no sense.

üè† Coadjute Context ‚Äî What Our AML Policy Covers

At Coadjute, our AML policy sets out our commitment to conducting AML on behalf of estate agents and conveyancers to the standard required by the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 and the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002. It defines the role of our MLRO, the CDD standards we apply, the process for internal suspicious activity reporting, and our training obligations. Every member of staff who interacts with AML processes must understand this policy ‚Äî not just be aware it exists.

Module 08 ‚Äî Section 3

AML Procedures in Practice

An AML policy sets out the "what" and "why" ‚Äî the organisation's goals and commitments. AML procedures translate those commitments into the "how" ‚Äî the practical, day-to-day operational steps that staff actually follow. Without robust procedures, even the best policy is just words.

The Relationship Between Policy and Procedures

The ICA draws a clear distinction: the policy is the statement of intent; procedures are the operational engine. Some procedures are required by secondary legislation (the MLRs); others are designed to give the firm more robust protection than the minimum legal requirement. Both types matter.

A MLRO designing an effective internal AML regime should build procedures that cover every stage of the client relationship lifecycle ‚Äî from initial onboarding through to exit ‚Äî and every type of risk that the firm has identified in its risk assessment.

Client Identification and CDD Procedures

The first and most fundamental category of procedures covers how the firm identifies and verifies clients. The ICA sets out a comprehensive list of what this must include:

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî What CDD Procedures Must Cover
  • Establishment and satisfactory verification of the identity of all clients ‚Äî both natural persons and legal entities
  • Identification of any person with a beneficial interest in the property or assets handled
  • Identification of any person able to exercise control over the property
  • Documented CDD information including: source of funds, source of wealth, commercial rationale for the relationship, occupation, nature of business interests, and identity of any beneficial owners

Risk Assessment Procedures for New Clients

Before accepting a new client, the firm must assess their risk profile against a range of factors. The ICA identifies eleven risk dimensions to consider:

#Risk DimensionExample Consideration
iGeographyIs the client based in, or transacting through, a FATF-blacklisted or greylist country?
iiNature of business interestsAre they in a high-risk sector (construction, cash-intensive business)?
iiiSensitive activitiesAre they defined as sensitive by the regulator?
ivValue of assetsIs the transaction value consistent with known income/wealth?
vClient typeIs this a listed company (lower risk) or an HNW individual (higher scrutiny)?
viPEP statusIs the individual or their family a Politically Exposed Person?

Operational AML Procedures: The Full List

Beyond CDD, the ICA sets out a substantial list of operational procedures that a well-designed AML regime should include. Many of these may surprise people ‚Äî particularly the annual leave requirement:

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Required AML Procedures (Learn This List)
  • Independent authorisation prior to acceptance of new clients
  • Documented client acceptance procedures
  • Verification of intermediaries and business introducers
  • CDD profile creation for each client
  • 30-, 60- or 90-day client reviews for consistency with CDD profile
  • Risk-based ongoing monitoring of relationship activity
  • Enhanced due diligence procedures for higher-risk clients
  • Automatic transaction-based reporting for unusual transactions
  • Annual client reviews ‚Äî ideally by someone other than the relationship manager
  • A "meet the client" policy (face-to-face requirement for certain client types)
  • Written internal suspicious transaction/relationship reporting system
  • Dual authorisation policy for significant transactions
  • Full supporting documentation for all payment requests
  • Checklists for fund receipts/payments flagging movements from/to non-compliant jurisdictions
  • Annual MLRO reports to the board on risk status and controls
  • Register of Powers of Attorney and third-party signatories
  • Procedures for managing files relating to suspect relationships
  • PEP policy including board approval for accepting PEPs
  • Procedure for exceptions and deviations ‚Äî requiring independent risk assessment and approval
  • Comprehensive staff education and training strategy
  • Procedures to verify staff awareness and competence
  • Staff minimum annual leave requirement (to detect fraud and suspicious activity concealed by the employee)
  • AML audits
  • Regular cross-checking of client names against sanctions lists and embargoed jurisdictions
  • Monitoring of fund transfers from/to FATF non-compliant jurisdictions or shell banks
  • Verification of signatures procedure
  • Record-keeping procedures
üìã Why Annual Leave Is an AML Control

The "Absence Exposes Fraud" Principle

The requirement that all staff take a minimum level of annual leave is a well-established fraud and AML control ‚Äî yet many people are surprised to see it on an AML procedures list. The logic is straightforward: an employee engaged in suspicious activity ‚Äî whether fraudulent or in facilitating money laundering ‚Äî may need to be at their desk to maintain the deception. Enforced absence creates a gap in which colleagues or managers may pick up their responsibilities and notice irregularities. Several significant financial fraud cases have been uncovered precisely because the perpetrator was unexpectedly absent.

üí° Procedures Must Be Dynamic, Not Static

"Once an AML framework is in place it needs to be continually monitored, tested, reported upon and adjusted. An effective internal system of control is not fixed or a one-off event; instead it is dynamic and constantly evolving to take account of changing threats and emerging weaknesses." ‚Äî ICA

Monitoring Compliance with the Policy

Adopting a policy is only the beginning. The harder challenge ‚Äî and the one where many organisations fall down ‚Äî is ensuring that the policy is actually followed in practice. This is where the MLRO's monitoring role becomes critical.

The Monitoring Obligation

The ICA states clearly: once a policy is adopted, it must be communicated, implemented, and adherence to it must be monitored. All three elements are essential. A policy that is communicated but not implemented is useless. A policy that is implemented but not monitored will drift over time as staff take shortcuts and pressures build.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The MLRO's Monitoring Role

Management should expect the MLRO (or Money Laundering Prevention Officer ‚Äî MLPO) to:

  • Monitor compliance with the AML policy on an ongoing basis
  • Report regularly to the board on the organisation's performance against that policy
  • Provide this information as part of the MLRO Annual Report

Where the firm is failing to comply with its own policy, management must be able to formulate appropriate remedial action, and a clear record of those enhancements must be retained.

When Management Is Part of the Problem

The ICA acknowledges a particularly difficult scenario: what happens when individual members of management have themselves contributed to the organisation's failure to comply with its own policy? This creates an obvious conflict of interest in the remedial process and may require escalation beyond the immediate management team ‚Äî potentially to the board, or to the regulator.

üìã Case Study ‚Äî The Fund Manager and the VIP Client

When Policy Is Bypassed for a "Important" Client

A is an employee of a fund management company. X is a very important investor in a range of the firm's funds. The fund rules state explicitly that redemptions must always be paid back to the "originating account" ‚Äî the account from which the funds were received, as a core anti-laundering control.

X requests that a large redemption be paid to a different account. A's manager ‚Äî unwilling to upset a VIP client ‚Äî authorises the deviation from procedure without following the exceptions process. The transfer is made. A month later, it emerges that the recipient account belongs to a sanctioned entity.

The ICA's point: The manager who authorised the bypass is personally exposed. The objective test of suspicion means the law can disregard explanations like "they were an important client" or "it wasn't my call." If a reasonable person in that role should have applied the procedure, the failure to do so is legally significant.

What the Compliance Function Should Provide

Management should look to the compliance function or MLRO to provide regular reports covering:

  • The way in which controls are functioning within the business
  • Whether those controls are operating adequately
  • Where controls are inadequate, specific recommendations for adjustment to the risk control framework
  • Documentation of all enhancements made

The MLRO Annual Report

The MLRO Annual Report is the key document through which the MLRO reports to the board on the effectiveness of the firm's AML framework. It typically covers the number of internal SARs received and filed externally, any regulatory developments requiring policy changes, training completion rates, significant compliance incidents, and an assessment of whether the current risk rating framework remains appropriate.

üè† Coadjute Context ‚Äî Monitoring in a PropTech Context

At Coadjute, monitoring compliance means ensuring that our AML checks are completed to the required standard across every transaction we process. Our MLRO is responsible for reviewing the quality of CDD decisions, tracking any cases where enhanced due diligence was triggered and how it was resolved, and reporting to leadership on the overall AML performance of the platform. Where we identify systematic gaps ‚Äî for example, a category of transaction where CDD is consistently weaker ‚Äî we adjust our procedures and document the change.

Module 08 ‚Äî Section 5

Resources & the Objective Test

AML compliance costs money and staff time. In commercial organisations under pressure to generate revenue, the compliance function is often viewed as a cost centre or even a "business prevention" function. The ICA is uncompromising on this point ‚Äî inadequate resourcing of AML functions is not a defence.

The Resource Obligation

One of the ten minimum elements of an AML policy is a statement of the organisation's willingness to provide sufficient resources for money laundering prevention. This is more than a commitment on paper ‚Äî it requires that in practice, the AML function has enough skilled staff, enough time, and enough technology to perform its functions adequately.

The pressures on resources are real. High transaction volumes, staff turnover, competing commercial priorities, and cost pressures can all lead to AML functions being under-resourced. This creates risk ‚Äî both regulatory risk and criminal risk for individual employees.

‚ö†Ô∏è "I Was Too Busy" Is Not a Defence

The ICA highlights the legal concept of the objective test of suspicion. This test assesses not what the individual actually knew or suspected, but what a reasonable person in their position ought to have known or suspected. Crucially, factors such as volume of work, lack of resources, and time pressure are irrelevant to this test. If a reasonable person in the same role, facing the same facts, should have been suspicious ‚Äî the law treats that individual as having had grounds for suspicion, regardless of how busy they were.

üìã Case Study ‚Äî Director C and the Routine Authorisation

The Danger of "Rubber Stamping"

Director C is responsible for authorising transactions across 600 client company bank accounts. On a particularly busy day, a payment instruction crosses her desk. Under pressure and running late for a meeting, she authorises it without scrutinising it in detail ‚Äî she trusts that the client relationship manager has checked it. A fellow director co-signs, relying on Director C's apparent review rather than performing an independent check.

One month later, it emerges that the transfer was made to an account in the name of a company featured on a list of suspect organisations linked to money laundering.

The objective test: Could a reasonable person in Director C's role, with access to the same information, have spotted warning signs? If yes ‚Äî volume of work is irrelevant. Both Director C and her co-signatory may face personal liability.

The ICA's broader point: this case should prompt management to ask whether they are "cutting corners at the expense of protecting a business and its employees from exposure to money laundering activity."

Staff Training as a Resource Requirement

Resources are not just about headcount. Training is a critical resource, and it's specifically required under the MLRs. Staff who don't understand how to identify suspicious activity, how to complete CDD, or how to make an internal report cannot do their jobs effectively ‚Äî regardless of how much time they have.

The ICA emphasises that training must be:

  • Comprehensive ‚Äî covering all relevant staff, not just those in designated "compliance" roles
  • Ongoing ‚Äî initial training is not sufficient; awareness must be maintained as regulations and typologies evolve
  • Verified ‚Äî firms must have procedures to check that staff have completed and understood training
üè† Coadjute Context ‚Äî This Course as a Resource

By completing the ICA Certificate in AML, Coadjute staff develop the technical knowledge needed to perform AML functions to a high standard. The investment in this training programme is a direct fulfilment of the firm's policy commitment to providing sufficient resources for AML prevention.

The Dual Authorisation Control

One specific resource-related procedure worth highlighting is the dual authorisation policy ‚Äî requiring two separate individuals to authorise significant transactions. This is both a fraud control and an AML control: it prevents any single individual from processing a suspicious transaction unilaterally, and it creates a second opportunity for suspicion to be identified and raised. As the Director C case study illustrates, however, dual authorisation only works if both signatories actually perform independent scrutiny ‚Äî not if one rubber-stamps the other's apparent approval.

Corporate Culture & Tone from the Top

Rules, policies and procedures are necessary ‚Äî but they're not sufficient. The most important determinant of whether an AML framework actually works is the culture of the organisation. Culture is what happens when no one is watching. It's the difference between staff who file internal SARs because they understand why it matters and staff who avoid doing so because they fear upsetting a fee-generating relationship.

Why Culture Is Hard to Get Right

Unlike a policy document or a procedure, culture is intangible. You can't point to it in a folder. You can't audit it on a checklist. The ICA acknowledges that culture is therefore often overlooked when organisations assess the quality of their AML controls ‚Äî and that this oversight is dangerous, because negative cultural attitudes can be just as damaging as gaps in written procedures.

The challenge is particularly acute in commercial organisations that exist to generate business and revenue. AML procedures can come to be seen as obstacles ‚Äî "business prevention" ‚Äî rather than as tools that protect both the firm and its people. A genuinely positive AML culture reframes this: good AML controls help firms attract good clients and repel bad ones.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Culture Obligations

"It is vitally important that everybody within an organisation, of whatever seniority, appreciates the important role that they can play in protecting the organisation against the risk."

Management must:

  • Encourage staff to communicate concerns to the MLRO ‚Äî even against contrary management pressure
  • Make the MLRO approachable and easily accessible
  • Ensure there is no environment of fear around raising suspicions

The Four Indicators of Poor Compliance Culture

The ICA identifies four specific warning signs that a firm's culture is working against its AML objectives:

‚ö†Ô∏è Signs of a Poor AML Culture
  1. "My suspicion doesn't count" ‚Äî Junior staff assume that because they're remote from senior client relationships, any concern they have will already have been spotted by someone more senior. This is usually false. Junior staff are often closer to transactional activity than their managers.
  2. Management pressure to transact ‚Äî When managers make clear, explicitly or implicitly, that completing a deal is more important than AML compliance, staff will behave accordingly. This is one of the most dangerous cultural failures.
  3. "Compliance is not my responsibility" ‚Äî The view that AML is a compliance team problem, not something that everyone in the business owns. This attitude leads to risk being concentrated in the compliance function while operational staff disengage.
  4. Territorial attitudes towards key clients ‚Äî Relationship managers or senior staff who resist scrutiny of their clients, block enhanced due diligence, or fail to pass on suspicious transaction reports because they don't want their client relationships disrupted.

The "My Suspicion Doesn't Count" Problem in Detail

This is the most pervasive cultural problem in larger organisations, and the ICA addresses it specifically. Junior staff process transactions. They see the detail. A senior relationship director may never actually look at a specific payment instruction ‚Äî a junior analyst does. That analyst's suspicion is as legally significant as the director's. If the analyst spots something that a reasonable person in their position should report and doesn't because they assume "someone more senior must have already dealt with this" ‚Äî they are personally exposed under the objective test.

üìã Case Study ‚Äî The Junior Analyst Who Said Nothing

Assuming Someone Else Has Spotted It

A junior compliance analyst at an estate agency notices that a buyer has provided source of funds documentation showing income from a business in a jurisdiction on FATF's grey list. She also notices that the purchase price is significantly above the local market rate. She doesn't raise it internally because the buyer has been introduced by a senior director who has worked with them for years ‚Äî she assumes the director has already conducted enhanced due diligence.

No one else has raised it. The transaction completes. Eighteen months later, the buyer is investigated for money laundering.

The question: Should the analyst have raised an internal SAR? Under the objective test, almost certainly yes. Her assumption that someone senior must have dealt with it is not a defence. The director's long relationship with the client doesn't immunise the transaction from scrutiny ‚Äî if anything, it is itself a warning sign of the "territorial attitudes" cultural indicator.

Tone from the Top

Culture is set by leadership. Tracey McDermott, formerly of the FCA, made a famous speech that the ICA uses to close this topic ‚Äî a fitting reminder that AML is a shared professional and societal obligation, not just a regulatory burden:

"Let's make it [the financial services industry] a hostile place for the criminal." ‚Äî Tracey McDermott, formerly of the FCA

For this to be more than a slogan, senior management must actively model the culture they want: celebrating staff who raise internal SARs rather than treating it as a nuisance; backing the MLRO when they push back on commercial pressure; and making clear that AML compliance is non-negotiable at every level of the business.

üí° Summary: What Good AML Culture Looks Like
  • Every staff member understands that AML is part of their job ‚Äî not just the compliance team's problem
  • Staff at all levels feel safe and encouraged to raise concerns and file internal SARs
  • The MLRO is accessible, respected, and backed by senior management
  • Management does not apply pressure ‚Äî explicit or implicit ‚Äî to complete transactions that raise compliance concerns
  • Key client relationships do not receive reduced AML scrutiny compared to other clients
  • AML procedures are seen as tools for protecting the business, not barriers to revenue
üè† Coadjute Context ‚Äî Culture in a PropTech Team

At Coadjute, our AML culture means that every team member who touches a transaction ‚Äî whether they're reviewing CDD documents, processing data, or building the systems that support AML checks ‚Äî understands that their role contributes to preventing financial crime. We don't expect every person to be an AML expert, but we do expect everyone to know how to escalate a concern and to feel confident doing so. That's the standard the ICA sets, and it's the standard we hold ourselves to.
