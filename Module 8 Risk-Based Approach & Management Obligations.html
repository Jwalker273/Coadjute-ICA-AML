The Risk-Based Approach

The Risk-Based Approach (RBA) is the cornerstone of modern AML regulation. Rather than applying identical controls to every customer and transaction, the RBA requires organisations to understand their own risk landscape and direct resources where money laundering risk is greatest.

Why the RBA Exists

Regulators ‚Äî from FATF to the EU to the FCA ‚Äî have moved firmly towards risk-based controls because a one-size-fits-all approach is both inefficient and ineffective. Treating a UK-listed company buying a commercial property with the same level of scrutiny as a cash-paying overseas individual with no verifiable source of funds is not a sensible use of time or resource. Worse, it dilutes attention away from genuinely high-risk transactions.

The ICA is clear that the RBA should operate at every level: not just at onboarding, but throughout the entire client relationship. A client who was low risk when they first instructed your firm may become higher risk over time as their circumstances change.

üè† At Coadjute: Why the RBA Matters for You

When we assess a property transaction on behalf of an estate agent or conveyancer, we are making a risk-based judgement. A cash buyer whose funds come from the sale of a previous property and who is purchasing in their own name is lower risk than a buyer using a recently incorporated offshore company with opaque beneficial ownership. The RBA is what justifies treating them differently ‚Äî and it's what ensures we apply enhanced scrutiny where it genuinely matters.

The Four Components of a Risk-Based Approach

The ICA identifies four key components that together constitute a proper RBA framework. All four must be present ‚Äî missing any one undermines the entire structure.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The Four RBA Components
  1. Risk Assessment ‚Äî A detailed, documented understanding of the ML/TF risks your firm faces, and the level of risk you consider acceptable (your risk appetite).
  2. Risk Mitigation ‚Äî A comprehensive set of policies and procedures designed to mitigate identified risks as far as possible. It is unlikely any risk can be removed entirely.
  3. Effective Implementation ‚Äî The policies and procedures must actually be put into practice. A policy that sits in a drawer serves no purpose.
  4. Monitoring & Review ‚Äî An ongoing process that identifies improvements, tests whether controls are working, and updates the framework as the risk environment evolves.

Risk Assessment: What Must You Consider?

Before risk can be mitigated, it must be understood. The ICA sets out the key risk factors a firm's risk assessment should address:

Risk FactorQuestions to Ask
Product typesWhat services do we offer? Which carry higher ML risk? (e.g. cash transactions, property conveyancing)
Distribution channelsDo we deal with clients face-to-face or remotely? Via introducers or intermediaries?
JurisdictionsWhere are clients based? Where do funds originate? Is any jurisdiction on the FATF blacklist or greylist?
Customer typesAre customers individuals, companies, trusts, PEPs, charities? Each carries different risk profiles.
Transaction volumes & sizesDo transaction values appear consistent with what we know of the customer's wealth and income?

Customer Risk Rating: The 11 Dimensions

Within the broader firm-level risk assessment sits the customer-level risk rating. The ICA identifies eleven risk dimensions that inform how a specific client or transaction should be rated:

#Risk DimensionProperty Sector Example
iGeographyBuyer based in or funds routed through a FATF greylist country?
iiNature of business interestsAre they a director of cash-intensive businesses?
iiiSensitive activitiesDefined as sensitive by the regulator ‚Äî e.g. dealing in high-value goods?
ivValue of assetsIs the purchase price consistent with known income and wealth?
vClient typeListed company (lower risk) vs HNW individual with complex structure (higher scrutiny)?
viPEP statusIs the buyer, or a family member, a Politically Exposed Person?

xiComplexity of arrangementMultiple SPVs, trust structures, or opaque ownership layers?

Risk Mitigation and the Limits of the RBA

Once risks are assessed, controls must be designed and implemented to mitigate them. The ICA is clear that it is probably not possible to remove risk entirely ‚Äî the goal is to mitigate it as far as possible. This is an important distinction: regulators do not expect firms to be risk-free. They expect firms to have thought carefully about their risks and acted proportionately.

This is why CDD is not binary ‚Äî not "full CDD or nothing." The RBA explicitly enables simplified CDD for lower-risk situations and requires enhanced CDD for higher-risk ones. But even simplified CDD still requires minimum standards. The RBA does not permit a firm to simply skip due diligence for any category of customer.

Monitoring, Review and Record-Keeping

Controls must be monitored on a continuous basis so that improvements can be made when required. This includes a regular review of the risk assessment itself ‚Äî a risk profile that was accurate two years ago may no longer reflect the firm's current business or client base.

Alongside monitoring sits record-keeping. The RBA requires a robust audit trail: documented risk assessments, control decisions, monitoring activity and the management information generated from that monitoring. If a regulator asks how you assessed a particular client's risk, you must be able to show them the answer. Records provide assurance to regulators and give senior management the tools to continuously develop and improve controls.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Failure Point

Firms sometimes conduct a risk assessment at the start of a client relationship and then treat it as a permanent record. This is dangerous. The ICA is explicit that monitoring must be continuous ‚Äî the risk profile must be regularly reviewed, and controls updated accordingly. A static risk assessment is not an RBA.

Formulating an AML Policy

Every organisation subject to AML regulation must have a formal AML and CFT policy, approved at the most senior level. This isn't a box-ticking exercise ‚Äî a well-crafted policy is a public statement of the organisation's ethics, its risk appetite, and its commitment to preventing financial crime.

Why the Policy Matters

The ICA makes a striking observation: an organisation's attitude towards combating money laundering is increasingly viewed as indicative of the ethics with which it conducts all of its business. In other words, your AML policy is not just a compliance document ‚Äî it is a signal of character to regulators, law enforcement, clients, and your own staff.

A policy serves as a statement of corporate intent that can be communicated to four key audiences: employees, clients, regulatory authorities, and law enforcement bodies. Its importance has grown significantly since 9/11, when the financial sector faced intense scrutiny over whether it had inadvertently facilitated the attacks through inadequate controls.

‚ö†Ô∏è The "Window Dressing" Problem

Too often, organisations simply pay external advisers to draft policies that are then adopted at board level without any internal consideration or discussion. The ICA's phrase for this: such policies "do little more than window dress." They provide the appearance of compliance without the substance. The danger compounds when regulators discover an organisation has failed to comply with its own stated policy ‚Äî that is far more damaging than having no policy at all.

What Must an AML Policy Contain?

The ICA sets out that formulating a policy requires management to first address four analytical questions about the organisation:

  • The nature, scale and complexity of its business
  • The diversity of its operations, including geographical diversity
  • The volumes and sizes of its transactions
  • The degree of ML/TF risk associated with each area of operation

Having answered those questions, the policy should then set out at minimum the following ten commitments:

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Ten Minimum Policy Elements
  1. Compliance with all applicable legislation, secondary legislation and best-practice guidance
  2. Cooperation with law enforcement and investigating authorities where required
  3. Respect for client confidentiality ‚Äî breached only where legally required
  4. Clearly defined responsibilities and accountabilities for AML prevention within the business
  5. A statement of the firm's willingness to provide sufficient resources for AML work
  6. Acceptance of new business only subject to appropriate CDD and risk assessment
  7. Continuation of existing business only where CDD, risk assessment and monitoring requirements are met
  8. A clear approach to staff education, training and awareness maintenance
  9. A statement of the firm's attitude towards persistent non-compliance with AML procedures
  10. An indication of the prevailing cultural attitude the firm wishes to create around AML prevention

Who Needs to Know About the Policy?

A policy that exists only in a folder serves no one. The ICA is clear that the policy must be actively communicated to all staff ‚Äî not just those with designated AML responsibilities. Every employee whose work touches clients, funds or transactions needs to understand the firm's approach. This includes frontline staff, relationship managers, and administrative personnel.

Communication alone is insufficient. The policy must also be implemented ‚Äî meaning systems and procedures must be put in place to turn the policy's commitments into day-to-day operational reality. And once implemented, adherence must be monitored.

Global Standards and Local Variations

For firms operating across multiple jurisdictions, the ICA recommends adopting "global standards" that meet the requirements of the most stringent national framework with which the firm must comply. This provides consistency and avoids the risk of different offices applying materially different standards that create regulatory arbitrage opportunities ‚Äî gaps that sophisticated money launderers can and do exploit.

üè† At Coadjute: What Our Policy Represents

Our AML policy exists at the level of the service we provide to estate agents and conveyancers. It reflects the fact that property transactions are one of the highest-risk sectors for money laundering in the UK. The policy is not a template borrowed from a competitor ‚Äî it reflects our specific business model, our client base, and the types of transactions we handle. Every staff member who touches a transaction needs to understand its key commitments.

AML Procedures in Practice

A policy sets out what an organisation stands for. Procedures are how those commitments are turned into operational reality. Without effective procedures, even the most well-intentioned policy is merely a statement of aspiration.

Policy vs Procedure: An Important Distinction

The ICA draws a clear line between policy and procedures. The policy is the "what" ‚Äî what the organisation commits to and why. Procedures are the "how" ‚Äî the specific steps, checks, escalation routes and documentation requirements that give effect to the policy in practice. Confusion between the two is a common problem: organisations sometimes treat their policy document as if it also covers procedures, which means neither is done well.

üí° Procedures Must Be Dynamic, Not Static

The ICA is explicit: "Once an AML framework is in place it needs to be continually monitored, tested, reported upon and adjusted. An effective internal system of control is not fixed or a one-off event; instead it is dynamic and constantly evolving to take account of changing threats and emerging weaknesses."

The Core Operational Procedures

The ICA sets out a comprehensive list of operational procedures that a well-designed AML regime should include. These go beyond CDD to cover every stage of the client relationship:

Procedure CategoryWhat It Covers
Customer acceptanceWho may accept new clients, on what terms, and subject to what checks
Standard CDDIdentity verification requirements for all new customers
Simplified CDDReduced requirements for genuinely lower-risk clients ‚Äî but minimum standards still apply
Enhanced CDDAdditional requirements for higher-risk clients, PEPs, complex structures, high-value transactions
Ongoing monitoringPeriodic review of existing clients; triggers for re-verification
Suspicious activity identificationHow to recognise and document red flags
Internal reportingHow to raise a concern to the MLRO; the internal SAR process
External reportingMLRO obligations for filing SARs with the National Crime Agency (NCA)
Record-keepingWhat records must be kept, for how long, and in what format
Staff trainingFrequency, content requirements, and how training is documented
Dual authorisationTwo-person sign-off requirements for high-value or high-risk transactions
Mandatory leaveRequiring all staff to take minimum annual leave to facilitate detection of concealed fraud

Dual Authorisation

Dual authorisation ‚Äî requiring two people to independently authorise certain transactions ‚Äî is a structural safeguard against both internal fraud and money laundering. The principle is that it is far harder for a single individual to facilitate a suspicious transaction if a second authorised person must independently review and sign off on it.

The ICA notes an important caveat: dual authorisation only works if both signatories actually perform independent scrutiny. If one routinely rubber-stamps the other's approval without genuine review, the control is defeated in practice even while appearing to function on paper. Firms must therefore take steps to ensure dual authorisation is genuinely independent.

Mandatory Leave

This requirement may seem unusual at first. The rationale is straightforward: an employee engaged in suspicious activity ‚Äî whether fraud or facilitating money laundering ‚Äî may need to be at their desk to maintain the deception. Enforced absence creates a gap in which colleagues or managers picking up their responsibilities may notice irregularities. Several significant financial fraud cases have been uncovered precisely because the perpetrator was unexpectedly absent.

üè† At Coadjute: Procedures We Apply

Our procedures include standard and enhanced CDD triggered by risk rating, an internal SAR escalation process to our nominated MLRO, source of funds verification requirements that vary by transaction value and client risk profile, and record retention policies. When in doubt at any stage of a transaction, the procedure is always to escalate ‚Äî never to proceed on the basis of an assumption.

Monitoring Compliance with the Policy

Adopting a policy is only the beginning. The harder challenge ‚Äî and the one where many organisations fall down ‚Äî is ensuring the policy is actually followed in practice. This is where the MLRO's monitoring role becomes critical.

The Monitoring Obligation

The ICA states clearly: once a policy is adopted, it must be communicated, implemented, and adherence to it must be monitored. All three elements are essential. A policy that is communicated but not implemented is useless. A policy that is implemented but not monitored will degrade over time as staff cut corners and risks go undetected.

Monitoring must be comprehensive ‚Äî it should cover all areas of the business that interact with clients, all product lines, and all stages of the client lifecycle from onboarding through to closure. It is not sufficient to monitor only the highest-risk clients; the monitoring framework must have full coverage.

The Role of the MLRO

The Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO) is the individual with day-to-day responsibility for monitoring the firm's compliance with its AML policy and procedures. Their role includes:

  • Receiving and evaluating internal suspicious activity reports from staff
  • Deciding whether to submit an external SAR to the National Crime Agency
  • Monitoring the quality and completeness of CDD across the business
  • Overseeing staff training programmes and ensuring records are maintained
  • Identifying systemic weaknesses and recommending procedural improvements
  • Producing regular management information (MI) for senior leadership

Management Information (MI)

MI is a critical tool for monitoring compliance and demonstrating it to regulators. The ICA identifies what effective AML MI should include:

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî AML Management Information
  • The number of clients and transaction volumes by risk rating (high, medium, low)
  • The number of internal SARs filed and their outcomes
  • External SARs filed with the NCA and results received
  • The number and nature of CDD exceptions or overrides granted
  • Training completion rates across the business
  • Results of internal audit or compliance testing
  • Any enforcement or regulatory contact received
  • Emerging typologies or red flags identified during monitoring

Remedial Action and Escalation

Monitoring alone is insufficient if it does not lead to action. Where monitoring identifies gaps, breaches or systemic weaknesses, the organisation must take remedial action. This might mean additional training for staff who are not following procedures, a redesign of a CDD form that is generating poor-quality data, or an escalation to senior management where a systemic control failure is identified.

The ICA notes that the best AML frameworks treat every identified weakness as a learning opportunity ‚Äî not something to be minimised or suppressed. Regulators take a far more positive view of firms that identify and address their own weaknesses proactively than of firms where problems are only discovered during regulatory visits.

The MLRO Annual Report

Most regulated firms are required to produce an annual report from the MLRO to the board or senior management. This report is both a monitoring tool and an accountability mechanism. It should provide an honest, comprehensive assessment of:

  • The volume and nature of suspicious activity reports during the year
  • Significant changes in the firm's risk profile
  • An assessment of whether existing controls remain adequate
  • Any regulatory developments that require policy or procedure changes
  • Training activity and outcomes
  • Recommendations for improvement
‚ö†Ô∏è When Monitoring Fails

A firm that discovers a systemic CDD failure during a regulatory inspection is in a far worse position than one that identifies and corrects the same failure through its own monitoring programme. Regulators assess not just whether a firm has good controls, but whether it has the capacity to identify and correct problems itself. A firm whose self-monitoring does not work is one whose compliance framework cannot be trusted.

Resources & the Objective Test

One of the most practically important concepts in the ICA's AML framework is the link between adequate resources and legal exposure. Organisations that fail to provide sufficient resources for AML functions cannot hide behind a claim of ignorance ‚Äî the law applies an objective test to what staff should have known or suspected.

The Resource Obligation

The ICA is clear that organisations have a duty to provide sufficient resources for their AML functions to operate effectively. This covers three dimensions:

  • Sufficient time: AML staff must have enough time to carry out their responsibilities properly. An MLRO who is also carrying a full client-facing workload and has no protected time for compliance work is an MLRO who cannot function effectively.
  • Sufficient staff: The number of people working on AML must be proportionate to the volume and complexity of the business. Growth in transaction volumes must be matched by growth in compliance capacity.
  • Sufficient training: All staff who interact with clients or transactions must receive adequate AML training ‚Äî not just once at induction, but on an ongoing basis as the risk landscape evolves.
üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The Objective Test

The legal test for suspicious activity is objective, not subjective. An employee cannot defend a failure to report by saying "I personally didn't think it was suspicious." The question the law asks is: would a reasonable person with the same information, in the same role, have been suspicious? If yes, the employee had grounds for suspicion regardless of their personal assessment.

This means that workload is not a defence. An employee who processes a suspicious transaction without reporting it cannot rely on the argument that they were too busy to notice. The law treats them as having had grounds for suspicion if a reasonable person in their role would have done so.

Why Resources and the Objective Test Are Connected

Here is the critical intersection: if an organisation routinely overloads its compliance staff to the point where they cannot realistically spot suspicious activity, the objective test still applies to those staff individually. An individual employee who is too overwhelmed to review transactions properly is still legally exposed if a reasonable person in their position should have been suspicious.

But the organisation is also exposed ‚Äî because it has created conditions in which its people cannot comply with the law, and that itself may constitute a failure of the organisation's AML systems and controls. Under UK law (POCA 2002 and MLR 2017), both individual employees and organisations can face criminal and regulatory consequences for AML failures.

Training: The Ongoing Obligation

Training is a resources question and a legal requirement. The ICA sets out what adequate training must cover:

  • The law ‚Äî what the money laundering offences are and what constitutes criminal liability
  • The firm's own AML policy and procedures ‚Äî so staff know the specific steps they must follow
  • How to identify suspicious activity ‚Äî both in general and in the specific context of their role
  • What to do when they suspect something ‚Äî the internal SAR process and escalation route
  • Tipping off ‚Äî what it is, why it is a criminal offence, and how to avoid it

Training records must be maintained. In the event of a regulatory review, the organisation must be able to demonstrate what training was given, to whom, and when. Undocumented training is, for regulatory purposes, training that did not happen.

Corporate Culture & Tone from the Top

Rules, policies and procedures are necessary ‚Äî but not sufficient. The most important determinant of whether an AML framework actually works is the culture of the organisation. Culture is what happens when no one is watching. It's the difference between staff who file internal SARs because they understand why it matters and staff who avoid doing so because they fear upsetting a fee-generating relationship.

Why Culture Is Hard to Get Right

Unlike a policy document or a procedure, culture is intangible. You cannot point to it in a folder. You cannot audit it on a checklist. The ICA acknowledges that culture is therefore often overlooked when organisations assess the quality of their AML controls ‚Äî and that this oversight is dangerous, because negative cultural attitudes can be just as damaging as gaps in written procedures.

The challenge is particularly acute in commercial organisations that exist to generate business and revenue. AML procedures can come to be seen as obstacles ‚Äî "business prevention" ‚Äî rather than as tools that protect both the firm and its people. When that attitude takes hold, staff begin to find ways around the procedures rather than complying with them.

The Indicators of Positive AML Culture

The ICA identifies a range of indicators that suggest a genuinely positive AML culture exists within an organisation:

IndicatorWhat it Looks Like in Practice
Board-level ownershipSenior leadership publicly and repeatedly affirm AML as a priority ‚Äî not just at annual compliance reviews
MLRO authorityThe MLRO has genuine authority to escalate concerns to the board and to refuse or exit client relationships without commercial pressure overriding compliance judgment
No commercial overrideThere is no culture of "working around" CDD requirements for high-value clients or important introducers
Psychological safetyStaff feel confident raising AML concerns ‚Äî including concerns about colleagues at a more senior level ‚Äî without fear of retaliation or being labelled as obstructive
Recognition and rewardStaff who identify suspicious activity are recognised positively, not treated as having caused a problem
Training is valuedAML training is treated as meaningful professional development, not a box-ticking exercise done at the minimum required frequency

Tone from the Top: The Board's Responsibility

The ICA is explicit that tone from the top is the single most important cultural determinant in AML. If board members and senior leaders treat AML as a compliance burden to be managed with minimum resources and maximum speed, that attitude will cascade through the organisation. If they treat it as a fundamental ethical and legal obligation that defines how the firm conducts its business, that too cascades downward.

The board cannot delegate its AML obligations away. Even where an MLRO has day-to-day responsibility, ultimate accountability rests at the most senior level. Boards that invest in AML, appoint experienced MLROs, provide adequate resources, and hold themselves accountable for compliance outcomes create the conditions for effective AML culture to develop.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Culture and Individual Responsibility

The ICA makes a point that is particularly important for junior staff: every individual's AML obligations are their own. A junior employee who decides not to raise a concern because they assume a more senior colleague has already dealt with it is not protected by that assumption. Their suspicion is as legally significant as anyone else's. Culture that makes junior staff feel their concerns don't count ‚Äî or that the "important" clients are handled differently ‚Äî directly undermines the firm's legal position.

The Tracey McDermott Challenge

The ICA closes its discussion of corporate culture with a quote from Tracey McDermott, formerly of the FCA: "Let's make it [the financial services industry] a hostile place for the criminal."

That is the standard. Not "we tried our best." Not "we had a policy." The standard is an industry that is actively and collectively hostile to those who seek to use it for financial crime. That standard begins with culture ‚Äî with every individual in every organisation understanding why their role in the system matters.
