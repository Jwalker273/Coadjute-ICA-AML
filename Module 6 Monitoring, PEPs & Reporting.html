Ongoing Monitoring

Completing CDD at onboarding is only the beginning. Regulation 28 of the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 (MLR 2017) requires firms to monitor client activity throughout the relationship ‚Äî not just at the start.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The Regulation 28 Requirement

Ongoing monitoring means two things under Regulation 28, MLR 2017:

  • Scrutiny of transactions to ensure they are consistent with the firm's knowledge of the customer, their business, and risk profile ‚Äî including source of funds where necessary.
  • Keeping CDD documents, data and information up to date on a risk-sensitive basis.

This does not mean reviewing every single transaction. It means adopting a risk-based programme ‚Äî higher-risk relationships get more frequent, more intensive monitoring.

What "Risk-Sensitive Monitoring" Actually Means

In practice, the frequency and depth of monitoring is calibrated to risk:

Risk LevelMonitoring FrequencyTrigger for Review
Low riskPeriodic ‚Äî annually or lessMaterial change in circumstances or transaction pattern
Standard riskQuarterly or semi-annuallySignificant transactions, adverse media, CDD document expiry
High risk (incl. PEPs)Monthly or more frequent; enhanced scrutiny of each transactionAny change; potentially real-time for highest-risk
‚ö†Ô∏è The Fixed Review Cycle Trap

A firm that reviews all client CDD on a flat 5-year cycle ‚Äî and does nothing in between ‚Äî is not meeting its Regulation 28 obligations. Monitoring must be responsive to change, not just time-based. If material changes occur, an immediate review is required regardless of when the last review was.

What Triggers a CDD Update?

CDD information has a shelf life. A client who was low-risk at onboarding can become high-risk because:

  • They have taken on a new role that makes them a PEP
  • Adverse media has appeared linking them to criminal conduct
  • Their country of residence or business has changed to a high-risk jurisdiction
  • Their transaction patterns have changed significantly
  • Their ID documents have expired
  • The nature of the business relationship has changed materially
  • A new beneficial owner has been identified

Automated Transaction Monitoring Systems

Larger firms use automated systems (ATM systems) to flag potentially suspicious transactions. The FCA published a comprehensive report on these systems in 2007, which the ICA cites as guidance on best practice.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî FCA Guidance on ATM Systems

The FCA's 2007 guidance on automated transaction monitoring systems states:

  • Senior management must be able to monitor the performance of TM systems ‚Äî especially where there are operational issues
  • Business unit stakeholders and IT must be closely involved in system implementation
  • The MLRO/compliance team must have clear allocation of responsibilities for reviewing, investigating and reporting alerts
  • Staff responsible for managing alerts must have appropriate skills and be subject to effective quality assurance
  • A backlog of unreviewed alerts is a regulatory failure and a sign of inadequate resources

Exception reports advise supervisors of unusual transactions for review. More sophisticated systems use "case management" tools that track and record all outputs, enquiries made, and conclusions reached ‚Äî creating an audit trail.

‚ö†Ô∏è Managing System Output

The ICA is explicit: "Every aspect of the transaction monitoring process must be actively managed, regularly reviewed and effectively maintained." Generating alerts means nothing if nobody is reviewing them. Quality assurance of alert review is just as important as the system itself.

Ongoing Monitoring in the Property Sector

Property transactions are typically discrete events rather than continuous banking relationships ‚Äî but ongoing monitoring still applies. At Coadjute, we need to think about monitoring across several touchpoints:

üè† Coadjute Context ‚Äî Ongoing Monitoring
  • Transaction-level monitoring: Does the structure, price, and funding of the transaction match what we know about the client? Are there late changes to purchase price, parties, or funding sources?
  • Multi-instruction patterns: A client buying multiple properties in quick succession, or changing details repeatedly, may warrant review
  • Adverse media screening: If new information emerges about a client mid-transaction, CDD must be revisited
  • Source of funds changes: If a buyer who declared one funding source introduces a different one late in the transaction, that is a red flag requiring re-assessment
  • Sanctions screening: Ongoing sanctions screening through the transaction, not just at the start

Beneficial Ownership

One of the most powerful tools available to money launderers is placing assets ‚Äî including property ‚Äî in the name of a legal entity rather than their own. Beneficial ownership rules exist to look through these structures to find the real human being in control.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Beneficial Owner Definition

A beneficial owner is the natural person who ultimately owns or controls the customer, or on whose behalf a transaction is conducted.

FATF Recommendation 10 requires that CDD includes identifying any beneficial owner, and taking reasonable measures to verify their identity so that the firm is satisfied it knows who the beneficial owner is.

The standard threshold: typically more than 25% of shares or voting rights, though this varies by jurisdiction.

Why Beneficial Ownership Matters: The Layering Problem

A company, trust, or other legal entity is not a human being. It cannot have criminal intent ‚Äî but it can be used by human beings to disguise their ownership of property. Consider what a simple corporate structure can do:

üè† Property Sector Example ‚Äî Corporate Layering

The Chain of Companies

A property worth ¬£3.2m is purchased by "Sunrise Holdings Ltd," a UK company. The sole shareholder of Sunrise Holdings is "Meridian Capital BV," a Dutch company. Meridian Capital's sole shareholder is "Pacific Nominees Inc.," a Cayman Islands company. Pacific Nominees issues bearer shares ‚Äî so there is no registered shareholder at all.

Who owns the property? Without beneficial ownership investigation, nobody knows. The property could be held for the benefit of a corrupt politician, a sanctions-designated individual, or an organised crime figure ‚Äî all while appearing on paper to be owned by a legitimate UK registered company.

This is precisely the structure the Panama Papers and Pandora Papers revealed at global scale.

Unwrapping Complex Structures

The ICA makes clear that firms must look through corporate structures to identify the individual who ultimately owns or controls the entity. The key principle:

‚ö†Ô∏è ICA Principle ‚Äî Unduly Complex Structures

The common difference between a potentially legitimate structure and one designed for concealment is whether there is a genuine business need for the complexity. A legitimate multinational may have many holding companies for tax or operational reasons. A private individual purchasing a single UK property through three offshore entities has no obvious legitimate need for that structure. The complexity itself is the red flag.

The Beneficial Ownership Registers

In response to high-profile scandals, many countries have introduced public registers of beneficial ownership:

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî UK Register of Overseas Entities

The UK's Register of Overseas Entities (ROE), introduced by the Economic Crime (Transparency and Enforcement) Act 2022, requires overseas entities that own or buy UK property to disclose their beneficial owners to Companies House. This was directly prompted by concerns about anonymous overseas property purchases used for money laundering.

The broader UK register ‚Äî the People with Significant Control (PSC) register ‚Äî requires UK companies to disclose individuals with more than 25% ownership, significant influence or control.

The ICA also notes that the Panama, Pandora and Paradise Papers have brought international pressure on governments to create transparency around beneficial ownership globally.

Who Can Access Beneficial Ownership Information?

The FATF standard requires that beneficial ownership registers allow access to:

  • Competent authorities (police, prosecutors, regulators)
  • Financial Intelligence Units (FIUs)
  • Professional sectors subject to AML rules (banks, lawyers, property professionals)
  • Anyone who can demonstrate a legitimate interest (by written request)

Beneficial Ownership in the Property Sector

üè† Coadjute Context ‚Äî Beneficial Ownership

When Coadjute carries out AML on behalf of estate agents and conveyancers, beneficial ownership is often the most complex and important issue to resolve. Key obligations:

  • Where the buyer or seller is a company, identify the ultimate beneficial owner ‚Äî the natural person
  • Do not accept the company itself as the "customer" without looking through the structure
  • For overseas entities, check the Register of Overseas Entities on Companies House
  • For UK companies, check the PSC register
  • Treat nominee directors and shareholders as red flags requiring deeper investigation
  • Where a trust is involved, identify the settlor, trustees, and all beneficiaries
  • Document your investigation ‚Äî regulators want to see how you unwrapped the structure

Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs)

PEPs represent one of the most significant risks in AML ‚Äî and also one of the most frequently examined topics. High-profile cases involving corrupt officials and stolen state assets have made PEP risk a central concern for regulators worldwide.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî PEP Definition

A PEP is an individual who is or has been entrusted with a prominent public function. The definition is extended to include their immediate family members and known close associates.

Categories of PEPs include:

  • Heads of state and government
  • Senior politicians (ministers, senior members of governing bodies)
  • Senior government, judicial and military officials
  • Senior executives of state-owned enterprises
  • Important political party officials

Former PEPs retain their status during a cooling-off period after leaving office. The duration is risk-assessed ‚Äî there is no fixed statutory period.

Why Are PEPs High Risk?

The danger is straightforward: a PEP may have used their public position to acquire wealth through corruption ‚Äî bribery, embezzlement of state funds, fraud, or other abuses of office. That wealth then needs to be laundered. Property is a common vehicle because:

  • High-value property holdings appear legitimate and prestigious
  • Property purchased through offshore companies obscures the true owner
  • Property in stable, rule-of-law countries (like the UK) is seen as a safe store of value
  • Rental income provides a legitimate-looking income stream
üî¥ Notable PEP Cases ‚Äî Property Sector

These real-world cases illustrate the scale of the risk:

Zamira Hajiyeva (UK)

Wife of a jailed Azerbaijani banker. Subject of the UK's first Unexplained Wealth Order (2018). Spent ¬£16m at Harrods; multiple UK properties suspected. UWO required her to explain her assets.

General Sani Abacha (Nigeria)

Former military ruler. Approximately $5bn looted and moved through European private banks and property acquisitions.

Teodoro Obiang (Equatorial Guinea)

Son of the President. US investigations revealed luxury assets ‚Äî including property ‚Äî purchased with state funds channelled through US banks.

Isabel dos Santos (Angola)

Daughter of Angola's former President. Luanda Leaks (2020) exposed billions in allegedly fraudulent dealings, including European property.

Domestic vs Foreign PEPs ‚Äî The 4MLD Change

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî 3MLD vs 4MLD on Domestic PEPs

The Third EU Money Laundering Directive (3MLD) required EDD for PEPs, but this originally applied mainly to foreign PEPs. There was a previous anomaly: domestic PEPs (those from your own jurisdiction) were treated differently.

The Fourth EU Money Laundering Directive (4MLD) removed this distinction. After 4MLD (incorporated into UK law via MLR 2017), domestic PEPs are also PEPs ‚Äî though firms may assess them as lower-risk than foreign PEPs if there is reasonable, documented justification. In practice, most UK firms were already treating senior domestic officials as high-risk, so the practical impact was limited.

EDD Requirements for PEPs

Once a client is identified as a PEP, EDD is mandatory throughout the relationship. The ICA identifies four key CDD elements that must be examined:

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The 4 Key EDD Elements for PEPs
  1. Geography ‚Äî what country/jurisdiction is the PEP associated with? High-risk jurisdictions require greater scrutiny.
  2. Source of wealth ‚Äî how did the PEP build their total wealth? Does this align with what is publicly known about their salary and position?
  3. Source of funds ‚Äî where did the specific money in this transaction come from?
  4. Commercial rationale ‚Äî does the nature of the transaction make sense for this PEP?

The Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) guidance, cited by the ICA, also requires:

  • Senior management approval before establishing or continuing business with a PEP
  • Enhanced monitoring ‚Äî increased degree and nature of transaction monitoring throughout the relationship
  • Verification of source of wealth and source of funds "by appropriate and reasonable means"

PEP Risk at Coadjute

üè† Coadjute Context ‚Äî PEPs in Property Transactions

The UK property market is a known destination for PEP-linked wealth. When carrying out AML checks, if a buyer or seller is identified as a PEP (or a close family member or associate of a PEP):

  • EDD is mandatory ‚Äî not optional
  • Source of wealth must be investigated and evidenced, not just asserted
  • Source of funds for the specific purchase must be verified
  • Check adverse media ‚Äî Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index can assist with geography risk
  • Senior sign-off is required before proceeding
  • The estate agent and conveyancer must be informed if EDD reveals concerns that affect the transaction

Identifying Suspicious Activity

You are not expected to be a detective. But you are expected to know your clients, remain alert, and report when something does not add up. This section covers what "suspicion" means in law, and the kinds of activity that should trigger it.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The Standard of Suspicion

The level of evidence required to form a suspicion is deliberately low. You are entitled to assume clients are honest unless and until information comes to your attention that leads you to know or suspect criminality.

You are not expected to suspect a particular type of crime, nor to prove that a crime has been committed. Suspicion of criminality in the broadest sense is sufficient.

The ICA distinguishes between:

  • Subjective suspicion ‚Äî you personally believe something is wrong
  • Objective suspicion ‚Äî a reasonable person in your position, with the same information, would have formed a suspicion

You can be found to have had reasonable grounds to suspect even if you did not personally notice anything wrong. Willful blindness ‚Äî deliberately shutting your eyes to the obvious ‚Äî is treated the same as actual knowledge.

What Counts as Suspicious Activity?

The ICA provides the following common examples of suspicious activity:

  • A transaction that is unusually large given what you know about the client
  • Activity or transactions that are out of line with what is normally seen from this client, or compared to similar client profiles
  • Inconsistencies or discrepancies in the CDD provided (e.g., stated profession does not match income level)

More specifically, in the property sector, the following patterns have been identified as red flags:

üè† Property Sector Red Flags
  • Purchase at significantly above or below market value
  • Cash purchase of high-value property with no clear source of funds
  • Buyer using a complex chain of companies, particularly offshore
  • Late changes to the purchase price, parties to the transaction, or funding source
  • Third-party funding (someone other than the buyer paying for the property)
  • Unusual urgency to complete with no commercial explanation
  • Multiple rapid purchases and re-sales (rapid resale / price cycling)
  • The buyer shows no interest in the property itself (never visits, never discusses details)
  • Requesting that the title be put in a different name from the buyer
  • Client reluctant or unable to explain the source of funds
  • Significant refurbishment costs settled in cash

Knowing Your Client ‚Äî The Foundation of Suspicion

Recognising suspicious activity is only possible if you know what "normal" looks like for a given client. This is why CDD is the foundation of everything ‚Äî you cannot identify something unusual without a baseline. The ICA requires that you:

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The Four Obligations
  1. Know your clients ‚Äî establish and understand their profile
  2. Remain alert to the threat of money laundering or terrorist financing
  3. Consider whether any activity is unusual and potentially suspicious
  4. Report when necessary ‚Äî following your organisation's reporting process

Unless your role specifically includes monitoring responsibilities, you are not expected to actively search for suspicious information. But when something comes to your attention, you must consider it and report if necessary.

Suspicion Across the Organisation

The duty to report suspicions applies universally ‚Äî to every employee at every level. This is one of the ICA's most important points:

‚ÑπÔ∏è ICA Principle ‚Äî Universal Reporting Obligation

Many employees mistakenly believe that if their managers do not appear suspicious about a client, there is no need for them to be suspicious. This is wrong.

Different employees with different roles are exposed to different aspects of the same relationship. More junior staff are often in a better position to formulate suspicions ‚Äî they are more closely involved with day-to-day transactional activity that senior managers may be unaware of.

The ICA is explicit: "Your suspicions are as valuable as those of the chairperson or managing director of your organisation."

‚ö†Ô∏è The "If in Doubt, Report" Principle

The adage "if in doubt, report" is endorsed by the ICA. The reporting threshold is deliberately low. It is always better to make an internal report to the MLRO and have it reviewed than to suppress a concern. Failing to report is an offence; making a good-faith report is protected.

The SAR Process

Once a suspicion is formed, what happens next? The Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) is the formal mechanism through which your suspicion enters the financial intelligence system. This section covers how the process works ‚Äî from the initial suspicion to the NCA.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî What Is a SAR?

A Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) is the formal written report of a suspicion that money laundering or terrorist financing is occurring. In some jurisdictions, the term Suspicious Transaction Report (STR) is used instead ‚Äî they are the same thing.

In the UK, external SARs are submitted by the MLRO to the National Crime Agency (NCA). The NCA is the UK's financial intelligence unit.

The Two-Stage Reporting Process

In most organisations, SAR reporting happens in two stages:

Stage 1 ‚Äî Internal SAR (Employee to MLRO)

You form a suspicion. You complete your organisation's internal SAR template and submit it in writing to the MLRO (also called the Nominated Officer). Writing is essential ‚Äî it provides statutory protection and creates an audit trail. The MLRO investigates.

Stage 2 ‚Äî External SAR (MLRO to NCA)

If the MLRO, after investigation, determines that the threshold of suspicion is met, they submit an external SAR to the NCA. If the MLRO decides not to escalate, they must document their reasoning. If the employee disagrees, they may still escalate directly in certain circumstances.

NCA Review ‚Äî Consent or DAML

If the firm needs to proceed with a transaction while the SAR is under review, they may seek a Defence Against Money Laundering (DAML) ‚Äî essentially, requesting consent from the NCA to proceed. The NCA has 7 days to respond (with a possible moratorium of a further 31 days). Proceeding without consent where criminal property is involved can constitute a money laundering offence.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Quality of SARs

The ICA emphasises that the best quality SARs contain "soft intelligence" ‚Äî contextual information about what the customer has said or done, not just dry transaction data. A SAR that explains why something is suspicious, with reference to what the client told you and how it contradicts known facts, is far more useful to the NCA than a bare list of transactions.

Key point: It is never too late to submit a SAR. However, you are obliged to report as soon as reasonably practicable once you have formed a suspicion. Delay can itself constitute a failure.

Who Can Be the Subject of a SAR?

‚ÑπÔ∏è Broad Scope of Reporting Obligation

SARs are not limited to existing clients. The duty to report applies to anybody encountered in the course of regulated activities ‚Äî including:

  • Prospective clients who approach you and then withdraw
  • Third parties involved in a transaction (e.g., a vendor or their solicitor)
  • Counterparties in a deal

Even if you turn a client away, a SAR may be required if you have formed a suspicion in the process.

Protecting the Identity of the Reporter

The case of HSBC v Shah (High Court, 2012) is a landmark case cited by the ICA. Shah filed a complaint against HSBC for delaying payments, and sought to discover who had filed the SAR against him. The key ruling:

‚úÖ HSBC v Shah ‚Äî Key Principle

The UK Court of Appeal ruled that a bank does not have to disclose the identity of employees who make internal SARs. The name of a member of staff submitting a SAR should not be disclosed, even to regulatory or law enforcement authorities.

This protects employees who report in good faith. Staff should be reassured that their identity is protected when they report concerns.

The DAML Process at Coadjute

üè† Coadjute Context ‚Äî SARs and Property Transactions

Property transactions create a particular tension around SARs: a transaction is often time-sensitive, with exchange and completion dates fixed. If a SAR is filed mid-transaction, proceeding to exchange or completion could constitute the "acquisition, use or possession" of criminal property under POCA 2002 s.329.

In practice, this means:

  • If a suspicion arises mid-transaction, a DAML must be sought from the NCA before proceeding
  • The NCA has 7 working days to grant or refuse consent (+ potential 31-day moratorium for further investigation)
  • During this period, the transaction must be paused ‚Äî which inevitably leads to client questions
  • How to handle those client questions without tipping off is covered in Section 6

Client Confidentiality & Tipping Off

Financial services businesses owe their clients a duty of confidentiality. But the law creates an important exception ‚Äî and a criminal offence ‚Äî that every AML professional must understand thoroughly.

The Confidentiality Duty

All financial services businesses owe clients a contractual obligation to maintain the confidentiality of their personal information. In some jurisdictions (notably Switzerland) breaching client confidentiality is itself a criminal offence. This creates an apparent conflict:

‚ö†Ô∏è The Apparent Conflict

How can you reconcile the duty to report your suspicions about a client with the duty of confidentiality you owe them?

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The Safe Harbour Clause

The law provides complete protection against any action for breach of client confidentiality where you report knowledge or suspicion of criminal conduct in good faith. This is known as the safe harbour provision.

In other words: making a SAR in good faith completely overrides and dissolves the duty of confidentiality. You cannot be sued for breach of confidentiality as a result of a good-faith SAR.

This safe harbour exists so that individuals can help in the fight against crime and money laundering without fear of civil action from clients.

The Tipping Off Offence

The flip side of the safe harbour is a serious criminal offence: tipping off. It arises when someone tells the subject of a SAR (or anyone else) that a report has been made, in a way that is likely to prejudice any investigation.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Tipping Off Under POCA 2002

Tipping off occurs when a person:

  1. Knows or suspects that a SAR has been made (internally or to the NCA)
  2. Discloses to another person information that is likely to prejudice any investigation that might be conducted

Critically, the offence can be committed by communicating prejudicial information to people other than the suspect ‚Äî including:

  • Family members of the suspected launderer
  • Their lawyers or advisers
  • Other third parties such as journalists

The obligation not to tip off overrides the normal duty of client confidentiality. If a client asks why their transaction is delayed, you must not indicate a SAR has been filed ‚Äî even hinting at it can constitute tipping off.

Normal Enquiries Do NOT Constitute Tipping Off

This is an important nuance the ICA specifically addresses. Making routine commercial enquiries of a client ‚Äî asking about the source of funds, asking about the nature of a transaction ‚Äî does not constitute tipping off, even if it might alert a criminal that they are under scrutiny. In fact, such enquiries may actually remove the suspicion.

‚úÖ Key Distinction

Asking questions of a client as part of due diligence = acceptable and often necessary.

Telling a client that a SAR has been filed, or that they are under investigation, = tipping off.

The ICA notes that routine enquiries are "often key to uncovering criminal activity." Don't confuse asking legitimate questions with tipping off.

How to Handle Client Queries When a SAR Is Filed

If a transaction is paused because a SAR has been filed and a DAML is being sought from the NCA, clients will inevitably ask what is happening. This requires careful handling:

‚ö†Ô∏è Practical Guidance ‚Äî Client Queries During a SAR
  • Do not say "we have filed a report about you" or anything indicating a SAR exists
  • Do not hint that money laundering is suspected
  • A carefully worded response about "internal compliance checks" or "regulatory requirements" is appropriate
  • All employees should seek guidance from the MLRO or management on how to respond
  • You can continue to talk to the client ‚Äî you just cannot discuss the SAR or the investigation
üè† Coadjute Scenario ‚Äî Tipping Off Risk

The Delayed Completion

A buyer's conveyancer calls to ask why Coadjute has requested additional information about the source of funds and why the completion is being delayed. The MLRO has filed a DAML with the NCA. What do you say?

Acceptable: "We have some internal compliance procedures to complete in line with our regulatory obligations. We'll be in touch as soon as we're in a position to proceed. We understand this is frustrating and we're working to resolve it as quickly as possible."

Not acceptable: "We've filed a suspicious activity report about your client and we're waiting for consent from the NCA." ‚Äî this is tipping off and a criminal offence.
