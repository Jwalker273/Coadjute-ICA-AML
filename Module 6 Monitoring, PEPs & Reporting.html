Ongoing Monitoring

Completing customer due diligence at onboarding is only the beginning. AML obligations do not end when the relationship starts ‚Äî they continue throughout its entire life. This section examines what ongoing monitoring means in practice, what the law requires, and what it looks like in a property transaction context.

Why Monitoring Is Not Optional

A firm that performs thorough CDD at the start of a relationship but never reviews it again is only partially compliant. Criminals are sophisticated: they may present clean at onboarding and only introduce suspicious elements later. The risk profile of a client can also change legitimately ‚Äî through changes in business, geography, or wealth ‚Äî which the firm needs to reflect.

Ongoing monitoring serves two purposes: it keeps CDD information accurate and current, and it identifies unusual or suspicious transactions that warrant investigation or reporting.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Regulation 28 MLRs 2017

Regulation 28 of the Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds (Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017 imposes an obligation of ongoing monitoring throughout a business relationship. This requires firms to:

  • Scrutinise transactions to ensure they are consistent with the firm's knowledge of the customer, their business, and their risk profile
  • Keep CDD documents, data, and information up to date and relevant
  • Apply the obligation on a risk-sensitive basis ‚Äî more intensive monitoring for higher-risk relationships

The key phrase is "risk-sensitive basis" ‚Äî monitoring is not identical for every client. A low-risk domestic buyer completing a standard residential purchase requires less intensive monitoring than a PEP purchasing through an offshore structure.

What Does "Scrutinising Transactions" Actually Mean?

In a property context, scrutiny means asking whether what the client is doing makes sense given what you know about them. You should be asking:

  • Is the transaction consistent with the customer's stated purpose and the type of business you expected to conduct?
  • Does the source of funds match what the customer told you about their financial background?
  • Does the level of activity match the risk profile established at onboarding?
  • Has the customer's behaviour changed in a way that suggests a change in risk ‚Äî or something more sinister?
üè† Property Sector Example

An estate agent onboards a developer client who says they typically sell properties in the ¬£300,000‚Äì¬£500,000 range to first-time buyers. Six months later, the same client attempts to instruct the agent on an ¬£8m commercial site purchase, paid entirely in cash from a newly opened account in Cyprus. This is inconsistent with the established risk profile and warrants immediate review ‚Äî not because cash or Cyprus accounts are automatically suspicious, but because the dramatic shift in transaction type, value, and funding method is unexplained.

When Must You Review CDD?

Monitoring is not just a periodic tick-box exercise. The ICA and the MLRs 2017 identify specific triggers that require a CDD review, regardless of when the last review was conducted:

  • Material change in the customer's circumstances ‚Äî new business interests, change of address to a high-risk jurisdiction, change of beneficial ownership
  • Adverse media or intelligence ‚Äî the customer features in news reports linking them to crime, corruption, or sanctions
  • Unusual transaction activity ‚Äî transactions that do not match the established profile
  • Change in the nature of the relationship ‚Äî a residential customer begins conducting commercial transactions
  • Notification from a third party ‚Äî law enforcement contact, a notification from another regulated firm, or information from a sanctions list update

Automated Transaction Monitoring Systems

Larger firms, including banks and larger estate agency groups, use automated transaction monitoring (ATM) systems to flag unusual patterns. These systems apply rules-based and algorithmic monitoring across large volumes of transactions ‚Äî for example, flagging multiple purchases by the same buyer in a short period, or payments from jurisdictions on watch lists.

However, the ICA is clear that automated systems are tools, not replacements for human judgement. A flagged transaction still requires a human to assess whether it is genuinely suspicious. Alert fatigue ‚Äî where staff dismiss alerts because they are too frequent ‚Äî is one of the most significant AML risks in firms that rely heavily on automated systems.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistake

Many firms interpret ongoing monitoring as an annual review cycle for all clients. This is incorrect. Regulation 28 requires monitoring that is responsive to risk. A scheduled annual review does not substitute for an immediate review when material adverse information emerges. Both obligations can exist simultaneously.

Beneficial Ownership

One of the most powerful tools available to money launderers is concealing who actually owns assets by placing them in corporate structures, trusts, or nominee arrangements. Identifying the real person behind a transaction ‚Äî the beneficial owner ‚Äî is one of the most important and most challenging obligations in AML practice.

What Is a Beneficial Owner?

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Beneficial Owner Definition

A beneficial owner is the natural person who ultimately owns or controls a customer, or on whose behalf a transaction is being conducted. The key word is ultimately ‚Äî you must look through layers of corporate structure to find the human being at the end of the chain.

For companies, beneficial ownership is defined under the MLRs 2017 as ownership or control of more than 25% of the shares or voting rights, or any other means of exercising significant control over the management of the company.

If you cannot identify any natural person who meets this threshold, you should treat the senior managing official(s) of the entity as the beneficial owner for AML purposes.

Why Corporate Structures Are Used to Launder Money

A company, trust, or partnership can hold property in its own name without the underlying owner ever appearing in a public record. By layering multiple corporate entities across different jurisdictions, launderers can make it virtually impossible to trace funds back to their criminal origin.

Classic structures used in property money laundering include:

  • UK company owned by a BVI (British Virgin Islands) company ‚Äî the BVI company has nominee shareholders, hiding the true owner
  • Scottish Limited Partnerships (SLPs) ‚Äî historically lightly regulated, used extensively in fraud and ML schemes
  • Discretionary trusts ‚Äî the beneficiaries are not fixed and may not be named in accessible documents
  • Nominee shareholders and directors ‚Äî professionals (or complicit individuals) who hold shares or act as directors on behalf of the true owner, for a fee
üè† Property Sector Example ‚Äî The Layered Structure

A conveyancer receives instructions to act for "UK Property Holdings Ltd" on the purchase of a London flat for ¬£1.4m. The buyer's representative says the funds are coming from another company, "International Investments Ltd", registered in Gibraltar. That company is 100% owned by a trust in Guernsey whose beneficiaries are "undisclosed".

Each layer adds distance from the true owner. The conveyancer's obligation is to look through every layer and identify the natural person who ultimately controls this chain ‚Äî or to refuse the instruction if they cannot.

The Persons with Significant Control (PSC) Register

Since 2016, UK companies are required to maintain a Persons with Significant Control (PSC) register, which is publicly accessible at Companies House. PSCs are those who own more than 25% of shares, control more than 25% of voting rights, have the right to appoint or remove the majority of the board, or otherwise exercise significant influence or control.

The PSC register is a valuable starting point for beneficial ownership verification, but it has limitations:

  • It covers UK-registered companies only ‚Äî overseas companies are not covered
  • Compliance with the register is self-reported and not always accurate
  • It may show another company rather than a natural person, requiring you to trace further
  • It does not cover trusts, partnerships, or informal nominee arrangements
üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Registers of Overseas Entities

Since January 2023, overseas entities that own UK property must register with Companies House under the Economic Crime (Transparency and Enforcement) Act 2022. The Register of Overseas Entities (ROE) requires disclosure of beneficial ownership of overseas companies that own or wish to acquire UK land.

This is particularly significant for the property sector, where overseas ownership structures have historically been a significant ML risk. Conveyancers and estate agents must check the ROE where overseas entities are involved in a transaction.

Trusts and Beneficial Ownership

Trusts present particular challenges because ownership is split: the trustee legally owns the assets, while the beneficiaries benefit from them. For AML purposes, you must identify:

  • The settlor ‚Äî the person who created the trust and transferred assets into it
  • The trustee(s) ‚Äî who control and manage the assets
  • The beneficiaries ‚Äî or, for discretionary trusts, the class of potential beneficiaries
  • Any person who exercises control over the trust
üí° The Key Principle

Your CDD obligation does not stop at the entity in front of you. You must always ask: who is the natural person ultimately behind this? If you cannot satisfactorily answer that question, you cannot proceed.

Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs)

PEPs are consistently one of the highest-risk categories in AML ‚Äî and one of the most heavily tested in the ICA exam. Public position gives opportunity; opportunity creates risk. Understanding who is a PEP, why they are high-risk, and what enhanced obligations apply is essential knowledge for anyone working in AML.

The Definition of a PEP

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî PEP Definition (MLRs 2017, Reg 35)

A Politically Exposed Person is an individual who is, or has been, entrusted with a prominent public function. The definition is extended to include their immediate family members and known close associates.

Categories of PEPs include:

  • Heads of state and heads of government
  • Senior politicians ‚Äî ministers, members of parliament, MEPs, senior officials of political parties
  • Senior government, judicial, and military officials
  • Senior executives of state-owned enterprises
  • Important political party officials

Family members include spouses/civil partners, children and their spouses/civil partners, and parents. Known close associates include business partners who are known to jointly own or control a business with the PEP.

Former PEPs retain their status for a risk-assessed cooling-off period after leaving office. There is no fixed statutory period ‚Äî the duration must be determined by risk assessment.

Why Are PEPs High-Risk?

The concern is straightforward: public office creates the opportunity to misuse it for personal financial gain ‚Äî through bribery, embezzlement of state funds, facilitation of contracts in exchange for kickbacks, or other forms of corruption. The resulting wealth ‚Äî known as proceeds of corruption ‚Äî then needs to be laundered.

High-profile cases illustrate the scale of the problem:

  • Former Nigerian dictator Sani Abacha and his associates are estimated to have stolen $3‚Äì5bn from state funds, much of it laundered through western banks and property markets
  • Azerbaijani officials were found to have moved hundreds of millions of pounds through UK property and luxury goods using shell companies and family members ‚Äî the "Azerbaijani Laundromat"
  • Property is a recurring theme: it is stable, appreciates in value, and can be titled in a family member's or company's name
üè† Property Sector Example

A senior official in a developing country's national oil company approaches an estate agent to purchase a ¬£3m London townhouse. His stated source of wealth is "salary and investments." His salary as a state oil executive is publicly known to be approximately ¬£60,000 per year. The gap between salary and purchase price is one of the clearest EDD red flags the ICA describes: the source of wealth claim is implausible and requires substantiation through documentation, not assertion.

The Domestic vs Foreign PEP Distinction

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Domestic vs Foreign PEPs

The MLRs 2017 introduced a tiered approach following the 4th EU Money Laundering Directive:

  • Foreign PEPs (from outside the EEA): automatic Enhanced Due Diligence. Senior management approval required before establishing a relationship.
  • Domestic PEPs (UK nationals in UK positions) and international organisation PEPs: must be identified, but EDD applies only where the firm's own risk assessment determines there is a higher risk. Lower-risk domestic PEPs may receive standard CDD.

The key distinction is that not all domestic PEPs automatically require EDD ‚Äî but all foreign PEPs do.

What Does EDD Look Like for a PEP?

Enhanced Due Diligence for PEPs goes significantly beyond standard CDD. The ICA specifies that EDD must include:

  • Establishing the source of wealth ‚Äî not just asking, but verifying with documentary evidence. "Business income" is not an acceptable answer on its own.
  • Establishing the source of funds ‚Äî specifically for the transaction in question
  • Senior management approval (for foreign PEPs) before establishing the relationship
  • Enhanced ongoing monitoring ‚Äî more frequent review of the relationship
  • Adverse media screening ‚Äî checking for reputational risks and corruption allegations
  • Understanding the nature and purpose of the relationship in more depth than for a standard client
üí° EDD Is Not About Refusing PEPs

Identifying a client as a PEP does not mean refusing to deal with them. PEPs are entitled to use regulated services. EDD means conducting a more thorough assessment of their risk profile and being satisfied that their wealth is legitimate. Refusing all PEPs would itself be discriminatory.

The Cooling-Off Period for Former PEPs

A key exam point: leaving public office does not immediately remove PEP status. A former prime minister, retired general, or ex-state executive retains their PEP classification for a period determined by risk assessment. Factors that affect the length of the cooling-off period include:

  • The nature and seniority of the office held
  • The jurisdiction ‚Äî higher-risk countries require longer cooling-off periods
  • Whether the person retains influence or connections from their former role
  • Whether there is any adverse information or ongoing investigation

Identifying Suspicious Activity

Spotting suspicious activity is not about certainty. It is not your job to prove money laundering is taking place ‚Äî that is the job of law enforcement. Your job is to recognise when something does not add up and to act on that recognition. This section covers the suspicion threshold, key red flags in property, and how plausibility testing works in practice.

The Suspicion Threshold

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The Suspicion Standard

The legal threshold for reporting is suspicion ‚Äî not proof, not certainty, and not even a balance of probabilities. POCA 2002 uses the phrase "knows or suspects or has reasonable grounds for knowing or suspecting."

The courts have defined suspicion as a state of mind more than mere speculation but below proof. Crucially, you do not need to:

  • Identify the specific predicate offence (what crime generated the money)
  • Have conclusive evidence
  • Be certain that ML is occurring

If the facts make you ask "this doesn't feel right ‚Äî how is this funded?" that instinct may be sufficient to trigger reporting. The test is objective: would a reasonable person in your position have suspected ML on these facts?

Red Flags in Property Transactions

The ICA, FATF, and HMRC all provide guidance on red flags specific to the property sector. The following are among the most commonly tested:

Red FlagWhy It Matters
Cash purchase without clear source of fundsProperty is a classic integration vehicle ‚Äî "clean" cash appears once it's converted into an asset

Plausibility Testing

Plausibility testing is the ICA's term for the active mental process of assessing whether a transaction "makes sense." It requires you to look at the whole picture and ask whether the client's stated purpose, financial profile, and transaction activity are consistent with each other.

The ICA describes plausibility testing as asking three key questions:

  1. Does this transaction fit the customer profile? Is the value, type, and source of funds consistent with what you know about this client?
  2. Is the explanation given by the customer credible? Does it make commercial and financial sense?
  3. Is there a rational, legitimate reason for this transaction? Can you construct a plausible legitimate explanation, or does the only explanation point toward criminal activity?
üìã Scenario ‚Äî Plausibility Testing in Practice

A buyer approaches an estate agent. They are purchasing a three-bedroom flat in Manchester for ¬£420,000. They say they work as a self-employed builder. They want to pay entirely in cash from a holdall. They cannot produce any bank statements, and they say their accountant "handles all of that." They want to complete within two weeks.

Applying plausibility testing:

Profile fit: A self-employed builder paying ¬£420,000 cash, with no accessible bank account evidence, is implausible on a financial basis. Even a high-earning sole trader would have a paper trail.

Explanation credibility: "My accountant handles it" is not an explanation of source of funds. It is a deflection.

Legitimate explanation: There is no rational, lawful reason to pay ¬£420,000 in cash from a holdall with no bank documentation.

Conclusion: This is a textbook SAR situation. The agent should not complete the transaction before the MLRO has been notified and has made a decision on external reporting.

‚ö†Ô∏è Wilful Blindness

The law does not allow you to "not look" in order to avoid suspicion. If you deliberately fail to ask obvious questions, avoid collecting information that would reveal a red flag, or close your eyes to the obvious, a court can find that you had reasonable grounds to know or suspect ‚Äî even if you claim you did not actually suspect. This is known as wilful blindness and it does not provide a legal defence.

The SAR Process

Once a suspicion is formed, there is a clear legal process for reporting it. Understanding this process ‚Äî from the initial internal report through to the MLRO's decision and any necessary contact with the NCA ‚Äî is essential. Getting it wrong can mean criminal liability for you personally.

The Two-Stage Reporting Structure

In a regulated firm, suspicious activity is not reported directly to law enforcement by individual employees. Instead, there is a structured two-stage process:

  • Stage 1 ‚Äî Internal report: The employee who forms a suspicion makes a report to the firm's MLRO (Money Laundering Reporting Officer) or their nominated deputy. This is typically done through an internal SAR or disclosure form.
  • Stage 2 ‚Äî External SAR: The MLRO assesses the internal report. If they concur that there are grounds for suspicion, they file a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) with the National Crime Agency (NCA) through the SAR Online portal.
üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The MLRO's Role in SAR Decisions

The MLRO is the sole gateway through which SARs pass to the NCA. Individual employees do not contact the NCA directly (unless they are the MLRO).

If an employee makes an internal report and the MLRO decides not to file externally, the MLRO must be able to demonstrate a rational basis for that decision. The employee who made the internal report in good faith is protected by having done so ‚Äî even if the MLRO disagrees with their assessment.

However, if the employee believes the MLRO's decision is itself wrong or that the MLRO may be involved in the suspicious activity, they should consider direct escalation to senior management or, in exceptional circumstances, directly to the NCA or regulator.

What a Good SAR Contains

A SAR is not just a notification. It is an intelligence document that helps law enforcement identify and track criminal activity. A high-quality SAR should include:

  • Full identification details of the subject (name, address, date of birth, ID numbers where known)
  • The nature of the suspicious activity or transaction
  • The specific grounds for suspicion ‚Äî exactly what triggered the concern and why
  • All relevant financial details ‚Äî amounts, accounts, currencies, transaction dates
  • Any other intelligence the firm holds that may be relevant
  • Whether any action is needed before the firm can act (i.e., whether a DAML is required)

Defence Against Money Laundering (DAML) SARs

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî DAML SARs

A DAML SAR (previously called a "consent SAR") is filed when a firm needs to proceed with a transaction that it suspects involves criminal property ‚Äî but proceeding without consent would itself be an offence under POCA (typically s.328: entering into arrangements).

The process:

  1. File the DAML SAR with the NCA before proceeding
  2. The NCA has 7 working days to respond
  3. If the NCA refuses consent within 7 days, it has a further 31-day moratorium period to investigate
  4. If no response is received within 7 working days, the firm may proceed with the defence intact

During the waiting period, you cannot proceed with the transaction ‚Äî and you cannot tell the client why. This creates the practical tension managed by the tipping off rules covered in the next section.

üè† Property Sector ‚Äî DAML in Practice

A conveyancer is mid-transaction when they realise the source of funds is potentially suspect. Completion is scheduled for Friday. They cannot simply walk away without explanation. The firm files a DAML SAR on Monday. The NCA has 7 working days ‚Äî so in this scenario, the NCA's window does not expire before the planned completion. The conveyancer must explain the delay without revealing that a SAR has been filed. This is where careful, neutral language is essential.

Statutory Protection for Reporters

A person who makes a SAR in good faith is protected from civil liability ‚Äî they cannot be sued by the client for breach of confidentiality, damage to reputation, or other civil claims arising from the report. This protection applies as long as the report was made honestly and on reasonable grounds.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî HSBC v Shah [2012]

In HSBC Bank Plc v Shah and others [2012], the UK Court of Appeal ruled that a bank does not have to disclose the identity of employees who make internal SARs, even in the course of litigation. This case provides important protection for employees who report in good faith ‚Äî their identity and their report are both protected. Staff should feel confident that internal reporting is safe.

Confidentiality & Tipping Off

Filing a SAR creates an immediate and significant practical problem: you are conducting business with a client you now suspect of money laundering, and you cannot tell them why you are delaying or what you are doing. This tension is managed by the tipping off rules ‚Äî one of the most important and most examined areas of AML law.

The Tipping Off Offence

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Tipping Off Under POCA 2002, s.333A

The offence of tipping off occurs when a person:

  1. Knows or suspects that a SAR has been made (internally to the MLRO or externally to the NCA); and
  2. Discloses information that is likely to prejudice any investigation that may arise from that SAR

The offence can be committed by disclosing to:

  • The client who is the subject of the SAR
  • A third party ‚Äî such as their solicitor, accountant, or family member
  • Colleagues within the firm who have no need to know
  • The press or public

The maximum penalty is 5 years' imprisonment. Tipping off does not require intent ‚Äî an innocent, inadvertent disclosure is still an offence if it prejudices an investigation.

What Constitutes Tipping Off?

Any disclosure that gives the subject reasonable grounds to believe that a SAR has been filed or that they are under investigation can constitute tipping off. Examples include:

  • Telling a client "we've had to report you to the authorities"
  • Saying "there's a compliance issue with your account that I can't discuss"
  • Responding to a client's question about a delay with "it's out of my hands"
  • Emailing a colleague "we've filed a SAR on the Patel transaction ‚Äî keep it quiet"
  • Asking a client follow-up questions in a way that signals investigation is underway
üìã Scenario ‚Äî Handling the Awkward Call

A buyer's solicitor calls you on a Wednesday afternoon. Completion was supposed to be on Monday. The solicitor is demanding an explanation. Your MLRO filed a DAML with the NCA on the previous Thursday ‚Äî you are still within the 7-working-day window. What do you say?

Acceptable: "We have some internal compliance procedures to complete in line with our regulatory obligations. We hope to be in a position to update you shortly and we understand this is causing frustration."

Not acceptable: "We've had to file a suspicious activity report and we're waiting for clearance from the NCA." ‚Äî This is tipping off, even if said with the best of intentions. It is a criminal offence.

Not acceptable: "It's nothing to worry about ‚Äî just a routine check." ‚Äî Providing false reassurance that there is no issue may also be problematic, and potentially misleading.

Permitted Disclosures ‚Äî The Safe Harbour

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Permitted Disclosures

Not all disclosures are tipping off. POCA 2002 and the MLRs 2017 provide a safe harbour for certain permitted disclosures:

  • Within the same firm: Disclosure to colleagues with a genuine need to know in order to perform their role ‚Äî for example, the MLRO, compliance officers, or the partner handling a transaction ‚Äî is permitted. But "need to know" is strictly interpreted: curiosity is not a basis for disclosure.
  • Between regulated firms: Where two regulated firms are involved in the same transaction (e.g., a law firm and a bank), they may disclose to each other that a SAR has been made if they are both subject to equivalent AML obligations and the disclosure is necessary for the purpose of preventing ML/TF.
  • To law enforcement: Disclosure to police, HMRC, the NCA or other authorities is not tipping off.
  • To legal advisers: Seeking legal advice from a solicitor about your own obligations is protected.

The Prejudicing an Investigation Offence

Separate from tipping off, POCA 2002 creates an offence of prejudicing an investigation. This applies to a wider set of circumstances and catches actions (not just disclosures) that interfere with a law enforcement investigation ‚Äî such as destroying documents, moving assets, or warning associates.

Balancing Client Confidentiality and AML Obligations

The standard professional duty of client confidentiality does not override AML reporting obligations. This is explicitly established in UK law: AML obligations under POCA take priority. Filing a SAR is not a breach of confidentiality ‚Äî it is a legal requirement.

However, the obligation cuts both ways: the confidentiality of the SAR itself is also protected. You cannot disclose the existence or content of a SAR to anyone other than those with a genuine legitimate purpose.
