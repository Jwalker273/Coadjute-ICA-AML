Module 06 — Section 1 of 6

Ongoing Monitoring

Completing customer due diligence at onboarding is only the beginning. AML obligations do not end when the relationship starts — they continue throughout its entire life. This section examines what ongoing monitoring means in practice, what the law requires, and what it looks like in a property transaction context.

Why Monitoring Is Not Optional

A firm that performs thorough CDD at the start of a relationship but never reviews it again is only partially compliant. Criminals are sophisticated: they may present clean at onboarding and only introduce suspicious elements later. The risk profile of a client can also change legitimately — through changes in business, geography, or wealth — which the firm needs to reflect.

Ongoing monitoring serves two purposes: it keeps CDD information accurate and current, and it identifies unusual or suspicious transactions that warrant investigation or reporting.

📘 ICA Exam Note — Regulation 28 MLRs 2017

Regulation 28 of the Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds (Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017 imposes an obligation of ongoing monitoring throughout a business relationship. This requires firms to:

  • Scrutinise transactions to ensure they are consistent with the firm's knowledge of the customer, their business, and their risk profile
  • Keep CDD documents, data, and information up to date and relevant
  • Apply the obligation on a risk-sensitive basis — more intensive monitoring for higher-risk relationships

The key phrase is "risk-sensitive basis" — monitoring is not identical for every client. A low-risk domestic buyer completing a standard residential purchase requires less intensive monitoring than a PEP purchasing through an offshore structure.

What Does "Scrutinising Transactions" Actually Mean?

In a property context, scrutiny means asking whether what the client is doing makes sense given what you know about them. You should be asking:

  • Is the transaction consistent with the customer's stated purpose and the type of business you expected to conduct?
  • Does the source of funds match what the customer told you about their financial background?
  • Does the level of activity match the risk profile established at onboarding?
  • Has the customer's behaviour changed in a way that suggests a change in risk — or something more sinister?
🏠 Property Sector Example

An estate agent onboards a developer client who says they typically sell properties in the £300,000–£500,000 range to first-time buyers. Six months later, the same client attempts to instruct the agent on an £8m commercial site purchase, paid entirely in cash from a newly opened account in Cyprus. This is inconsistent with the established risk profile and warrants immediate review — not because cash or Cyprus accounts are automatically suspicious, but because the dramatic shift in transaction type, value, and funding method is unexplained.

When Must You Review CDD?

Monitoring is not just a periodic tick-box exercise. The ICA and the MLRs 2017 identify specific triggers that require a CDD review, regardless of when the last review was conducted:

  • Material change in the customer's circumstances — new business interests, change of address to a high-risk jurisdiction, change of beneficial ownership
  • Adverse media or intelligence — the customer features in news reports linking them to crime, corruption, or sanctions
  • Unusual transaction activity — transactions that do not match the established profile
  • Change in the nature of the relationship — a residential customer begins conducting commercial transactions
  • Notification from a third party — law enforcement contact, a notification from another regulated firm, or information from a sanctions list update

Automated Transaction Monitoring Systems

Larger firms, including banks and larger estate agency groups, use automated transaction monitoring (ATM) systems to flag unusual patterns. These systems apply rules-based and algorithmic monitoring across large volumes of transactions — for example, flagging multiple purchases by the same buyer in a short period, or payments from jurisdictions on watch lists.

However, the ICA is clear that automated systems are tools, not replacements for human judgement. A flagged transaction still requires a human to assess whether it is genuinely suspicious. Alert fatigue — where staff dismiss alerts because they are too frequent — is one of the most significant AML risks in firms that rely heavily on automated systems.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Many firms interpret ongoing monitoring as an annual review cycle for all clients. This is incorrect. Regulation 28 requires monitoring that is responsive to risk. A scheduled annual review does not substitute for an immediate review when material adverse information emerges. Both obligations can exist simultaneously.

A conveyancer reviewed a client's CDD 18 months ago and assessed them as low-risk. That client has now been named in a national newspaper report as a subject of a Serious Fraud Office investigation. The next scheduled review is in 6 months. What should the conveyancer do?

Beneficial Ownership

You already know what a beneficial owner is and why the 25% threshold matters — that was Module 5. This section focuses on what actually goes wrong in verification practice: where the PSC register fails you, what to do when a chain goes offshore, and how to handle the situations where a client won't — or genuinely can't — provide you with the information you need.

Where Verification Breaks Down in Practice

In theory, tracing a beneficial owner is straightforward: follow the ownership chain until you reach a natural person. In practice, you will routinely hit one of these dead ends:

  • The chain goes offshore. A UK company's PSC register shows another company — incorporated in the BVI, Cayman, or Panama — as its sole shareholder. There is no public equivalent of Companies House in those jurisdictions. You are now entirely dependent on what the client chooses to tell you.
  • Nominees are disclosed but the real controller isn't. A client confirms that nominee directors are in place and even provides their names. This tells you nothing about who instructs those nominees. The identity of the true controller can still be concealed behind a compliant-looking set of nominees.
  • Discretionary trust beneficiaries are unidentified. Unlike fixed-interest trusts, discretionary trusts do not name fixed beneficiaries at outset — the trustees have discretion over who benefits and when. A trustee may legitimately say no beneficiaries are currently determined. This is not necessarily evasion, but it leaves you unable to complete the verification chain.
  • Circular ownership structures. Company A owns Company B, which owns Company C, whose sole shareholder appears to be Company A. Circular structures designed to prevent tracing are a known ML technique and a clear red flag in their own right.
⚠️ Cannot Identify the UBO? You Cannot Proceed

The ICA's position is explicit: if you cannot satisfactorily identify the beneficial owner after reasonable enquiry, you cannot complete the transaction. You should also consider whether the refusal or inability to provide the information is itself a red flag warranting an internal report to the MLRO. The absence of information is not neutral — it is a risk indicator.

The PSC Register — More Limited Than It Looks

The Persons with Significant Control register at Companies House is a useful starting point, but it has real limitations that matter for verification:

  • Self-reported and unverified. Companies House does not verify what is filed. A company can list a nominee, an incorrect person, or a false entry — and the register will reflect it. Treating a PSC register entry as conclusive is a compliance failure.
  • Corporate PSCs still need tracing. If the PSC entry shows another company rather than a natural person, your obligation is not met. The register has told you where to look next, not who the UBO is.
  • Non-compliance is widespread. Failure to file accurate PSC information is a known enforcement gap, particularly in higher-risk sectors. Do not assume a company has properly disclosed.
📘 ICA Exam Note — Register of Overseas Entities (ROE)

Since January 2023, overseas entities that own UK land must register with Companies House under the Economic Crime (Transparency and Enforcement) Act 2022 and disclose their beneficial owners. For any property transaction involving an overseas entity, checking the ROE is mandatory — not optional. An overseas entity that refuses to register, or discloses nominees without identifying natural persons behind them, is a significant red flag requiring immediate MLRO escalation.

When a Trustee Refuses to Disclose Beneficiaries

A common resistance in property transactions is the claim that trust documents are confidential and that a trustee is not obliged to disclose beneficiary details to a third party. This is sometimes true as a matter of trust law — but it does not override your AML obligations.

As a regulated firm, you are legally entitled to request the information required to comply with the MLRs. If a trustee refuses to cooperate with reasonable and proportionate requests, that refusal is a red flag — not a legitimate reason to proceed without the information.

🏠 The "Family Trust" Deflection — What Coadjute's Team Should Know

One of the most common deflections in property transactions processed through Coadjute's platform is "the purchase funds are coming from a family trust." This sounds benign but answers almost nothing: Who is the settlor? Who are the trustees? Who are the beneficiaries — or what class of people could benefit? What is the trust's source of assets?

"Family trust" as a complete answer to source of funds is not acceptable. The response should always be to request the trust deed, trustee identification, and beneficiary class. If that documentation is refused, escalate to the MLRO before proceeding.

✋ Quick Check
A property buyer is "Global Estates Ltd", a UK-registered company. Its PSC register shows "International Holdings Corp" (BVI-registered) as the sole person with significant control. The client says the BVI company's ownership is "private and not publicly available." What is the correct approach?
Module 06 — Section 3 of 6

Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs)

Module 5 covered who PEPs are, why they require EDD, and what source of wealth verification involves. This section goes into the harder questions — the ones that generate compliance failures in practice: who counts as a close associate, how long a cooling-off period should last, what adverse media screening actually involves, and where the domestic/foreign distinction gets complicated.

The Edge Cases That Catch People Out

Close Associates — Wider Than You Think

The MLRs 2017 extend PEP status to "known close associates" — a category that goes beyond family and catches people that firms routinely fail to flag. A known close associate is not limited to immediate social contacts. It includes:

  • Business partners who are known to jointly own or control a business or business relationship with the PEP
  • Any natural person who is the sole beneficial owner of a legal entity or arrangement set up for the benefit of the PEP

The word "known" matters: you are not required to investigate every client for hidden connections to PEPs. But if information emerges during CDD — in a company structure, a business filing, a news search, or the client's own disclosure — that a business partner is a PEP, the close associate obligation is triggered.

The Cooling-Off Period — No Fixed Duration

There is no statutory cooling-off period for former PEPs in the UK. The MLRs require a risk assessment — and that assessment must be proportionate and documented. In practice, this means:

FactorEffect on Cooling-Off Period
Seniority of the role heldA former head of state requires a much longer period than a former local councillor
Jurisdiction riskA former minister from a high-corruption country requires longer than one from a low-risk FATF-member country
Retained influenceIf the person continues to advise, consult, or hold positions in connected bodies, their risk profile may not diminish
Ongoing investigations or adverse mediaAny unresolved allegations effectively extend the period indefinitely until resolved
Nature of wealth accumulatedWealth accumulated during tenure in a high-corruption jurisdiction requires more scrutiny for longer
📘 ICA Exam Note — Domestic vs Foreign PEPs: The Key Distinction

The MLRs 2017 introduced a tiered approach — but it is frequently misapplied in exam questions. The correct rule is:

  • Foreign PEPs (those from outside the EEA/UK): automatic EDD. Senior management approval required before establishing a relationship. No discretion.
  • Domestic PEPs (UK nationals in UK public positions) and international organisation PEPs: must be identified, but EDD only applies if the firm's own risk assessment determines higher risk. A lower-risk domestic PEP may receive standard CDD.

Common exam trap: EU member state officials are foreign PEPs under UK MLRs, not domestic PEPs. EEA membership does not make someone a domestic PEP for a UK firm.

Adverse Media Screening — What It Actually Requires

EDD for PEPs includes adverse media screening, but firms often apply this too narrowly — searching only for the client's name in a basic news search. Effective adverse media screening for PEPs should cover:

  • The PEP's own name — including variations, transliterations, and aliases
  • Their immediate family members named in the CDD file
  • The companies, trusts, and business interests disclosed in the CDD process
  • Sources including international news, NGO reports (Transparency International, Global Witness), court records, and official sanctions lists
  • Not just direct ML/fraud allegations — corruption, bribery, political controversy, and unexplained wealth are all relevant

Adverse media screening is not a one-time exercise at onboarding. For PEPs, it is part of the enhanced ongoing monitoring obligation — a change in political fortune or a new investigation can emerge at any point in the relationship.

🏠 The Close Associate Problem in Property Transactions

A conveyancer is acting on a £2.1m residential purchase. The buyer is a UK national with no apparent PEP flags. During source of funds review, it emerges that the purchase funds come from a business sale. The co-owner of that business — who received the other half of the sale proceeds — is a recently retired regional governor from a Central Asian state. The buyer is now a "known close associate" of a foreign PEP. EDD is triggered — not just for the governor, but for the implications of the shared business relationship and the origin of the buyer's share of the sale proceeds.

💡 EDD Does Not Mean Refusal

Being a PEP does not disqualify a client from using regulated services. Many PEPs — including the majority of UK domestic PEPs — are entirely legitimate. EDD means a more thorough, documented assessment. Blanket refusal of PEP clients is not an AML requirement and could itself constitute discrimination.

✋ Quick Check
A client is a former Minister of Finance from an Eastern European EU member state. They left office two years ago and are purchasing a property for £950,000. Their stated source of wealth is "ministerial salary and property investments." Which statement is correct?

Identifying Suspicious Activity

Module 5 covered the suspicion threshold, the reporting obligation, and the basics of plausibility testing. This section goes further — focusing on how red flags combine and escalate, how the objective test works under pressure, wilful blindness, and the harder judgement calls that arise in real property transactions.

Red Flag Combinations — When One Becomes Many

Individual red flags are sometimes explicable. It is the combination of multiple flags, or the pattern of a client's behaviour over time, that typically crosses the threshold into reportable suspicion. The ICA does not expect you to refuse every cash buyer or question every urgency request — it expects you to assess the overall picture.

ScenarioSingle Flag ReadingCombined Flag Reading
Cash purchase of £800,000Unusual but not inherently suspicious — some buyers do have liquid assetsCash purchase + urgency + buyer indifferent to price + third-party funds from unrelated company + vague source of wealth = strong suspicion profile
Property purchased rapidly and then re-soldCould be a legitimate investor strategy or change of circumstancesRapid resale at a loss + buyer originally paid above market value + no apparent commercial rationale = classic layering pattern requiring investigation
Complex corporate structureSome legitimate businesses do use multi-layer structures for tax or commercial reasonsComplex structure + offshore jurisdiction + nominee directors + refusal to identify UBO = the structure exists to obscure, not for commercial reasons
Third-party source of fundsFamily gifts are common, especially for first-time buyersThird-party funds + giftor is an unrelated corporate entity + giftor refuses to provide ID + buyer cannot explain the relationship = not a normal family gift

The Objective Test Under Pressure

The legal test for whether a report should be made is objective: would a reasonable, informed professional in your position have suspected ML on these facts? This matters in two directions:

  • It protects you when you report in good faith. If a reasonable professional would have suspected ML, and you reported, you are protected — even if the suspicion turns out to be wrong. POCA provides a statutory defence for good-faith reporters.
  • It exposes you when you don't report. If a reasonable professional would have suspected ML, and you failed to report because you were reluctant, liked the client, or were under commercial pressure to complete — the objective test means that position is not defensible. Your subjective comfort with the situation is not the standard.
⚠️ Wilful Blindness — A Criminal Standard

The law does not permit you to deliberately avoid learning information that would create suspicion. If you ask fewer questions than you should, decline to follow up on an obvious inconsistency, or instruct a colleague not to dig further into a transaction — a court can find that you had "reasonable grounds to know or suspect" ML, even though you claim you did not actually suspect it.

This is not a theoretical risk. Wilful blindness is how many AML failures in regulated firms are prosecuted — not as outright complicity, but as a deliberate avoidance of knowledge. Commercial pressure ("this is a big transaction and the client will walk"), relationship pressure ("I've known this client for years"), and workload pressure ("I don't have time to investigate") are not legal defences against the wilful blindness standard.

The "Won't Ask, Won't Tell" Culture

A particularly dangerous compliance failure occurs when the culture within a team normalises not asking difficult questions. This can take several forms:

  • Managers who signal that a transaction is important commercially and should not be delayed by "unnecessary" compliance queries
  • Informal pressure on junior staff not to raise concerns that might upset a client or lose a fee
  • A pattern of internal reports being dismissed by the MLRO without proper documentation of the decision
  • A firm-wide assumption that certain transaction types (e.g. repeat developer clients) are "known" and do not need fresh scrutiny

Any of these patterns can constitute a systemic AML failure, and individual employees are not protected by a manager's apparent comfort with the situation. Your own reporting obligation runs to the MLRO — not to your line manager's assessment of commercial risk.

📋 Scenario — Pressure to Complete

A conveyancer is three days from completion on a £1.2m property purchase. The buyer has been a client of the firm for two years — two previous standard transactions, both unremarkable. This transaction is different: the purchase price is 20% above the valuation, the funds have arrived from a newly opened account in a jurisdiction on the UK's high-risk list, and the buyer's solicitor has been pressing unusually hard for completion.

The conveyancer raises it with their manager. The manager says: "We know this client. It's probably a currency issue with their overseas account — just complete." The conveyancer is uncomfortable but says nothing further.

The compliance failure: Two previous clean transactions do not establish a pattern that rules out ML in a third. The combination of above-market price, high-risk jurisdiction funds, and unusual urgency is a red flag cluster. The conveyancer had an independent obligation to report to the MLRO — not to accept the manager's dismissal as a final answer. Completing under these circumstances without an MLRO review may constitute both wilful blindness and a failure to make a required report.

The employee raises it with their manager, who seems unconcerned and says to proceed. What should the employee do?

The SAR Process

Once a suspicion is formed, there is a clear legal process for reporting it. Understanding this process — from the initial internal report through to the MLRO's decision and any necessary contact with the NCA — is essential. Getting it wrong can mean criminal liability for you personally.

The Two-Stage Reporting Structure

In a regulated firm, suspicious activity is not reported directly to law enforcement by individual employees. Instead, there is a structured two-stage process:

  • Stage 1 — Internal report: The employee who forms a suspicion makes a report to the firm's MLRO (Money Laundering Reporting Officer) or their nominated deputy. This is typically done through an internal SAR or disclosure form.
  • Stage 2 — External SAR: The MLRO assesses the internal report. If they concur that there are grounds for suspicion, they file a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) with the National Crime Agency (NCA) through the SAR Online portal.
📘 ICA Exam Note — The MLRO's Role in SAR Decisions

The MLRO is the sole gateway through which SARs pass to the NCA. Individual employees do not contact the NCA directly (unless they are the MLRO).

If an employee makes an internal report and the MLRO decides not to file externally, the MLRO must be able to demonstrate a rational basis for that decision. The employee who made the internal report in good faith is protected by having done so — even if the MLRO disagrees with their assessment.

However, if the employee believes the MLRO's decision is itself wrong or that the MLRO may be involved in the suspicious activity, they should consider direct escalation to senior management or, in exceptional circumstances, directly to the NCA or regulator.

What a Good SAR Contains

A SAR is not just a notification. It is an intelligence document that helps law enforcement identify and track criminal activity. A high-quality SAR should include:

  • Full identification details of the subject (name, address, date of birth, ID numbers where known)
  • The nature of the suspicious activity or transaction
  • The specific grounds for suspicion — exactly what triggered the concern and why
  • All relevant financial details — amounts, accounts, currencies, transaction dates
  • Any other intelligence the firm holds that may be relevant
  • Whether any action is needed before the firm can act (i.e., whether a DAML is required)

Defence Against Money Laundering (DAML) SARs

📘 ICA Exam Note — DAML SARs

A DAML SAR (previously called a "consent SAR") is filed when a firm needs to proceed with a transaction that it suspects involves criminal property — but proceeding without consent would itself be an offence under POCA (typically s.328: entering into arrangements).

The process:

  1. File the DAML SAR with the NCA before proceeding
  2. The NCA has 7 working days to respond
  3. If the NCA refuses consent within 7 days, it has a further 31-day moratorium period to investigate
  4. If no response is received within 7 working days, the firm may proceed with the defence intact

During the waiting period, you cannot proceed with the transaction — and you cannot tell the client why. This creates the practical tension managed by the tipping off rules covered in the next section.

🏠 Property Sector — DAML in Practice

A conveyancer is mid-transaction when they realise the source of funds is potentially suspect. Completion is scheduled for Friday. They cannot simply walk away without explanation. The firm files a DAML SAR on Monday. The NCA has 7 working days — so in this scenario, the NCA's window does not expire before the planned completion. The conveyancer must explain the delay without revealing that a SAR has been filed. This is where careful, neutral language is essential.

Statutory Protection for Reporters

A person who makes a SAR in good faith is protected from civil liability — they cannot be sued by the client for breach of confidentiality, damage to reputation, or other civil claims arising from the report. This protection applies as long as the report was made honestly and on reasonable grounds.

📘 ICA Exam Note — HSBC v Shah [2012]

In HSBC Bank Plc v Shah and others [2012], the UK Court of Appeal ruled that a bank does not have to disclose the identity of employees who make internal SARs, even in the course of litigation. This case provides important protection for employees who report in good faith — their identity and their report are both protected. Staff should feel confident that internal reporting is safe.

Confidentiality & Tipping Off

Filing a SAR creates an immediate and significant practical problem: you are conducting business with a client you now suspect of money laundering, and you cannot tell them why you are delaying or what you are doing. This tension is managed by the tipping off rules — one of the most important and most examined areas of AML law.

The Tipping Off Offence

📘 ICA Exam Note — Tipping Off Under POCA 2002, s.333A

The offence of tipping off occurs when a person:

  1. Knows or suspects that a SAR has been made (internally to the MLRO or externally to the NCA); and
  2. Discloses information that is likely to prejudice any investigation that may arise from that SAR

The offence can be committed by disclosing to:

  • The client who is the subject of the SAR
  • A third party — such as their solicitor, accountant, or family member
  • Colleagues within the firm who have no need to know
  • The press or public

The maximum penalty is 5 years' imprisonment. Tipping off does not require intent — an innocent, inadvertent disclosure is still an offence if it prejudices an investigation.

What Constitutes Tipping Off?

Any disclosure that gives the subject reasonable grounds to believe that a SAR has been filed or that they are under investigation can constitute tipping off. Examples include:

  • Telling a client "we've had to report you to the authorities"
  • Saying "there's a compliance issue with your account that I can't discuss"
  • Responding to a client's question about a delay with "it's out of my hands"
  • Emailing a colleague "we've filed a SAR on the Patel transaction — keep it quiet"
  • Asking a client follow-up questions in a way that signals investigation is underway
📋 Scenario — Handling the Awkward Call

A buyer's solicitor calls you on a Wednesday afternoon. Completion was supposed to be on Monday. The solicitor is demanding an explanation. Your MLRO filed a DAML with the NCA on the previous Thursday — you are still within the 7-working-day window. What do you say?

Acceptable: "We have some internal compliance procedures to complete in line with our regulatory obligations. We hope to be in a position to update you shortly and we understand this is causing frustration."

Not acceptable: "We've had to file a suspicious activity report and we're waiting for clearance from the NCA." — This is tipping off, even if said with the best of intentions. It is a criminal offence.

Not acceptable: "It's nothing to worry about — just a routine check." — Providing false reassurance that there is no issue may also be problematic, and potentially misleading.

Permitted Disclosures — The Safe Harbour

📘 ICA Exam Note — Permitted Disclosures

Not all disclosures are tipping off. POCA 2002 and the MLRs 2017 provide a safe harbour for certain permitted disclosures:

  • Within the same firm: Disclosure to colleagues with a genuine need to know in order to perform their role — for example, the MLRO, compliance officers, or the partner handling a transaction — is permitted. But "need to know" is strictly interpreted: curiosity is not a basis for disclosure.
  • Between regulated firms: Where two regulated firms are involved in the same transaction (e.g., a law firm and a bank), they may disclose to each other that a SAR has been made if they are both subject to equivalent AML obligations and the disclosure is necessary for the purpose of preventing ML/TF.
  • To law enforcement: Disclosure to police, HMRC, the NCA or other authorities is not tipping off.
  • To legal advisers: Seeking legal advice from a solicitor about your own obligations is protected.

The Prejudicing an Investigation Offence

Separate from tipping off, POCA 2002 creates an offence of prejudicing an investigation. This applies to a wider set of circumstances and catches actions (not just disclosures) that interfere with a law enforcement investigation — such as destroying documents, moving assets, or warning associates.

Balancing Client Confidentiality and AML Obligations

The standard professional duty of client confidentiality does not override AML reporting obligations. This is explicitly established in UK law: AML obligations under POCA take priority. Filing a SAR is not a breach of confidentiality — it is a legal requirement.

However, the obligation cuts both ways: the confidentiality of the SAR itself is also protected. You cannot disclose the existence or content of a SAR to anyone other than those with a genuine legitimate purpose.
