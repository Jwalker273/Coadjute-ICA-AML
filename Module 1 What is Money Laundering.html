What is Money Laundering?

Most people have heard the term ‚Äî but the real legal definition is far wider than "dirty money" or "drug money." Understanding exactly what the law means is the foundation of everything that follows.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Core Definition
"Quite simply, money laundering occurs every time any transaction takes place, or any relationship is formed that involves any form of property or benefit that has come from any crime."
This is deliberately wide. Any property. Any crime. A luxury watch bought with stolen cash. Fraud proceeds sitting in a bank account. Property purchased through a corrupt offshore company.

The Three Key Objectives

Whatever the method, money laundering always pursues one or more of these goals:

i. Disguise the source of criminal property ii. Conceal ownership of criminal property iii. Control and benefit from the proceeds securely
üí° Consider ‚Äî Who Gets Caught Up?

You might assume that honest, reputable firms are rarely affected. The opposite is true. Criminals actively seek out legitimate businesses to process their funds. The ICA lists those at highest risk: banks, insurers, lawyers, accountants, trust companies, money transmitters, betting firms, real estate agents, high-value goods retailers, and virtual currency businesses.

Reputational damage, criminal prosecution, regulatory censure, and civil proceedings can all follow ‚Äî even for firms who had no idea what was happening.

A Simple Example ‚Äî How Far the Law Reaches

A drug dealer gives ¬£100 to a friend and asks them to hide it under their mattress until "the coast is clear." Is the friend laundering money?
Yes. The friend has entered into an arrangement involving the drug dealer's proceeds of crime. Whether a criminal offence has been committed depends on what the friend knew or suspected ‚Äî a critical distinction we cover in Section 4.
üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî AML vs CFT
AML (Anti Money Laundering) ‚Äî legislation and procedures designed to detect and prevent criminals from disguising the proceeds of crime.

CFT / CTF (Countering the Financing of Terrorism) ‚Äî making it illegal to support terrorists financially and materially. Focus intensified significantly after 11 September 2001.
While often grouped together, money laundering and terrorist financing are distinct ‚Äî covered in Module 2.

The Three Stages of Money Laundering

The three-stage model is fundamental to the ICA exam. You must be able to define each stage precisely, recognise real-world examples, and ‚Äî critically ‚Äî understand where the model breaks down.

üí∞

1. Placement

Cash from criminal activity is introduced into the financial system. Criminals are most vulnerable at this stage ‚Äî cash may be contaminated with drugs, security dye, or forensic markers.

üîÄ

2. Layering

A series of transactions designed to hide the source and ownership of funds, confuse the audit trail, and create distance between the criminal and the money.

üè†

3. Integration

Laundered funds re-enter the legitimate economy appearing to come from a legitimate source. The property technically remains the proceeds of crime.

Placement ‚Äî Techniques in Practice

Direct cash deposits were once common, but modern AML legislation has made this dangerous. Cash transactions over threshold (¬£10,000 UK / $10,000 US / ‚Ç¨10,000 EU) must be reported. Criminals have adapted:

Smurfing / Structured Deposits

Large sums broken into smaller amounts ‚Äî each below the reporting threshold ‚Äî deposited across multiple accounts and banks. Requires a network of people ("smurfs") acting together.

Currency Conversion into High-Value Assets

Cash used to buy real estate, precious metals, art, luxury watches, or motor vehicles ‚Äî which are later sold for "clean" funds. Items can be transported internationally to add distance from the crime.

Cash-Intensive Business Commingling

Criminal cash mixed with legitimate takings of a cash-heavy business (restaurants, car washes, nail bars) and declared as business income ‚Äî almost impossible to separate after the fact.

üè° Property Sector Example

A buyer arrives with a large cash deposit, splitting payment across multiple sources. This is a classic placement red flag. By the time your firm is involved, layering may already have begun ‚Äî your CDD at this point is critical.

Layering ‚Äî Creating Distance

Once placed, criminals create as many transaction layers as possible: offshore accounts, shell companies, complex chains across multiple jurisdictions, and increasingly virtual currencies which add technical complexity. The goal is always to put maximum distance between the criminal and the money.

üìù Note

Even when proceeds are initially generated electronically (e.g. fraud), criminals often withdraw cash, transport it internationally, and redeposit it ‚Äî deliberately creating a messy, multi-step paper trail to confuse investigators.

Integration ‚Äî Reintegration Methods

At integration, laundered money re-enters the economy looking legitimate. The ICA identifies specific methods you should know:

MethodHow It Works
Loan-backMoney transferred from an overseas account controlled by the launderer and presented as a legitimate loan to themselves
Sham transactionsPayments disguised as proceeds of a real estate deal, royalty, or purchase of goods/services that never existed ‚Äî supported by false invoices at inflated prices
Inheritance / GiftFunds in another country presented as an inheritance or gift when transferred home
Insurance policy redemptionFunds placed with an insurer as a lump sum; later cashed in so the payment appears to come from a legitimate financial institution
Sale of assetsA property, yacht, or vehicle originally purchased with criminal funds sold to a legitimate buyer ‚Äî the sale proceeds appear entirely clean
üè° Property Sector ‚Äî Rapid Resale

An offshore company purchases a property in cash. Six months later it is sold at full market value to a legitimate buyer. The sale proceeds are entirely "clean" ‚Äî paid by an innocent party. Your firm may be the final step in a laundering chain without ever knowing it.

Limitations of the Three-Stage Model

The three-stage model is a useful teaching tool ‚Äî but the ICA is explicit that understanding its limitations is just as important as knowing the model itself. This is a tested exam topic.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Key Learning Point
"Money laundering does not always occur in a manner that fits into the three-stage model."
The model assumes all crime generates cash that is then deposited into the financial system. This is frequently not the case.

Two Core Limitations

1. Not all crime produces cash

Financial crime ‚Äî fraud, insider trading, embezzlement ‚Äî generates proceeds already within the financial system. There is no placement stage because there is no cash to place. The funds are criminal property from the moment they land in the account.

2. Layering and integration can't always be separated

The line between these stages is often impossible to draw in practice. "Integration" itself may involve further layering. The process is rarely a neat linear sequence ‚Äî and money laundering can occur even where none of the three stages is clearly identifiable.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important

If you only look for the classic three-stage pattern, you will miss money laundering that doesn't fit the template. This is exactly what the ICA exam tests ‚Äî the ability to recognise laundering even when it looks nothing like the textbook model.

The Insider Trading Example

Mr Jones uses inside information to sell stock early, avoiding a loss. The proceeds sit in his bank account. No withdrawals or third-party payments are made. Is Mr Jones laundering money through the bank?
Yes. The proceeds of his insider trading crime sit in the account ‚Äî they are criminal property. There is no placement, no obvious layering, no integration. Yet money laundering is occurring, and the bank is technically part of a money laundering relationship.
üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The Critical Point
"It is not necessary to actively manage the entire laundering process, or any particular stage of it, in order to commit a money laundering offence. All that is required is a contribution to the process by dealing in some way with another person's benefit from crime, however innocent this activity was."

Section 4 of 7

Predicate Offences & Criminal Liability

This is the most examined distinction in the entire ICA course. The act of money laundering and the criminal offence of money laundering are not the same thing ‚Äî and confusing them costs marks.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Memorise This
"Criminal offences of money laundering are only committed when both the act of money laundering takes place AND the person or business that handles the property has the required state of mind, i.e., knows or suspects or has reasonable grounds to suspect, that the property derives from crime."
The key phrase: required state of mind. Without it, the act may be occurring ‚Äî but the person handling the funds may not have committed a criminal offence. Note: "reasonable grounds to suspect" is an objective test ‚Äî you can be liable even if you personally claim not to have suspected anything.

Five Scenarios ‚Äî Act vs Criminal Offence

All five answers are "Yes ‚Äî money laundering is occurring." The question for each is whether the professional has also committed a criminal offence.

1. A jeweller sells a luxury watch to a thief who uses proceeds from stolen goods. Is the jeweller involved in money laundering?
Yes ‚Äî the act of money laundering is occurring. The stolen goods have been converted into a watch. Whether the jeweller committed a criminal offence depends on whether they knew or suspected the money was stolen.
2. A drug dealer deposits ¬£200,000 across 20 accounts in ¬£10,000 batches. Are the banks laundering money?
Yes ‚Äî classic smurfing / placement. The banks are facilitating the placement even if individual cashiers suspect nothing. A bank with proper monitoring should detect the pattern. Criminal liability depends on what each bank knew or should have suspected.
3. An insider trader instructs his stockbroker to sell shares. Proceeds go to his bank. Did the stockbroker launder money? Did the bank?
Yes ‚Äî both. Insider trading proceeds are criminal property. The stockbroker executed the trade; the bank received the funds. Whether criminal offences were committed depends on each party's state of mind.
4. An employee defrauds her employer and uses the funds to buy an insurance contract. Did the bank launder money? What about the insurer?
Yes ‚Äî both. The fraud proceeds are criminal property. The bank processed them; the insurer accepted them as a premium. Criminal liability for each depends on their state of mind.
5. A corrupt politician places bribery proceeds into an offshore trust. Is the trustee laundering money?
Yes. Whether the trustee committed a criminal offence depends on what they knew, suspected, or should have suspected. The objective "reasonable grounds to suspect" test means the trustee may be liable even if they claim not to have known the source of funds.

Predicate Offences

Money laundering requires an underlying crime ‚Äî known as a predicate offence. These typically include drug trafficking, fraud, tax evasion, bribery, corruption, and many others. The list varies by jurisdiction.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Single vs Dual Criminality
Single criminality ‚Äî looks only at whether the conduct is criminal under the law of the country where laundering occurs, regardless of where the original crime took place.

Dual criminality ‚Äî requires the underlying conduct to be unlawful in both the country where the crime occurred AND the country where laundering takes place.

The SOCPA 2005 Defence (UK)

SOCPA introduced a limited defence where overseas conduct was lawful in that jurisdiction ‚Äî but only if the conduct would not be punishable in the UK by more than 12 months' imprisonment. This is the "bullfighter scenario": money earned legitimately from bullfighting abroad is not money laundering in the UK. However, for serious crimes like drug trafficking and tax evasion, this defence does not apply regardless of where the crime occurred.

‚ö†Ô∏è Your Personal Responsibility

Report any knowledge or suspicion of criminality to your MLRO. It is then the MLRO's responsibility ‚Äî not yours ‚Äî to decide whether to escalate to the authorities. Even if your report turns out to be nothing, it helps your firm manage risk and protects you personally.

What Forms of Property Can Be Laundered?

The name "money laundering" suggests it only involves cash. This is one of the most common and dangerous misconceptions in the field.

üìò ICA Exam Note
"There is a common misconception that the only form of property that can be laundered is cash. In fact, any form of property can be laundered."

Proceeds can take any form ‚Äî once criminal funds convert into any of the following, they become criminal property:

CashBank account fundsReal estate Luxury watches & jewelleryArt & antiquesMotor vehicles Precious metalsBranded goodsPrepaid store cards GoldVirtual currenciesInsurance policies

Why Real Estate Is Particularly High Risk

Property combines high transaction values, relative anonymity (especially through corporate structures), and the ability to generate legitimate-looking income through rents and resale. The UK property market ‚Äî particularly London ‚Äî has been identified by FATF and the UK's own National Risk Assessments as a significant vulnerability for international money laundering.

üè° Property Sector Example

A cash buyer uses an offshore company to purchase a London flat. The company's ultimate beneficial owner is unknown. Three years later, the flat is sold at a modest profit and proceeds wired to another jurisdiction. This is a complete laundering cycle using real estate ‚Äî and your firm may be involved at both ends without ever knowing the true source of funds.

Why Money Laundering Matters

The consequences of being caught up in money laundering ‚Äî even innocently ‚Äî are severe and increasingly personal. The numbers below are real.

The Scale of the Problem

A UNODC study estimates that money laundered globally each year represents between 2‚Äì5% of global GDP ‚Äî equivalent to $800 billion to $2 trillion. Illicit drugs account for roughly a fifth; human trafficking, fraud, bribery, and corruption make up the rest. The true figure is almost certainly higher ‚Äî by its nature, money laundering is hidden.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Four Forms of Damage
A firm found to have laundered criminal property faces: (i) criminal prosecution, (ii) regulatory sanction, (iii) reputational damage, and (iv) commercial damage through loss of business. Note: the first three also apply to individuals personally ‚Äî and you may lose your job.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Three Reasons to Avoid Involvement
i. It is morally and ethically wrong to facilitate the laundering of criminal property.
ii. Even without criminal liability, reputational damage can follow simply from being associated with a case.
iii. Regulated businesses have a legal obligation to take measures to protect against exposure to money laundering risk.
