Terrorist Financing vs Money Laundering

Terrorist financing and money laundering share many techniques ‚Äî but the flow of money works in fundamentally different directions. Understanding the distinction is a core ICA exam topic.

The ICA manual frames the comparison across three dimensions: the source of funds, the destination of funds, and the purpose of the activity.

Money Laundering
Terrorist Financing
Source of Funds
Always derived from criminal activity ‚Äî drug deals, fraud, tax evasion, bribery, human trafficking, etc.
May be criminal or entirely legitimate ‚Äî salary, charity donations, personal savings. The source does not have to be criminal.
Destination of Funds
Profits the criminal ‚Äî described as "circular" (though funds may also end up with innocent recipients who have no idea of their origin).
Sent to a terrorist or terrorist organisation ‚Äî described as "linear". The sender may not be a terrorist themselves.
Purpose of the Activity
To generate and enjoy profit from criminal activity.
To support terrorist operations, fund attacks, purchase weapons.
üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Money Laundering in Reverse
"Terrorist financing is often seen as money laundering in reverse. Terrorists collect money from perfectly legitimate sources ‚Äî clean money ‚Äî which is then subsequently used for criminal purposes."
This is the critical exam trap: because the source of terrorist funds can be legitimate, TF transactions can look indistinguishable from normal financial activity. You cannot rely on spotting "dirty money" ‚Äî you must consider what the funds are destined for.

What Terrorist Financing and Money Laundering Have in Common

Both require the assistance of the financial sector Both aim to disguise source or destination of funds Both use the same techniques to move money Both exploit legitimate businesses and structures
üí° Consider

Many techniques used to disguise the destination of terrorist funds are identical to those used to disguise the source of criminal proceeds. You must stay alert to the possibility that property you handle may at some future point be used to fund terrorism ‚Äî even if it arrived through apparently legitimate channels.

How Terrorist Financing Works

Terrorist organisations range from large, sophisticated networks to small cells operating on minimal budgets. Understanding how each raises and moves funds is essential to recognising the risk.

The Scale Varies Enormously

Large terrorist organisations like ISIS and Al-Qaeda require significant ongoing funding ‚Äî to pay fighters, acquire weapons, operate internationally. But many attacks are carried out for remarkably little money.

üìã Case Studies ‚Äî Low-Cost Attacks

November 2015 Paris Attacks: The French government indicated the bombings and mass shootings cost no more than $10,000 to execute.

2019 Sri Lanka Easter Attacks: Similarly, devastating attacks were carried out at minimal financial cost.

Other low-cost methods: Simply renting a vehicle or purchasing a knife to carry out attacks requires almost no financial infrastructure at all.

‚ö†Ô∏è Why This Makes Detection So Difficult

When a terrorist cell only needs $10,000, and that money may have come from legitimate employment income or small charitable donations, the transaction is almost impossible to distinguish from normal financial activity. There are no large suspicious cash deposits. No obvious red flags. This is why the financial sector must remain alert to behavioural patterns, not just transaction sizes.

How Terrorist Organisations Raise Funds

Charities and Front Organisations

A classic method ‚Äî a charity collects legitimate donations from well-meaning members of the public. Clean money is then channelled to a terrorist organisation. The donors may have no idea their money is funding terrorism.

Criminal Proceeds

Terrorist groups also raise funds through criminal activity ‚Äî drug trafficking, kidnapping for ransom, extortion, fraud. ISIS in particular generated significant revenue through oil sales, taxation of occupied territories, and looting.

Personal Funds and Employment Income

Individual terrorists may fund their own activities from personal savings or salary. Small, irregular transfers to overseas accounts or individuals may be the only financial footprint.

State Sponsorship

Some terrorist organisations receive funding from sympathetic state actors ‚Äî transferred through diplomatic channels or state-controlled institutions that are harder to monitor.

Methods Used to Move Terrorist Funds

Emerging Technologies

The 2008 Mumbai attacks used VOIP (Voice Over Internet Protocol), prepaid and value-stored cards, and pre-loaded mobile phones to coordinate the attack logistics and move funds. Terrorists in the Gaza Strip have used virtual currencies to fund operations. Electronic and online payments are a growing resource for terrorists.

Cash Smuggling

Several high-profile attacks ‚Äî including the Bali Bombings ‚Äî bypassed the financial system entirely by using cash couriers to physically transport funds across borders to terrorist cells.

Hawala and Informal Value Transfer

An ancient money transfer system operating outside the formal banking system. A sender gives cash to a hawala broker in one country; the recipient collects an equivalent sum from a broker in another country. No money physically crosses borders ‚Äî it is settled between brokers later. Transactions leave minimal records.

üè° Property Sector Relevance

A property transaction involving a buyer linked to a front charity, or funds originating from a jurisdiction known for terrorist activity, may require enhanced due diligence even if the source of funds appears clean. The destination ‚Äî not just the source ‚Äî must be considered as part of your risk assessment.

What Are Sanctions?

Sanctions are one of the primary tools used by governments and international bodies to combat money laundering, terrorist financing, and threats to national security. Understanding what they are and who they apply to is a core exam topic.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Definition
Sanctions are legal restrictions prohibiting dealings with designated individuals, entities, countries, or industry sectors. In most jurisdictions it is a regulatory requirement for financial institutions to comply with sanctions controls ‚Äî in addition to their AML and CFT obligations.

Who and What Can Be Sanctioned?

üåç Countries / Jurisdictions

Iran, North Korea, Syria, Russia ‚Äî broad sanctions prohibiting a wide range of activities with a long list of associated individuals and entities

üë§ Individuals

Politicians, drug traffickers, terrorists, and those who assist them ‚Äî collectively known as Specially Designated Nationals (SDNs)

üè≠ Industry Sectors

E.g. Sectoral Sanctions Indicators (SSI) lists targeting Russian oil & gas, deep-sea drilling, and banking sectors

üè¢ Entities

Companies owned or controlled by sanctioned individuals or governments ‚Äî e.g. Commercial Bank of Syria

üö¢ Named Ships / Aircraft

Vessels known to be involved in financial crime, terrorism, or owned by a sanctioned government

üì¶ Trading Activities

Importing/exporting to sanctioned countries; trading in arms, diamonds, prohibited cultural property, agricultural products

‚ö†Ô∏è Why Sanctions Matter for Your Firm

A firm must not provide economic benefits or conduct impermissible business connected to sanctioned individuals, entities, or countries. This requires a sanctions compliance programme proportionate to the scale of your business ‚Äî including screening, detection, training, risk assessment, and escalation procedures.

The Four Key Sanctions Regimes

There are four main international sanctions regimes your firm must be familiar with. Each has different coverage, legal force, and ‚Äî critically ‚Äî different extraterritorial reach.

üá∫üá≥
Widest Reach

United Nations (UN)

Sanctions imposed via UN Security Council Resolutions (UNSCRs). Member states are required to implement them ‚Äî though they are not always obliged to do so, and implementation can vary significantly between countries.

The UN publishes names of individuals and organisations subject to financial sanctions specifically relating to ISIS (Da'esh), Al-Qaeda, and the Taliban. All UN member states must freeze the funds and assets of named parties and report suspected matches.

üá™üá∫
All EU States

European Union (EU)

EU sanctions are issued under the Common Foreign and Security Policy (CFSP). Unlike UN sanctions, EU sanctions apply automatically to all EU member states and EU citizens ‚Äî there is no opt-out.

The EU also issues its own autonomous sanctions beyond what the UN requires ‚Äî for example, against Syria, Russia's energy and banking sectors, and for human rights violations and cyberattacks.

üá∫üá∏
Extraterritorial

OFAC (United States)

The Office of Foreign Assets Control administers US sanctions targeting foreign countries, terrorists, narcotics traffickers, WMD proliferators, and other threats to US national security.

Critical point for non-US firms: While OFAC doesn't directly apply to non-US firms outside the US, using US dollars automatically engages OFAC regulations ‚Äî all USD transactions clear through New York, giving the US jurisdiction. OFAC publishes the SDN (Specially Designated Nationals) and SDGT (Specially Designated Global Terrorists) lists.

CAATSA (2017) added sweeping sanctions against Iran, North Korea, and Russia. Breaches carry monetary fines, imprisonment, and loss of US market access.

üá¨üáß
Post-Brexit

UK / OFSI

After Brexit (31 December 2020), the UK established its own independent sanctions regime. HM Treasury's Office of Financial Sanctions Implementation (OFSI) ‚Äî established in 2016 ‚Äî now administers UK financial sanctions independently of the EU.

The UK Policing and Crime Act 2017 gives OFSI significant powers including monetary penalties and a maximum custodial sentence of seven years for breaching financial sanctions. The FCA retains separate powers to fine regulated firms for inadequate sanctions systems and controls.

HM Treasury may add names to the UK list that don't appear on UN or EU lists ‚Äî for example, under the Terrorism Orders relating to Al-Qaeda and the Taliban.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The USD Trigger
"While US sanctions do not directly apply to non-US firms outside the US, the use of the US dollar currency automatically engages OFAC regulations. US prosecutors take the view that if a financial transaction has cleared in the US (as all US dollar payments do, in New York City), the US has jurisdiction over the offence."
This is frequently tested. A UK property firm accepting a USD payment from an overseas buyer could theoretically fall within OFAC's jurisdiction ‚Äî even if it has no US operations whatsoever.

Country Restrictions

Several countries are subject to extensive sanctions regimes. Understanding the context behind each helps you apply them correctly ‚Äî and recognise when a client's connections may require enhanced scrutiny.

üáÆüá∑ Iran

Sanctions in place since 1995 from UN, EU, US, and local regimes. Some restrictions were lifted in 2016 as part of the nuclear deal (JCPOA). However, the US re-imposed extensive sanctions in November 2018 ‚Äî the largest ever single-day re-imposition, targeting over 700 individuals, entities, aircraft, and vessels. Many activities remain prohibited, including financial assistance for military goods. The situation continues to evolve.

üá∞üáµ North Korea

In September 2017, the UN imposed its harshest ever sanctions following North Korea's sixth nuclear test. Objectives include capping oil imports, banning textile exports, ending overseas labour contracts, suppressing smuggling, stopping joint ventures, and sanctioning government entities. Diplomatic attempts at resolution have so far failed.

üá∑üá∫ Russia

Following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, sweeping sanctions were imposed by the US, EU, UK, Canada, Japan, Australia, Switzerland, and New Zealand. Russia's FATF membership was suspended in February 2023. The sanctions landscape is dynamic ‚Äî firms must stay current with guidance from relevant sanctions bodies. FATF currently counts Russia as suspended, not expelled.

üåç Other Jurisdictions

Sanctions also apply to Syria, Sudan, Libya, Cuba, Myanmar, and others. The EU Syria sanctions include an oil embargo, asset freezes of the Syrian central bank, export restrictions on repression technology, and travel bans. Sanctions lists and restrictions change regularly ‚Äî always refer to your firm's sanctions policy and the relevant regulatory body for current requirements.

üí° Consider ‚Äî Dynamic Landscape

A challenging aspect of sanctions compliance is that lists and restrictions change frequently. A client may not be on a sanctions list when you onboard them ‚Äî but may be added later. Your firm's screening must account for ongoing changes, not just a one-time check at the start of the relationship.

üè° Property Sector Example

A buyer of a UK property has a shareholding in a Russian oil company that falls under EU sectoral sanctions. Even if the buyer is not personally named on any list, your firm may be facilitating a sanctions breach by proceeding without enhanced scrutiny. Beneficial ownership checks are essential ‚Äî sanctions can apply to entities controlled by a sanctioned party, not just the named individuals themselves.

Sanctions Screening

Knowing the sanctions regimes is one thing ‚Äî being able to screen against them effectively is another. The ICA sets out clear requirements for how screening must be conducted.

Types of Sanctions Lists

Public Domain Lists

Available to all ‚Äî OFAC's SDN list, UN lists, EU lists, HM Treasury (OFSI). Your firm must screen against these as a minimum.

Local Jurisdiction Lists

Specific to individual countries. Some jurisdictions maintain their own lists that may include names not appearing on international lists.

Non-Public Global Lists

Global lists that are not available in the public domain ‚Äî shared within specific law enforcement or intelligence networks.

Internal Firm Lists

Private lists maintained within individual firms, based on customer and transactional intelligence gathered internally. These supplement the public lists.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Ongoing Obligation
"Names should be screened at the outset of a relationship, and the whole database of existing customers should be screened frequently thereafter to account for changes to sanctions lists. A customer may not be on a sanctions list when a relationship is established, but they may be added later."
A one-time check at onboarding is not sufficient. Screening must be continuous throughout the relationship.

What Must Be Screened?

‚ö†Ô∏è Scope of Screening

A robust sanctions programme must screen: all employees, all vendors, all third parties, all payment activity, all prospective clients, all beneficial owners, and all counterparties to transactions ‚Äî including identifying the nature of goods and, where relevant, screening ships. Sanctions controls must be in place at all stages of the client lifecycle.

Automated vs Manual Screening

There is no legal requirement to screen automatically ‚Äî but automated screening is industry best practice where high transaction volumes are involved (e.g. retail banking). Automated systems filter data ‚Äî names, dates of birth, addresses ‚Äî against sanctions lists to detect potential matches.

Handling Matches ‚Äî True vs False Positives

Not every potential match is a genuine sanctions hit. Firms must have a clear investigation process. The ICA identifies three types of true match:

i. True matches to restricted countries ii. True matches with payments rejected iii. Other true matches ‚Äî e.g. SDNs
‚ö†Ô∏è True Match Response

A clear process must be in place to respond to confirmed sanctions hits. This may include freezing the transaction, reporting to external authorities (OFSI in the UK), and considering whether a SAR should be filed. Failing to act on a confirmed hit is itself a sanctions breach.

Quality Assurance

All screening processes ‚Äî manual and automated ‚Äî must be subject to regular quality assurance checks to ensure investigations are completed in a timely manner, list updates are applied promptly, and the programme remains effective. Screening without proper QA is insufficient for regulatory purposes.
