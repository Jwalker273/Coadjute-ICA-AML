How Terrorist Financing Works

Terrorist organisations range from large, sophisticated networks to small cells operating on minimal budgets. Understanding how each raises and moves funds is essential to recognising the risk in property transactions.

Large terrorist organisations like ISIS and Al-Qaeda require significant ongoing funding â€” to pay fighters, acquire weapons, and operate internationally. But smaller cells and lone actors can cause devastating harm with very little money.

The Four Key Sanctions Regimes

There are four main international sanctions regimes your firm must be familiar with. Each has different coverage, legal force, and â€” critically â€” different extraterritorial reach.

Widest Reach

United Nations (UN)

Imposed via UN Security Council Resolutions (UNSCRs). Member states are required to implement them, though compliance and implementation can vary significantly between countries.

The UN publishes names of individuals and organisations subject to financial sanctions specifically relating to ISIS (Da'esh), Al-Qaeda, and the Taliban. The Taliban Sanctions Committee can remove individuals who renounce violence, sever links to international terrorist organisations, and respect the Afghan constitution.

Post-Brexit Separate

European Union (EU)

EU sanctions are implemented via EU Regulations, which have direct legal effect in all member states. Post-Brexit, the UK maintains its own separate sanctions regime â€” but UK firms operating in EU markets still need to check EU lists as well.

The EU's asset-freeze list includes designated terrorists, state actors, and human rights violators. EU sanctions bind all EU member state firms and can affect non-EU firms operating in EUR or with EU counterparties.

United States â€” OFAC

The Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) administers US sanctions. OFAC has the most aggressive extraterritorial reach of any sanctions authority â€” secondary sanctions can apply to non-US firms with no US connection at all.

Why it matters even for UK firms: Any transaction denominated in USD clears through the US banking system. This gives OFAC jurisdiction. Using USD with a sanctioned party could expose a UK firm â€” like Coadjute â€” to OFAC enforcement, fines, and loss of USD banking access.

United Kingdom â€” OFSI

Since Brexit, UK sanctions are implemented under the Sanctions and Anti-Money Laundering Act 2018 (SAMLA). The Office of Financial Sanctions Implementation (OFSI) within HM Treasury administers and enforces UK financial sanctions.

OFSI can impose civil monetary penalties of up to Â£1 million or 50% of the estimated value of the breach (whichever is higher) for non-criminal breaches. Criminal penalties are higher. Building a Sanctions Compliance Programme

Knowing that sanctions exist is not enough. Firms must have active, documented processes to screen against them and respond when a match is found. This section covers what a compliant programme looks like in practice.

1. Screening

All parties to a transaction â€” buyers, sellers, beneficial owners, directors, intermediaries, and connected entities â€” must be screened against all relevant sanctions lists before and throughout the transaction. All relevant staff must receive regular sanctions training â€” including what sanctions are, how to use the screening system, how to escalate a match, and what not to do (for example: do not tell the client you have screened them or that a match has been found â€” this could constitute tipping off).

Everyone must know what to do when a potential match is found. Escalation procedures should be documented, tested, and reviewed. They should specify: who to contact, how quickly, what information to provide, and what actions may or may not be taken pending investigation.
