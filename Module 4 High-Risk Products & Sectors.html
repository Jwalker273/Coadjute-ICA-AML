Insurance Products

Life insurance and investment-linked policies offer criminals a seemingly legitimate way to break the money trail and generate clean funds. Understanding how this works is essential for anyone in financial services.

Why Insurance is Attractive to Money Launderers

Insurance policies ‚Äî particularly life insurance with an investment element ‚Äî are vulnerable because they allow criminals to do something simple but powerful: place dirty money in, and receive a clean payment back out. The fact that the refund or payout comes from a reputable insurance company gives it an air of legitimacy that a direct bank transfer could never achieve.

There are two key appeals. First, the money trail is effectively broken ‚Äî between placement (paying premiums) and the eventual payout (surrender or claim), the funds look like the proceeds of a legitimate insurance arrangement. Second, it provides cover: a criminal can genuinely point to an insurance company as the source of their funds.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Fundamental Mechanism

Criminal funds ‚Üí paid as insurance premiums ‚Üí policy surrendered or claimed ‚Üí insurer pays out "clean" money. The criminal accepts losing a small percentage (early surrender fees, etc.) as the cost of laundering. This is simply the price of doing business.

The Core Insurance Laundering Technique

The ICA manual describes a classic example of how this works in practice:

üìã Case Study ‚Äî Art & Yacht Fraud

The Offshore Company Premium Scheme

A fraudster wire-transfers proceeds from a fraud into an offshore company's account. The company then pays a single large premium to insure a high-value asset ‚Äî a work of art or a yacht.

Six months later, the assets are said to have been sold and the insurance policy is cancelled. The insurance company refunds the majority of the "unused" premium. The company can now demonstrate a legitimate source for those funds ‚Äî a refund from an insurer.

The small percentage lost to surrender charges is simply the cost of laundering ‚Äî far cheaper than the alternative of having funds that can be traced directly to crime.

The Many Ways Insurance Products Are Exploited

The ICA identifies several specific techniques beyond the basic model:

1. Single-Premium Return Policies

A criminal purchases a large, single-premium policy, then quickly redeems it. Even with high early redemption fees, they receive a cheque from a reputable insurer. The source of funds now appears to be the insurance company rather than the crime.

2. Second-Hand Endowments (Life Policy Secondary Market)

Criminals purchase second-hand life insurance policies using laundered funds. The policy pays out when the original life assured (someone completely unconnected to the criminal) dies. At that point, the criminal receives a legitimate insurance company cheque, with nothing to connect it to the original criminal funds.

3. Overpayment of Premiums

A relatively simple technique: deliberately overpay premiums, then wait for the insurer to refund the excess by cheque. The cheque appears to be from a legitimate source ‚Äî the insurer ‚Äî regardless of how the premiums were originally funded.

4. Assignment of Claims

The criminal arranges with legitimate business owners to have genuine insurance claims paid to the criminal instead of the policy holder. The criminal pays the legitimate holder their claim value in cash (plus a premium for the arrangement), while receiving a clean insurance company cheque for themselves.

5. Bogus Claims (Arson and Asset Destruction)

A criminal purchases a legitimate business, then arranges for an insurance event ‚Äî through arson or other means ‚Äî to generate a claim. Crucially, money launderers using this method typically seek only to recover their premium investment (not inflated amounts that might attract suspicion), making them harder to spot than outright insurance fraudsters.

6. Life Insurance as Loan Collateral

Life policies with value can be used as collateral for a mortgage or loan. The loan proceeds appear clean (they come from a bank), while the underlying collateral is funded by criminal premiums. The ICA manual provides a detailed example:

üìã Case Study ‚Äî The Protected Species Trader

C: Trader in Illegal Wildlife Products

C transfers criminal profits to an offshore bank account held by a company owned by a blind trust. The money funds premiums on two life insurance policies. Those policies are then used as collateral for a mortgage on a property, which is rented to a third party. The rental income ‚Äî appearing perfectly legitimate ‚Äî funds C's lifestyle.

Result: criminal money ‚Üí insurance premiums ‚Üí loan collateral ‚Üí mortgage ‚Üí rental income ‚Üí clean lifestyle. At every step, the source looks legitimate.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Insurance Red Flags

The ICA identifies the following as key red flags in insurance products. Memorise these ‚Äî they are commonly tested:

  • Early surrender of a policy, particularly where the client accepts a significant financial penalty
  • Payment of premiums from third-party sources (who is really funding this?)
  • Unexplained change in premium payment method
  • Reluctance to provide source of funds for large single-premium policies
  • Single large one-off premium payments, especially to investment-linked products
  • Overpayment of premiums
  • Requests to redirect payouts to third-party accounts
üè† Coadjute / Property Sector Link

Insurance may seem like a separate sector from property ‚Äî but the two intersect. Consider a criminal who uses an insurance policy as collateral for a mortgage to purchase a property. Or one who inflates a claim on a property following arson in order to receive a "clean" payout. Your team may encounter clients referencing insurance payouts as a source of funds ‚Äî this warrants careful source-of-funds questioning.

‚úã Quick Check
A new client wants to purchase a property using funds from an early surrender of a life insurance policy. The client says the policy was with a major UK insurer. Should this immediately satisfy your source of funds concerns?

Correct answer: early surrender is explicitly listed as an insurance red flag. The original source of the premiums matters just as much as the identity of the insurer paying out.

Investment Products & Services

A common misconception is that investment products are not attractive to money launderers because "the money needs to keep moving." This is wrong ‚Äî and the ICA is explicit about it.

The Myth About Investment Products

Many people in the investment industry hold two incorrect beliefs:

  • Myth 1: Criminals won't use investment funds because money needs to keep moving quickly.
  • Myth 2: If a fund's rules require liquidated proceeds to be returned to the originating account, it can't be used for laundering.
‚ö†Ô∏è ICA Critical Point ‚Äî Both Myths Are Wrong

The ICA is direct on this: "Parking criminally derived funds in an investment fund where the money can accumulate steadily does provide a layer of concealment." Even if the money eventually goes back to the same account, it has been in a legitimate investment vehicle ‚Äî and this change of format confuses the money trail.

Also, a commercial driver complicates this further: relationship managers may allow commercial considerations to override their AML obligations. The desire to win and retain business can make people less willing to scrutinise whether they're helping a client launder proceeds.

How Investment Products Are Used for Laundering

The "Change of Format" Layering Technique

Once criminal money enters the system and purchases units in an investment fund, those funds have been converted from cash or bank balances into something different ‚Äî units, shares, or interests in a fund. When eventually sold, the proceeds look like investment returns rather than criminal funds. This change of format is a classic layering technique.

Strict vs. Flexible Remittance Rules

Some investment funds require that any proceeds be paid back only to the account from which the original investment came. These are more secure. However:

  • Funds with relaxed rules allowing payments to third parties or third-party accounts are significantly more vulnerable.
  • Even "strict remittance rule" funds don't eliminate risk ‚Äî the criminal's account receiving the proceeds may itself be a step removed from the original crime.

Long-Term "Parking"

Criminals are not always in a hurry. Placing funds in a long-term investment product ‚Äî a pension wrapper, a bonds portfolio, an accumulation fund ‚Äî allows those funds to sit legitimately for years, accumulating notional returns, before eventually being extracted as what appear to be long-term savings or investment proceeds.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Investment Red Flags
  • Requests to redirect investment proceeds to third-party accounts not held by the investor
  • Unusual levels of interest in the AML controls of the firm (may indicate testing them)
  • Large single lump-sum investments with vague or no explanation of source of wealth
  • Requests to structure investments to avoid reporting thresholds
  • Early redemption requests shortly after investment, accepting penalties
  • Investment decisions inconsistent with the client's apparent financial profile
‚úã Quick Check
An investment manager's firm operates a fund with strict rules requiring all proceeds to be returned to the originating account. A compliance officer says this means the fund "cannot be used for money laundering." Is this correct?
‚ÑπÔ∏è Why This Matters for Coadjute

In property transactions, you will often encounter clients stating their source of funds as "investment proceeds" or "ISA withdrawal." These are legitimate for most clients ‚Äî but your AML obligation is to understand the underlying source. Where did the money in the ISA or investment fund originally come from? Source of funds questions must go back to the original source, not just the most recent account.

Betting, Gaming & Casinos

The gambling sector offers launderers a powerful cover story: "I won it." The ability to convert cash into chips, gamble minimally, and cash out creates a credible-seeming legitimate source for criminal funds.

Why Gambling Is a High-Risk Sector

The FATF highlighted the vulnerability of casinos and the gaming sector in a 2009 paper ‚Äî and while dated, subsequent enforcement actions and enquiries have only confirmed the risks. The 2022 Cullen Commission in Canada ‚Äî set up after millions of dollars were laundered through British Columbia casinos ‚Äî brought 101 recommendations, demonstrating just how real and large-scale the problem is.

üåç Real World ‚Äî The Cullen Commission (Canada, 2022)

British Columbia Casino Money Laundering Scandal

The Commission of Inquiry into Money Laundering in British Columbia was launched in 2019 and reported in 2022. It examined the laundering of millions of dollars through casinos, real estate, and luxury cars. Criminals would bring bags of cash to casinos, purchase chips, do minimal gambling, and cash out ‚Äî receiving casino cheques as their "winnings."

This example is significant for Coadjute: the same laundered funds then moved into property purchases ‚Äî demonstrating the direct link between casino laundering and real estate vulnerabilities.

The Basic Casino Laundering Technique

The technique is disarmingly simple:

  1. Criminal arrives at casino with criminal cash.
  2. Cash is exchanged for chips.
  3. Minimal gambling occurs (sometimes deliberately losing a small amount to add credibility).
  4. Remaining chips are cashed out ‚Äî and the criminal receives a casino payment (often a cheque or bank transfer).
  5. The funds now appear to be gambling winnings from a legitimate casino.
üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Casino CDD Threshold (UK)

Under the UK Money Laundering Regulations 2017, casino operators must apply CDD measures for any transaction exceeding ‚Ç¨2,000. This applies:

  • Regardless of whether a business relationship exists
  • To single transactions and multiple transactions that appear to be linked
  • To non-remote casinos (token threshold), gaming machines (‚Ç¨2,000+), and remote casinos (deposit/withdrawal threshold of ‚Ç¨2,000+)

Casino operators may take either a "verify all on entry" approach or the "threshold approach" (verify when the ‚Ç¨2,000 threshold is reached).

AML Requirements in the Gaming Sector

In the UK, the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 apply to casinos (remote and non-remote) licensed by the Gambling Commission. Key requirements include:

  • A risk-sensitive approach to CDD
  • Risk assessment based on business and customer profile
  • Remote casinos must evaluate their specific risks (customers not seen face-to-face)
  • Relevant employees (not catering/bar staff) must receive AML training
  • Appointment of a "nominated officer" to review SARs ‚Äî in the UK this person must hold a personal management licence from the Gambling Commission
  • Records kept for five years from end of business relationship

The Risk-Based Approach in Gaming

FATF guidance emphasises that casinos should assess and manage their risks, not simply prohibit entire categories of business. The risk-based approach in gaming covers three areas: CDD; business internal control systems; and ongoing oversight and monitoring.

Customer Risk

The ICA identifies the following as higher-risk customer categories in the gaming sector:

  • Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs)
  • High spenders
  • Disproportionate spenders (spending inconsistent with personal financial profile)
  • Casual or one-off customers (no ongoing relationship ‚Äî harder to track)
  • Improper use of third parties
  • Junket operators (who bring groups of high-spending players, often from overseas)
  • Customers with multiple casino accounts or player-rating accounts

Transaction Risk

Higher-risk transactions include:

  • Cash transactions (especially large amounts)
  • Transfers between customers (passing funds between accounts)
  • Loan sharking at casinos (lending money to gamblers at inflated rates)
  • Use of casino deposit accounts
  • Redemption of chips using different payment methods than deposit (non-closed loop)
  • Online: multiple casino accounts or "casino wallets"

Red Flags and Suspicious Activity in Casinos

The ICA does not prescribe a definitive list (since that could help criminals evade detection), but the following are recognised red flag indicators:

Red FlagWhy It's Suspicious

Intentional loss of a gameDeliberately losing to transfer funds to another player disguises the movement

üìã ICA Case Study ‚Äî Multiple Identities (Online Casino)

Customer A: ¬£5M+ Laundered Across Four Accounts

Customer A became a UK customer as "Fred Smith." Two years later, same surname but different address ‚Äî and deposited ¬£5,000,000 across both accounts. Two years after that, a European account opened under a different first name with ‚Ç¨1,000,000. A fourth account, same surname again, UK address again.

Total funds: over ¬£6.5 million across four accounts, over several years. The multiple identities, addresses, and jurisdictions were designed to prevent any single account triggering a SAR ‚Äî classic structuring applied to the casino context.

‚úã Quick Check
A customer deposits ¬£400,000 into their online casino account. Over three months, they bet occasionally but withdraw ¬£395,000 ‚Äî almost the entire balance. Is this suspicious?
üè† Coadjute / Property Sector Link

The Cullen Commission showed directly that casino proceeds flowed into property purchases. When clients state gambling winnings as a source of funds for a property transaction, this demands enhanced scrutiny. Casino winnings should be verifiable ‚Äî the casino will have records of payouts. Vague references to "a good run at the casino" without documentary evidence should not be accepted as source of funds.

Mobile Financial Services & Prepaid Cards

Mobile financial services have revolutionised access to banking in the developing world ‚Äî but have also created new channels for money laundering, especially where regulatory oversight is weak and agent networks are large.

What Are Mobile Financial Services?

The term "mobile financial services" (m-FS) covers a wide range of activity. The ICA identifies four main types:

  1. m-Info ‚Äî viewing financial information (e.g. checking a bank balance via an app)
  2. m-Banking ‚Äî conducting bank transactions remotely (e.g. mobile banking with a traditional bank)
  3. m-Money ‚Äî using mobile phones to store value (the main AML risk area)
  4. m-Payments ‚Äî using mobile phones to make payments

Types 1 and 2 are essentially existing banking products accessed via mobile ‚Äî the AML risk comes from the underlying product. The ICA focuses its concern on Types 3 and 4, particularly m-Money, which has grown fastest in developing economies where large portions of the population have no access to traditional banking.

‚ÑπÔ∏è Global Scale

The World Bank estimates nearly three billion people worldwide have no access to traditional bank accounts. Services like M-Pesa (Kenya), MTN Mobile Money (Africa), G-Cash (Philippines), and others have signed up tens of millions of users ‚Äî M-Pesa alone had 25 million users in Kenya by 2020, with over 430,000 agents across multiple countries.

How m-Money Works

A customer registers with an authorised agent (a retailer or outlet), receiving an electronic money account linked to their mobile number. They can load funds by paying an agent in cash, transfer funds to other account holders, pay bills, make retail purchases, and withdraw cash by visiting an agent.

Critically, the electronic money accounts are typically backed 100% by a pooled bank account held by the Mobile Network Operator (MNO) with a regulated bank ‚Äî meaning the end liability is real money, not just digital tokens.

The Four Risk Categories in m-FS

1. Geographic Risk

m-FS has grown fastest in jurisdictions that often have the weakest AML controls. The ICA identifies geographic risk factors as:

  • Countries with weak or deficient AML regimes
  • Countries subject to international sanctions or embargoes
  • Countries with significant corruption, drug trafficking, human trafficking, or smuggling
  • Countries identified as providing support to terrorist organisations

2. Customer Risk

The m-FS customer base presents unique challenges: many customers are low-income, may lack national identity documents, and operate primarily in cash. Risk factors include:

  • Difficulty obtaining standard CDD documentation from customers without formal ID
  • Customers opening multiple accounts to bypass low transaction limits (smurfing via m-FS)
  • Use by unregistered consumers (withdrawing via third parties)

3. Agent Network Risk

This is the ICA's most detailed risk category for m-FS. Agents are the direct point of contact with customers ‚Äî and they are often small-scale retailers, geographically remote, with high staff turnover and limited AML training.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Agent Problem

The ICA notes that agents from non-financial services backgrounds may have little exposure to AML compliance. In some developing countries, cultural norms ‚Äî such as close kinship ties within communities ‚Äî may actively discourage AML reporting. An agent may be reluctant to submit a SAR on a family member or community elder.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Agent Red Flags
  • Agents conducting unusually high numbers of transactions (especially in high-risk geographic areas)
  • Agents with disproportionately high transaction values
  • Agents with poor CDD collection standards
  • Agents with seasonal fluctuations inconsistent with other agents (may indicate drug trafficking links)
  • Agents established as fronts for criminal activity
  • Agents not complying with internal AML procedures

4. Product Risk

Product features that increase ML/TF risk include:

  • Products favouring anonymity ‚Äî minimal or no CDD at account opening
  • Products enabling withdrawals by unregistered consumers
  • Very high or unlimited transaction values
  • Functionality beyond domestic money transfers (international capability)
  • Ability for customers to open multiple accounts
  • Cross-border fund movement capabilities
  • Services operated in partnership between an MNO and a bank ‚Äî creating ambiguity about who is responsible for AML

Prepaid Cards: Stored Value, Anonymous Risk

Prepaid cards (also called "stored value cards") are a related risk. They allow users to load cash onto a card, which can then be used like a debit card ‚Äî spending, withdrawals, or transfers ‚Äî without requiring a traditional bank account.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Prepaid Card AML Risk

The primary risk: criminal cash can be loaded onto a prepaid card without triggering bank-level AML scrutiny. The card then provides an apparently legitimate payment method. Key risks:

  • Anonymous loading with cash ‚Äî no CDD required for small amounts
  • Multiple cards can be loaded and aggregated (structuring)
  • Cards can be transferred between individuals, effectively transferring value anonymously
  • International usability creates cross-border layering opportunities
  • Funds on the card are difficult to trace back to the original cash source

Mitigating factor: lower transaction limits on many prepaid card products restrict the scale of potential abuse.

The Key Mitigating Factor: The Digital Trail

Despite the risks, m-FS has one significant advantage over physical cash: it leaves a digital record. Every m-Money transaction is recorded ‚Äî who sent what to whom, when, from which location. This makes m-FS more traceable than physical cash, and regulators can (with appropriate access) reconstruct transaction histories. This is a genuine mitigating factor that the ICA recognises.

‚úÖ Countering m-FS Risks

The ICA identifies the following as firm-level controls: agent due diligence (including ongoing monitoring of agent behaviour), CDD at account opening, watch lists and sanctions screening, staff training, automated transaction monitoring, and compliance monitoring programmes.

Virtual Currencies

The growth of cryptocurrency and virtual currencies has created both legitimate innovation and significant AML vulnerabilities. The ICA dedicates substantial attention to this area ‚Äî and it's a live issue in property transactions.

What Are Virtual Currencies?

The terminology has evolved. "Bitcoin" was originally used as shorthand for all cryptocurrencies, but "virtual currencies" (VCs) is now the preferred term, encompassing all types. Examples include:

  • Convertible/bilateral VCs ‚Äî can be bought and sold for fiat money: Bitcoin, Ethereum, Litecoin, and thousands of others
  • Unidirectional VCs ‚Äî flow in only one direction; cannot easily be converted back: loyalty scheme points, Amazon Coins
  • Corporate VCs ‚Äî issued by major companies (Facebook's proposed Libra, now abandoned, was an example)
  • Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs) ‚Äî nation-state issued VCs; emerging globally but the sector remains mostly unregulated

There are two main ways to obtain virtual currencies: purchase them with "real" (fiat) money, or earn them through trading, promotions, or surveys.

Why VCs Are Attractive to Criminals

The core appeal is pseudo-anonymity. On most public blockchains, transactions are recorded and publicly visible ‚Äî but they are linked to wallet addresses, not real-world identities. Tracing a specific wallet address back to an individual requires either technical analysis or cooperation from an exchange that may have conducted KYC on the account.

‚ö†Ô∏è ICA Position on Virtual Currency Risk

"Due to its reputation for anonymity and being untraceable, a virtual currency can be used for criminal activities such as laundering money, buying and selling illegal goods and services, and transferring money to support criminal or terror activities."

The practice of using VCs to transfer illegal funds has grown substantially and is well documented ‚Äî both for ML and TF.

How Virtual Currencies Facilitate Money Laundering

Step 1: Conversion to Crypto (Placement)

Criminal cash is exchanged for virtual currency, often through a cryptocurrency exchange, peer-to-peer (P2P) trading platform, or Bitcoin ATM. Some exchanges have weak or no KYC ‚Äî making this the most vulnerable entry point.

Step 2: Layering via Crypto

Once in the crypto ecosystem, layering techniques include:

  • Chain hopping ‚Äî converting between different cryptocurrencies multiple times (e.g. Bitcoin ‚Üí Ethereum ‚Üí Monero) to confuse the trail
  • Mixers / Tumblers ‚Äî services that blend multiple users' transactions together, making it extremely difficult to trace the original source of any specific coins
  • Privacy coins ‚Äî currencies like Monero or Zcash designed with enhanced anonymity features that make tracing virtually impossible
  • Decentralised exchanges (DEXs) ‚Äî peer-to-peer trading without a central operator who might conduct KYC
  • Multiple wallets ‚Äî moving funds through dozens or hundreds of wallets before exit

Step 3: Back to Fiat (Integration)

Eventually, the criminal wants clean spendable money. This may involve:

  • Converting back to fiat through a compliant exchange (which may trigger KYC, so layering has been done first)
  • Spending directly on high-value assets ‚Äî real estate, luxury goods, art ‚Äî from sellers who accept cryptocurrency
  • Using crypto debit cards that convert at point of sale
  • Peer-to-peer cash sales (crypto sold for cash directly to individuals)
üî¥ Blockchain Immutability ‚Äî The Double Edge

Once a transaction is confirmed on a blockchain, it cannot be reversed or deleted. This is both a risk and an opportunity. For investigators: the transaction record is permanent and public ‚Äî forensic blockchain analysis firms (Chainalysis, Elliptic) can often trace funds across wallets. For criminals: once at integration stage with appropriately layered crypto, tracing becomes extremely challenging without exchange cooperation.

Case Study: Liberty Reserve

üìã ICA Case Study ‚Äî Liberty Reserve (Shut down 2013)

The Black-Market Bank

Liberty Reserve, incorporated in Costa Rica in 2006, was shut down by US authorities in June 2013. It operated as, in the words of prosecutors, "essentially a black-market bank," and is reported to have masked more than $6 billion in criminal proceeds across millions of users.

The platform allowed users to open accounts with minimal information and transfer "LR" currency anonymously. Drug traffickers, fraudsters, hackers, and child exploitation networks all used it. The case remains the definitive study in how unregulated virtual currency platforms enable large-scale, global money laundering.

Silk Road: Crypto and Criminal Marketplaces

Bitcoin attracted major adverse media attention following its association with Silk Road, an online marketplace for drugs and other criminal activity. Shut down by the FBI in 2013, Silk Road demonstrated that crypto could serve as the payment infrastructure for entire criminal economies operating openly online. Bitcoin's pseudo-anonymity was key to Silk Road's operation ‚Äî and its seizure led to billions in confiscated Bitcoin.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Virtual Currency Red Flags
  • Use of cryptocurrency mixing/tumbling services
  • Multiple wallet addresses used in rapid succession
  • Conversion between multiple cryptocurrencies (chain hopping)
  • Use of privacy coins (Monero, Zcash) where tracing is near-impossible
  • Use of peer-to-peer exchanges without KYC requirements
  • Unexplained large cryptocurrency holdings claimed as source of funds
  • Crypto-funded property purchases without clear exchange provenance

Key point: the sector is mostly unregulated globally. In the UK, crypto asset businesses must register with the FCA for AML purposes (not as authorised firms) ‚Äî but international enforcement is patchy.

üè† Coadjute / Property Sector Link ‚Äî Crypto in Property

Crypto-funded property purchases are a live and growing AML risk. Where a client states they wish to fund a property purchase (partly or wholly) using cryptocurrency, you must establish:

  • Which exchange was used to acquire the crypto (regulated, KYC-compliant?)
  • The original fiat source of the crypto purchase
  • Whether mixing services or privacy coins have been used (often untraceable)
  • Provenance documentation from the exchange (transaction records, KYC confirmation)

"I have Bitcoin" is not a source of funds ‚Äî it's a current asset form. You need to go back further: where did the money come from to buy the Bitcoin in the first place?

Red Flags & Sector Summary

Before the quiz, let's consolidate the key red flags across all the sectors covered in this module ‚Äî and consider how they intersect with your work at Coadjute.

The Common Thread: Why These Sectors Are Targeted

Across insurance, investment products, gaming, mobile financial services, and virtual currencies, a common pattern emerges. Criminals seek sectors where:

  • Cash can enter without triggering automatic suspicion
  • Value can be converted into a different format that obscures its origins

But each requires you to go one step further: what was the original source of the funds that went into those products?
