Insurance Products

Life insurance and investment-linked policies give criminals a powerful tool: place dirty money in as premiums, receive a clean payout from a reputable insurer. Understanding the mechanism ‚Äî and the red flags ‚Äî is essential for anyone in financial services.

Why Insurance Attracts Money Launderers

Insurance policies ‚Äî particularly life insurance with an investment element ‚Äî are vulnerable because they allow criminals to do something simple but powerful: place criminal funds in and receive a clean payment back out. The fact that the refund or payout comes from a reputable insurance company gives it an air of legitimacy that a direct bank transfer could never achieve.

There are two key appeals. First, the money trail is effectively broken ‚Äî between placement (paying premiums) and the eventual payout (surrender or claim), the funds appear to be the proceeds of a legitimate insurance arrangement. Second, the payer of premiums and the recipient of the payout do not need to be the same person, creating additional distance between the criminal and the money.

Step 1: Placement

Criminal pays large single-premium or regular premiums into a life/investment-linked policy. Funds enter the insurance system.

üîÄ

Step 2: Layering

Policy sits in force. Time passes. Trail between original criminal funds and current policy cools. Format has changed ‚Äî funds are now "invested."

Step 3: Integration

Criminal surrenders policy. Receives a payment from the insurer ‚Äî appearing entirely legitimate. Can use this as source-of-funds documentation.

The Early Surrender Red Flag

One of the clearest indicators of insurance-based laundering is early surrender ‚Äî cancelling a policy before its intended term, often accepting a financial penalty in doing so. This seems irrational for a genuine investor, because it means losing money. But for a launderer, the penalty is simply a cost of laundering ‚Äî the price of receiving a clean payment from the insurer.

The ICA is explicit about this. Early surrender, particularly with acceptance of a penalty, is one of the top insurance sector red flags. If a client presents funds from an insurance surrender as their source of funds for a property purchase, you need to go one step further: how were the premiums originally funded?

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Insurance Red Flags

The ICA identifies the following as key red flags in insurance products. These are commonly tested ‚Äî memorise them:

  • Early surrender of a policy, especially where the client accepts a significant financial penalty
  • Payment of premiums from third-party sources (who is really funding this?)
  • Unexplained change in premium payment method
  • Single large one-off premium payments, especially to investment-linked products
  • Reluctance to provide source of funds for large single-premium policies
  • Overpayment of premiums
  • Requests to redirect payouts to third-party accounts

Third-Party Premiums: A Deeper Red Flag

When a third party pays premiums on someone else's policy, this should immediately raise questions. Legitimate reasons exist ‚Äî a business might pay premiums on a key person policy, for example ‚Äî but where the third-party relationship is unclear, or where the policyholder cannot explain who is funding their policy, this is a significant red flag.

The structure serves laundering perfectly: criminal A pays premiums on criminal B's policy, so even if A's funds are later investigated, the trail from A to the insurance payout appears to be a legitimate gift or arrangement. When B eventually surrenders the policy, the connection to A's criminal activity is obscured.

üìã Property Sector Scenario

A client approaches your estate agency to purchase a ¬£650,000 flat. Their stated source of funds is a surrender payment from a life insurance policy ‚Äî ¬£680,000 received from a major UK insurer 3 months ago. The client says they "needed the money for a property purchase."

Should this satisfy your source of funds obligation? No. The surrender is itself a red flag (deliberate liquidation). You must trace back to how the premiums were originally funded ‚Äî the insurer's payment tells you nothing about the original source. Request documentation showing the premium payment source ‚Äî ideally years of bank statements demonstrating regular funded payments from legitimate employment or other verified income.

üè† Coadjute / Property Sector Link

Insurance may seem like a separate sector from property ‚Äî but the two intersect directly. Consider: a criminal uses an insurance policy as collateral for a mortgage on a property purchase. Or one who inflates a claim on a property following arson to receive a "clean" payout. Your team will encounter clients referencing insurance payouts as a source of funds ‚Äî every time, you must go back further and verify how the premiums were funded.

Investment Products

Investment funds, shares, and retail financial products are often assumed to be protected by strict remittance rules. The ICA directly challenges this assumption ‚Äî and understanding why matters for your day-to-day work.

The Common Misconception

There is a widespread belief ‚Äî even among financial services professionals ‚Äî that investment products cannot be used for money laundering if proceeds must return to the originating account. The logic seems sound: if the money goes out and comes back to the same place, what has been laundered?

The ICA explicitly addresses and rejects this reasoning. The critical point is that the format of the funds has changed. Criminal cash that enters an investment fund is converted into units, shares, or other instruments. When redeemed, even to the same account, the criminal now has documented investment income ‚Äî not cash of unknown origin. This is layering.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The "Strict Remittance" Fallacy

"The investment manager who says 'I know funds can't be laundered through our products because there are strict remittance rules meaning proceeds can only go back to the originating account' is wrong." ‚Äî ICA. Even with strict rules, the change of format is a layering technique. The originating account may itself be part of a layering scheme.

Why Investment Products Are Vulnerable

Beyond the format-change technique, the ICA identifies several structural features that make investment products attractive to launderers:

  • Volume of transactions: Investment managers handle enormous transaction flows. Individually monitoring each one is practically difficult, creating opportunities to hide illicit transactions within normal volume.
  • Complex products: The use of derivatives, structured products, and multi-layered investment vehicles can make tracing the ultimate source of funds extremely difficult.
  • Commercial pressure: Investment managers face intense pressure to gather assets under management. This can create reluctance to ask probing questions of large investors, or to decline business on AML grounds. The ICA identifies this commercial pressure as a specific sector vulnerability.
  • Disconnect from source: Retail investment products are often sold through intermediaries, meaning the investment manager may never have direct contact with the underlying client or their source of funds documentation.

The Change-of-Format Technique in Property

The change-of-format technique is directly relevant to property work. Consider a client who presents as their source of funds the proceeds of a portfolio of unit trusts redeemed over several years. The investment returns look legitimate. But the question is always: what funded the original investment? If that question cannot be answered satisfactorily ‚Äî if the client cannot provide documentation showing the original source of the capital that went into the investments ‚Äî the source of funds chain is broken.

üè† Coadjute / Property Sector Link

At Coadjute, you may encounter clients presenting investment accounts, ISA proceeds, or share portfolio sales as their source of funds for a property transaction. In each case, your obligation is the same: trace back to the original source. "I redeemed my investments" is not a source of funds ‚Äî it is a description of the most recent transaction in the chain. You need to go back to where the investment capital originated.

Betting & Gaming

Casinos, online betting, and gaming platforms have long been associated with money laundering. The Cullen Commission in British Columbia ‚Äî examining corruption in BC casinos ‚Äî provided the clearest documented case study of how directly casino laundering flows into property markets.

Why the Gambling Sector is Vulnerable

The gambling sector presents a unique combination of features that make it attractive to launderers:

  • Cash-based transactions: Traditional casinos accept large amounts of cash, which can be converted into chips and then back to a casino cheque ‚Äî a document appearing to represent gambling winnings.
  • Legitimate cover for large transactions: Gambling is a legitimate activity, and large wins are plausible. A casino cheque for ¬£50,000 does not automatically look suspicious.
  • Convertibility: Casino chips can be exchanged between players, effectively transferring value with minimal paper trail.
  • International operations: Online gaming platforms may operate across multiple jurisdictions, complicating regulatory oversight.

The Classic Casino Technique: Chip Washing

The most well-documented casino laundering technique is chip washing (also called "chip dumping" in variations). The criminal purchases chips with criminal cash, gambles for a short period to create the appearance of gambling activity, then cashes out the chips ‚Äî receiving a casino cheque that documents the payment as gambling winnings.

The acceptability of a loss is key to understanding why this works. If a launderer spends ¬£80,000 in chips and cashes out ¬£70,000, they have effectively paid a 12.5% commission on their laundering. They are happy to do this because the ¬£70,000 cheque from a regulated casino is now entirely clean. The "loss" is the cost of laundering.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Casino Red Flags
  • Low betting relative to account/chip balance ‚Äî depositing and withdrawing with minimal gambling
  • Intentional losing or passing funds to other players ‚Äî transferring value disguised as losses
  • Multiple casino accounts or accounts in different names
  • Large cash buy-ins followed by early cash-out
  • Third parties cashing out on another's behalf
  • Requests to pay out in the form of a casino cheque rather than returning to original payment method
  • Structuring deposits below CDD thresholds across multiple visits

UK threshold: Under the Money Laundering Regulations 2017, UK casino operators must apply CDD when a customer conducts transactions totalling ‚Ç¨2,000 or more.

Online Gaming and Sports Betting

The shift to online gaming has created new vulnerabilities. The ability to create multiple accounts, use payment intermediaries, and operate across jurisdictions makes online platforms harder to monitor. The ICA notes several specific online gaming typologies:

  • Account funding and withdrawal: Funding an account, making minimal bets, and withdrawing ‚Äî using the platform as a conduit rather than a genuine gambling environment.
  • Matched betting laundering: Placing bets on all outcomes of an event via multiple accounts to guarantee a near-zero loss while converting the deposit into "winnings."
  • Sports betting vouchers and tokens: Some platforms issue vouchers or tokens that can be transferred ‚Äî creating an untracked store of value.
üìã Case Study: BC Casinos / Cullen Commission

The Cullen Commission in British Columbia (2020-2022) documented in detail how criminal cash ‚Äî much of it linked to drug trafficking ‚Äî was laundered through Lower Mainland casinos and then funnelled directly into the Vancouver real estate market. The Commission found casino operators had accepted known bags of suspicious cash from high-value gamblers, with some operators actively facilitating the process. Casino proceeds were traced directly into property purchases across BC.

This is the most significant documented case of casino-to-property laundering in recent history, and directly illustrates the property sector's exposure to gambling proceeds.

üè† Coadjute / Property Sector Link

When a client states gambling winnings as a source of funds for a property transaction, this demands enhanced scrutiny. Casino winnings should be verifiable ‚Äî the casino will have records of payouts. Vague references to "a good run at the casino" or "online poker winnings" without documentary evidence (casino cashier records, online account statements showing deposit and withdrawal history) should not be accepted. The volume of the win relative to the stated wealth of the client also matters ‚Äî a ¬£200,000 casino payout from a client with modest documented income is a significant red flag.

A customer deposits ¬£400,000 into their online casino account. Over three months, they bet occasionally but withdraw ¬£395,000 ‚Äî almost the entire balance. Is this suspicious?

Mobile Financial Services & Prepaid Cards

Mobile financial services have transformed access to banking in the developing world ‚Äî but they've also created new channels for money laundering where regulatory oversight is uneven and agent networks are large and hard to monitor.

What Are Mobile Financial Services?

Mobile financial services (m-FS) covers a wide range of activities:

  • Mobile money (m-Money): Services like M-Pesa (Kenya/Africa) that allow users to store and transfer funds via a mobile phone without a traditional bank account.
  • Mobile banking: Accessing existing bank accounts via a smartphone app.
  • Mobile payments: Making payments via phone (Apple Pay, Google Pay, etc.).
  • Prepaid cards: Cards pre-loaded with a fixed value, often without full identity verification at the loading stage.

The Four m-FS Risk Categories

The ICA organises m-FS vulnerabilities into four distinct risk categories. These are testable ‚Äî you need to know all four:

CategoryKey Risks
Geographic RiskServices operating across borders or in countries with weak AML frameworks; large unbanked populations where cash is predominant
Customer RiskAnonymous or pseudonymous users; customers using multiple SIM cards or accounts; customers whose identity cannot be reliably verified at onboarding
Agent RiskThird-party agents distributing the service (especially in retail networks) who may facilitate fraudulent accounts, identity theft, or fail to apply required CDD
Product RiskHigh transaction limits; cross-border transfer capability; minimal friction at account loading; ability to load anonymously with cash

Agent Risk in Detail

The agent network deserves particular attention. In m-FS models ‚Äî especially in emerging markets ‚Äî the service provider distributes through thousands of third-party agents (kiosks, shops, mobile phone dealers). These agents are the first point of contact with customers, and they are responsible for conducting customer due diligence at account opening.

The problems are obvious: agents may be inadequately trained, may be corrupt, may be colluding with criminal customers, or may face competitive pressure to onboard customers quickly without proper checks. A corrupt agent can create accounts for criminal customers using false identities, and the transaction records will appear entirely legitimate from the service provider's perspective.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî m-FS Agent Risk Factors
  • The agent is an intermediary ‚Äî the service provider has no direct contact with the customer
  • Agents may not adequately understand or apply CDD requirements
  • Agents may be corrupt or colluding with criminals
  • Agents are incentivised to register customers quickly ‚Äî commercial pressure reduces scrutiny
  • Multiple agents can register the same criminal customer under different identities

Prepaid Cards: The Anonymous Loading Problem

Prepaid cards are one of the most direct tools available to launderers seeking to convert cash. The key vulnerability is anonymous cash loading ‚Äî in many jurisdictions, prepaid cards can be loaded with cash at retail locations without full identity verification, up to certain limits. A criminal can load multiple cards, use them for purchases, and effectively convert cash into card payments without creating any identity-linked record.

Mitigating factors include loading limits, transaction limits, and requirements to register above certain thresholds ‚Äî but these vary significantly by jurisdiction and product. The ICA notes that prepaid cards present particular challenges in cross-border money movement.

Why m-FS is Less Risky Than Physical Cash

Despite the vulnerabilities above, the ICA makes an important point: m-FS transactions are less risky than physical cash because every transaction creates a digital audit trail. Who sent what to whom, when, from which location ‚Äî all of this is recorded. This makes m-FS considerably more traceable than physical cash, and regulators can (with appropriate access) reconstruct transaction histories.

‚úÖ Countering m-FS Risks ‚Äî Firm-Level Controls

The ICA identifies the following as appropriate firm-level controls: agent due diligence including ongoing monitoring of agent behaviour; CDD at account opening; watch lists and sanctions screening; staff training; automated transaction monitoring; and compliance monitoring programmes.

Virtual Currencies

Cryptocurrency and virtual currencies have created both legitimate financial innovation and significant AML vulnerabilities. This is a live and growing issue in property transactions ‚Äî clients presenting crypto proceeds as source of funds is now commonplace.

What Are Virtual Currencies?

The ICA uses the term virtual currencies (VCs) as the preferred broad term, encompassing all types. This includes:

  • Bitcoin: The original and largest cryptocurrency. Pseudonymous ‚Äî transactions tied to wallet addresses rather than identities, but all transactions publicly recorded on the blockchain.
  • Privacy coins (Monero, Zcash): Designed specifically to obscure transaction details, offering significantly greater anonymity than Bitcoin. The ICA treats these as a heightened risk.
  • Stablecoins: Cryptocurrencies pegged to a fiat currency (USD, GBP etc.). Reduce volatility but retain the transfer and pseudonymity features of other VCs.
  • Tokens and NFTs: Digital assets issued on blockchain platforms. Can represent anything from gaming items to financial instruments.

Why Virtual Currencies Are Attractive for Laundering

The ICA identifies three primary features that make VCs attractive to money launderers:

üé≠

Pseudonymity

Transactions tied to wallet addresses ‚Äî not names. On most blockchains, anyone can transact without identity verification at the protocol level.

üåç

Global Transfer

Cross-border transfers occur in minutes, without bank intermediaries. Jurisdictional controls are easily bypassed.

‚ö°

Speed & Irreversibility

Transactions are often near-instant and irreversible. Once funds move, recovery is extremely difficult.

Tumbling and Mixing Services

A particularly important VC laundering technique is the use of tumbling or mixing services. These services take cryptocurrency from multiple users, pool and mix the transactions together, then redistribute equivalent amounts (minus a fee) to output addresses. The effect is to break the on-chain link between input and output addresses ‚Äî making it extremely difficult for investigators to trace the origin of specific funds.

The ICA is explicit about mixers/tumblers: they serve no legitimate commercial purpose. Their sole function is to obscure the origin of funds. The use of a mixer or tumbler service should be treated as a significant red flag indicating potential laundering activity.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Liberty Reserve Case Study

Liberty Reserve (shut down 2013) laundered over $6 billion across more than one million users. It operated as a centralised VC exchange requiring only a name, email address, and date of birth to register ‚Äî with no verification of any of these. Transactions were processed through a network of third-party exchangers, allowing criminals to convert cash into LR dollars and move funds globally with minimal trail. Its shutdown by US authorities (SDNY) was the largest money laundering prosecution in US history at the time, and established the benchmark for VC ML regulation.

Exchanges: Regulated vs Unregulated

A key distinction in VC risk assessment is between regulated and unregulated exchanges. Regulated exchanges (registered with HMRC's cryptoasset register in the UK, or equivalent overseas) are required to apply CDD measures. They will have records of who opened accounts and, ideally, the original fiat source of funds.

However, not all exchanges are equal. A client who says "I bought Bitcoin on Binance" is making a different statement from one who says they transacted through a peer-to-peer platform with no KYC. Both require scrutiny, but the latter carries significantly higher risk. And crucially ‚Äî the exchange is not the end of the trail. You need to establish where the fiat currency came from before it was converted into crypto.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî VC Red Flags

Key red flags identified by the ICA for virtual currencies:

  • Use of mixing/tumbling services (identified from blockchain analysis tools)
  • Use of privacy coins (Monero, Zcash) ‚Äî designed for anonymity, not legitimate use
  • Transactions through unregulated or offshore exchanges with no KYC
  • Multiple small VC purchases below AML thresholds (structuring)
  • Converting VC proceeds to cash via multiple peer-to-peer transactions
  • Inability to explain the original fiat source of crypto holdings

Crypto Source of Funds: What You Actually Need

When a client presents cryptocurrency proceeds as their source of funds for a property transaction, the question to ask is not "where did the crypto come from?" but rather "where did the money come from that was used to buy the crypto?" A client presenting a Coinbase statement showing proceeds of ¬£900,000 needs to demonstrate:

  • The exchange they used (regulated? KYC-compliant?)
  • The original fiat source that funded the crypto purchase ‚Äî bank statements showing the transfer in
  • Whether any mixing services or privacy coins were involved
  • Provenance documentation from the exchange (transaction records, KYC confirmation)

"I have Bitcoin" is not a source of funds ‚Äî it is a current asset form. You always need to go back further.

üè† Coadjute / Property Sector Link

Crypto-funded property purchases are increasingly common, particularly for higher-value London and South East properties. The risk is significant. The National Crime Agency has flagged crypto proceeds as a growing source of funds in property ML cases. Your firm's SOF process must be equipped to handle crypto ‚Äî asking not just for the exchange statement but for the full fiat origin chain. If the chain cannot be established, the transaction should not proceed.

Red Flags & Sector Summary

Before the quiz, let's consolidate the key red flags across all sectors covered in this module ‚Äî and consider how they connect to your work at Coadjute.

The Common Thread: Why These Sectors Are Targeted

Across insurance, investment products, gaming, mobile financial services, and virtual currencies, a common pattern emerges. Criminals seek sectors where:

  • Cash or criminal funds can enter without triggering automatic suspicion
  • Value can be converted into a different format that obscures origins
  • Payouts appear legitimate ‚Äî from insurers, investment accounts, casinos, or crypto exchanges
  • Regulatory oversight is weak or inconsistent
  • Transaction values can be structured to avoid reporting thresholds

The Coadjute Principle: Follow the Money Back

When a client presents source of funds documentation, your obligation is not just to accept the most recent payment ‚Äî it's to understand the underlying origin. Insurance payouts, investment redemptions, gambling winnings, crypto sales ‚Äî all of these are legitimate for many clients. But each requires you to go one step further: what was the original source of the funds that went into those products?

‚úÖ The Follow-the-Money Principle

Each high-risk sector creates a potential "laundry exit point" ‚Äî a point at which dirty money emerges appearing clean. Your job is to trace the chain back before that exit point. The insurer, investment manager, or crypto exchange cannot tell you where the original criminal funds came from. Only rigorous source-of-funds questioning can do that.
