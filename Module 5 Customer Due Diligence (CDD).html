What is Customer Due Diligence?

CDD is the foundation of every firm's AML framework. It is not a one-time identity check ‚Äî it is an ongoing process of knowing your customer well enough to spot when something is wrong.

CDD in Plain English

At its core, CDD is a risk assessment. It is the process by which a business obtains sufficient information about a client to assess the ML/TF risks that relationship poses ‚Äî at the outset, and throughout.

The ICA is direct: without good CDD, you cannot understand the potential a relationship has for exposing your organisation to the risk of money laundering. CDD is your most effective weapon against being used to launder the proceeds of crime.

You may also hear it referred to as KYC ‚Äî Know Your Customer. KYC is another term for the same concept: understanding the personal and financial circumstances of a client.

‚ÑπÔ∏è CDD Serves Multiple Purposes

Beyond AML compliance, good CDD: protects genuine clients from being suspected of ML; guards against identity theft; protects the firm from reputational and financial risk; assists in detecting fraud; and is a legal obligation in the UK under the MLR 2017.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The FATF Definition of CDD

FATF defines CDD as comprising four elements. Memorise these ‚Äî they are tested in multiple forms:

  • (a) Identifying the customer and verifying that customer's identity using reliable, independent source documents, data or information
  • (b) Identifying the beneficial owner, and taking reasonable measures to verify the identity of the beneficial owner ‚Äî for legal persons and arrangements, understanding the ownership and control structure
  • (c) Understanding and, as appropriate, obtaining information on the purpose and intended nature of the business relationship
  • (d) Conducting ongoing due diligence on the business relationship and scrutiny of transactions to ensure they are consistent with the institution's knowledge of the customer, their business and risk profile, including, where necessary, the source of funds

When Must CDD Be Applied?

A firm must apply CDD measures in the following four situations:

  1. When establishing a business relationship
  2. When carrying out an occasional transaction (even a one-off)
  3. When there are suspicions of ML or TF, regardless of any other trigger
  4. When there are doubts about the veracity or adequacy of documents or information previously obtained for identification or verification purposes
‚ö†Ô∏è ICA Non-Negotiable Rule

"CDD must be completed in all cases before any transactions are completed for any client." There is no minimum transaction threshold, no grace period, no exception for trusted long-standing clients. CDD first ‚Äî then the transaction.

The Three-Tier CDD Framework

Regulation expects a risk-based approach. Not every client carries the same risk, so CDD intensity varies across three levels:

Simplified (SDD)

Reduced measures for genuinely low-risk situations. Still requires basic identification ‚Äî NOT an exemption.

Standard (CDD)

The default for most clients. Full identification, verification, understanding of business relationship and ongoing monitoring.

Enhanced (EDD)

Mandatory for PEPs, high-risk third countries, and any higher-risk situations. Deep-dive source of wealth, senior management sign-off.

üè† Coadjute / Property Sector Context

At Coadjute, you apply CDD on behalf of estate agents and conveyancers. Every property transaction involves a buyer, a seller, and often a company or trust. Your CDD obligation is to identify who those people really are ‚Äî and to understand where their money comes from. The property sector is specifically recognised as high-risk in UK AML regulations (MLR 2017), so standard CDD applies as a minimum for all transactions, with EDD required where risk indicators are present.

Standard CDD in Practice

Standard CDD is the default for most clients. Understanding what it requires ‚Äî and how to calibrate it ‚Äî is the core practical skill this module builds.

The Core Building Blocks

Standard CDD requires a firm to do three things:

  1. Identify the client ‚Äî including beneficial owners of any legal entity
  2. Verify that identity ‚Äî using reliable, independent source documents, data or information
  3. Record sufficient additional information ‚Äî to understand the relationship and assist in detecting suspicious activity

That third element is often underweighted. The ICA is clear that identification and verification alone are not enough. You also need to understand the client's circumstances well enough that you can spot when something doesn't fit.

What "Additional Information" Means in Practice

For an individual, the additional information the ICA expects includes:

  • The type of business or employment they are engaged in
  • Their industry or sector
  • Whether they are new or established in that business
  • Their stage of life (a 22-year-old buying a ¬£2m property warrants more scrutiny than a 55-year-old)
  • The counterparties they deal with ‚Äî who they pay and who pays them
  • The geographical areas in which they operate
  • The expected turnover on the account ‚Äî values, volumes and frequency of money in and out

This information creates a client profile. That profile is what allows you to detect when activity is unusual ‚Äî because "unusual" only has meaning when you have a baseline expectation.

üìã ICA Scenario ‚Äî Calibrating CDD Intensity

Same Process, Very Different Depth

The ICA illustrates how the same CDD framework applies at very different levels of depth:

Client A: A salaried individual working for a multinational company who wants a credit card. Standard ID verification, basic income check ‚Äî straightforward.

Client B: A politician based in a country with a reputation for high levels of corruption and poor regulation, seeking trade finance and large term deposits. Extensive inquiry into source of wealth, source of funds, senior management sign-off, enhanced ongoing monitoring ‚Äî a completely different undertaking.

Both are applying CDD. The difference is the depth and rigour ‚Äî driven by the risk profile.

CDD for Individuals vs. Legal Entities

For Individuals

Standard verification typically requires government-issued photo ID (passport or driving licence) plus evidence of address (utility bill, bank statement). The ICA notes that even standard documents can be fake ‚Äî the emergence of websites supplying fraudulent utility bills and bank statements is a real challenge.

For Companies and Legal Entities

When the client is a company, the CDD obligation does not stop at the company's name. You must understand the ownership and control structure ‚Äî who ultimately owns and controls the company. This means identifying the beneficial owner: the natural person who ultimately owns or controls the entity.

Client TypeWhat to Identify & VerifyKey Risk
IndividualFull name, address, date of birth, nationality; verify with reliable ID documentsFake documents; identity theft
Company (UK)Registered name, address, company number; directors; shareholders; ultimate beneficial owners (UBOs)Nominee directors/shareholders; complex structures
Company (Overseas)As above, plus understand the jurisdiction's legal framework and transparency standardsSecrecy jurisdictions; no public register of ownership
TrustSettlor, trustees, protector (if any), beneficiaries; understand purpose and termsNominee settlors; discretionary trusts concealing true beneficiaries
PartnershipAll partners; nature of business; source of partnership fundsSilent partners; LLPs used as layering vehicles
‚ö†Ô∏è The "Legal Entity" Trap

A company name on a title deed tells you almost nothing. A company can have nominee directors, nominee shareholders, and be owned by another company in a different jurisdiction. Your CDD obligation is to look through the corporate structure to the natural person who ultimately controls it. This can require multiple layers of inquiry.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Applying CDD: The Two Scenarios

Mrs A: Opens a joint current account with her employed husband. Resident in the account country, depositing savings from salary. ‚Üí Standard CDD applies.

Mrs B: A Filipino national, UK resident, senior diplomat, opening current and savings accounts with an initial ¬£12,000 deposit. ‚Üí Must determine PEP status. If PEP (senior diplomat may qualify), Enhanced Due Diligence applies ‚Äî including source of wealth investigation.

These examples appear in the ICA manual and are representative of the type of scenario-based questions you should expect in the exam.

Simplified Due Diligence (SDD)

SDD is not an exemption from CDD ‚Äî it is a reduction in what is required, available only in genuinely low-risk situations that your firm has actively assessed and documented.

What Is SDD?

Most AML regulations allow for a lighter-touch approach in the lowest-risk situations. The key word is allow ‚Äî it is not automatic. A firm must make an active, documented decision that the specific relationship or transaction presents a genuinely low ML/TF risk before applying SDD.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî JMLSG Definition of SDD (2023)

The JMLSG 2023 guidance states:

"A firm may apply SDD measures in relation to a particular business relationship or transaction if it determines that, taking into account its risk assessment, the business relationship or transaction presents a low degree of risk of ML/TF."

Critical: The FATF Interpretive Notes make clear that SDD is a reduction in measures, not an elimination. Basic identification requirements still apply. Any decision to use SDD must be carefully documented and justifiable to regulators.

What Can Be Reduced Under SDD?

The FATF Interpretive Notes to Recommendation 10 set out examples of what SDD measures might look like in practice:

  • Verifying the identity of the customer after establishing the business relationship (e.g. when account transactions rise above a defined monetary threshold) ‚Äî delayed verification rather than no verification
  • Reducing the frequency of customer identification updates
  • Reducing the degree of ongoing monitoring and transaction scrutiny based on a reasonable monetary threshold
  • Not collecting specific information to understand the business relationship's purpose, but inferring the purpose from the type of transaction or relationship
‚ö†Ô∏è SDD is NOT a Free Pass

The ICA is explicit: SDD may provide opportunities to reduce costs and remove paperwork ‚Äî but it does not mean you can ignore the client entirely. In a simple term-assurance life insurance policy, for example, minimal documents may be collected at account opening ‚Äî but greater checks apply at the claim pay-out stage. The risk hasn't disappeared; it has shifted to a different point in the relationship.

When Is SDD Typically Available?

Specific situations permitting SDD are usually set out in the legislation applying to your firm. Common examples include:

  • Listed companies on regulated, recognised exchanges (ownership is transparent)
  • Regulated financial institutions themselves (subject to their own AML regimes)
  • Government or public bodies
  • Low-value, low-risk product types (e.g. basic term life assurance with no investment element and low premiums)
  • Products or services with no realistic ML risk due to structural limitations
üî¥ When SDD Is Never Available

SDD can never be applied to Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs), clients from high-risk third countries, or any situation where there are active suspicions of ML or TF. These situations always require EDD ‚Äî regardless of any other assessment. There is no discretion here.

üè† Coadjute / Property Sector Context

In property transactions, SDD will be uncommon. The property sector is explicitly high-risk under UK AML regulation. However, SDD might conceivably apply for very small value transactions, or where the counterparty is a regulated UK financial institution whose own AML framework you can rely on. In practice, your firm's compliance guidance will specify exactly when (if ever) SDD is available for property work.

Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)

EDD is mandatory in specific high-risk situations. It goes beyond standard ID verification to examine the source of wealth and funds in depth ‚Äî and requires senior management involvement.

When Is EDD Mandatory?

Regulations specify certain situations where EDD is not optional ‚Äî it is a legal requirement:

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Mandatory EDD Triggers
  • Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs) ‚Äî and their close family members and associates
  • Customers from, or transactions involving, high-risk third countries identified by FATF as having strategic deficiencies
  • Correspondent banking relationships
  • Any situation identified by the firm's own risk assessment as presenting higher ML/TF risk
  • Non-face-to-face business relationships (in some jurisdictions ‚Äî increased risk of identity fraud)
  • Complex or unusually large transactions with no apparent economic purpose

What Does EDD Actually Involve?

EDD means going significantly further than standard identity checks. The ICA outlines several additional measures:

Source of Wealth vs Source of Funds

These two concepts are frequently confused and are both tested in the ICA exam:

ConceptDefinitionExample
Source of Wealth (SoW)How the client built up their total wealth over their lifetime"Built and sold a manufacturing business in 2018 for ¬£4m"
Source of Funds (SoF)Where the specific funds in this transaction came from"¬£850,000 from the proceeds of selling my previous home"

For EDD clients, both must be established and documented. Simply asserting a source is not enough ‚Äî the ICA is explicit about which explanations are not acceptable:

üî¥ Unacceptable EDD Source of Wealth Descriptions

The ICA explicitly states that the following descriptions are insufficient as source of wealth for EDD purposes. They require further investigation, not acceptance:

  • "Savings" ‚Äî from what income, over what period?
  • "Profits from investments" ‚Äî which investments, where held, how generated?
  • "Inheritance" ‚Äî from whom, what was their wealth, documented with probate?
  • "Business dealings" ‚Äî what business, where, what does it do?
  • "Sale of business" ‚Äî to whom, for what sum, with what evidence?

These are starting points for enquiry, not final answers.

Methods for Corroborating Source of Wealth

The ICA identifies the following as valid approaches:

  • In-depth client interviews (and documenting the answers thoroughly)
  • Collection of documentary evidence ‚Äî accounts, contracts, tax returns, sale agreements, probate documents
  • Reference to publicly available information from reliable sources ‚Äî Land Registry, Companies House, news sources
  • Background checks using commercial databases
  • Verification of information with third parties (where appropriate and legal)

Senior Management Approval

For EDD clients ‚Äî particularly PEPs ‚Äî establishing the relationship requires senior management approval. The Singapore MAS Notice (quoted in the ICA manual) requires a bank to obtain approval from senior management to establish or continue business relations with a PEP client, and to conduct enhanced monitoring throughout the relationship.

üìã Property Scenario ‚Äî EDD in a Real Transaction

The Offshore Company Purchase

A buyer wants to purchase a ¬£3.5m London property through a company registered in the Cayman Islands. The company has a nominee director. The stated source of funds is "proceeds of business in West Africa."

EDD Required. Here's what that looks like:

  • Identify and verify the ultimate beneficial owner behind the Cayman company ‚Äî look through the nominee structure
  • Obtain senior management sign-off before proceeding
  • Conduct in-depth source of wealth investigation ‚Äî what is the West African business? Who are the counterparties? Obtain audited accounts, contracts, banking records
  • Assess country risk ‚Äî which West African country? Are there FATF concerns?
  • Check PEP status of the UBO ‚Äî do they have any connection to government or public functions?
  • Apply enhanced ongoing monitoring throughout the transaction
‚ÑπÔ∏è The "Domestic PEP" Change (4MLD)

The Fourth EU Money Laundering Directive (4MLD) removed a previous anomaly: prior to 4MLD, only foreign PEPs automatically required EDD. After 4MLD (incorporated into UK law), domestic PEPs ‚Äî those from your own jurisdiction ‚Äî also require EDD. In practice this made little difference, as most UK firms were already risk-rating domestic senior officials as high-risk. Firms may assess local PEPs as lower risk than foreign PEPs if there is reasonable, documented justification.

Plausibility Testing

Plausibility testing is the ICA's term for what is, at heart, common sense. It means stepping back from the documents and asking: does this client's story actually make sense?

What Is Plausibility Testing?

The ICA defines plausibility testing as "quite simply using common sense when applying CDD." It is the professional obligation to assess whether a client's stated circumstances, financial profile, and explanation of source of funds/wealth are credible ‚Äî given everything you know about them.

CDD is never a tick-box exercise. Even in a routine, low-risk case, each situation should be treated on its merits. Plausibility testing is the mental discipline that prevents CDD from becoming administrative box-ticking.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The ICA Plausibility Test Example

The manual sets out a detailed example that appears in various forms in exam questions:

Client profile: 24-year-old, single, UK national, resident in UAE for 12 months. Wants to open current and savings accounts. Previously banked with a rival in UAE. Occupation: IT Consultant, income ~AED 150,000 ($41,000). Has a property in UAE valued at 5 million Dirhams AND one in Germany valued at ‚Ç¨13 million ‚Äî both rented, no mortgages.

ICA verdict: On the face of it, not plausible.

  • A large amount of assets for his age
  • How were the property purchases funded without mortgages on an IT consultant's income?
  • How long has he had the properties? Pre-employment?
  • What else does he receive from his employer? Other income streams?

The ICA notes there may be a rational explanation ‚Äî perhaps inherited wealth, or early tech success. But: "unless you ask the question you will not know."

The Four Plausibility Testing Questions

The ICA sets out four key questions to work through when applying plausibility testing ‚Äî particularly for EDD clients:

üîç ICA Plausibility Testing Framework
  • Are you convinced that the funds and wealth can be reasonably explained and established to be legitimate?
  • Can you independently obtain evidence of the client's source of wealth for higher-risk accounts?
  • Are you able to establish the relationship between the client and any third party where accounts are funded by a third party?
  • Do you continue asking for information and persist in seeking clarity wherever circumstances are unclear or account structures are complex?

Property Sector Plausibility Scenarios

Plausibility testing is especially important in property transactions, where large sums change hands and the sector is specifically identified as high-risk. Some common scenarios:

Client StatementPlausibility QuestionWhat to Do
"I saved ¬£450,000 cash from my market stall over 10 years"What is a realistic annual profit from a market stall? Does the maths work? Is there any banking history to support this?Request trading accounts, bank records showing accumulation over time
"The funds are from my family in Nigeria"Who is the family member? What is the source of their wealth? Is there any connection to PEP status or high-risk business?Identify the third-party source; apply EDD to the relationship between parties
"I run a cash business ‚Äî restaurant"What is the restaurant's turnover? Is the amount being deposited consistent with HMRC records and trading accounts?Request business accounts, HMRC records; sense-check against industry benchmarks
"I sold my company last year"To whom? For how much? Are there Companies House / news records of the sale?Obtain sale agreement, completion statements, evidence of funds received
"I won at gambling"Is there documentary evidence? Casino winnings of this scale would be documented by the casinoRequest casino payment records; a vague claim is not acceptable
‚úÖ The ICA's Key Insight on Plausibility

You are not a detective. You are not required to prove that a crime has been committed. You are simply required to satisfy yourself that the explanation makes sense ‚Äî and to persist with questions when it doesn't. Accepting an implausible explanation without enquiry is a failure of your CDD obligation.

Ongoing Monitoring

CDD is not a one-time event at onboarding. Regulation 28 of the MLR 2017 imposes a clear, ongoing obligation to scrutinise transactions and keep CDD records up to date throughout the relationship.

The Legal Basis: Regulation 28, MLR 2017

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Regulation 28 (MLR 2017)

Regulation 28 of the UK Money Laundering Regulations 2017 states that a relevant person must conduct ongoing monitoring of a business relationship, including:

  • (a) Scrutiny of transactions undertaken throughout the course of the relationship ‚Äî including, where necessary, the source of funds ‚Äî to ensure that transactions are consistent with the firm's knowledge of the customer, their business and risk profile
  • (b) Undertaking reviews of existing records and keeping the documents or information obtained for CDD purposes up to date

This obligation is ongoing ‚Äî it does not cease after the first transaction.

What Ongoing Monitoring Means in Practice

Ongoing monitoring has two distinct components: transaction scrutiny and keeping CDD records up to date. Both are legally required.

Transaction Scrutiny

Each transaction should be assessed for consistency with what the firm knows about the client. This means your initial CDD ‚Äî the client profile ‚Äî is the benchmark. A transaction is suspicious not in the abstract, but in relation to that specific client's known circumstances, history, and profile.

The ICA provides a framework for monitoring methodologies. An effective system should:

  • Compare account or transaction history against the client's specific profile
  • Compare against established ML/TF typologies and scenarios
  • Set up processes to compare transactions against risk models for "normal" clients ‚Äî not all large transactions are unusual; some clients regularly transact at high values
  • Generate alerts where appropriate
  • Track alerts to ensure they are actioned and suspicions reported as required
  • Be available for audit purposes
  • Provide management information and statistics
üìã ICA Case Study ‚Äî 9/11 Hijackers' Banking Activity

How the Terrorists Blended In (and the Monitoring Lessons)

The 19 hijackers of the 9/11 attacks opened 24 accounts. A review of their transaction activity showed patterns that, in retrospect, should have generated alerts. Their activity included:

  • Direct transfers to and from foreign jurisdictions (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Germany)
  • Most withdrawals were from debit cards immediately following deposits
  • Very few cheque transactions ‚Äî cash or card only
  • Transactions generally below reporting requirements (deliberate structuring)
  • Multiple hijackers withdrawing at the same ATM machines ‚Äî a pattern
  • Numerous balance enquiries between transactions

No single transaction was dramatic. The picture only emerged when transactions were viewed as a pattern over time. This is exactly what automated transaction monitoring is designed to detect.

Keeping CDD Records Up to Date

CDD information has a shelf life. A client who was low-risk at onboarding may become high-risk because:

  • They have taken on a new role that makes them a PEP
  • Adverse media has emerged linking them to criminal conduct
  • Their country of residence has changed to a high-risk jurisdiction
  • Their transaction patterns have changed significantly
  • Their ID documents have expired and need renewal
  • A business relationship has changed its nature significantly
‚ö†Ô∏è The Fixed 5-Year Review Trap

A firm that reviews all CDD records on a 5-year cycle ‚Äî and does nothing in between unless prompted ‚Äî is NOT meeting its ongoing monitoring obligations. Regulation 28 requires monitoring that is risk-sensitive and responsive to change. Material changes must trigger an immediate review. Periodic reviews are a minimum floor, not a substitute for responsiveness.

Automated Transaction Monitoring (ATM Systems)

Larger firms use automated systems to flag potentially suspicious transactions. The ICA (citing FCA guidance) emphasises that there must be a clear allocation of responsibilities for reviewing, investigating, and reporting alerts generated by these systems. Staff responsible for this work must have appropriate skills and be subject to effective quality assurance.

Automated alerts are only useful if they are acted upon. A backlog of unreviewed alerts is a regulatory failure ‚Äî and a sign of inadequate resources.

üè† Coadjute / Property Sector Context ‚Äî Ongoing Monitoring

Property transactions are typically discrete events rather than ongoing banking relationships ‚Äî but ongoing monitoring still applies. If a client returns for a second transaction, your earlier CDD must be reviewed and refreshed. If circumstances have changed (new address, new business, adverse media), those changes are material. For estate agents with ongoing client relationships, monitoring covers the continuing relationship ‚Äî not just each individual sale.

Identifying Suspicious Activity

Good CDD is what makes suspicious activity detectable. Without knowing your client, you cannot identify when they are behaving unusually. This section brings the module together ‚Äî how CDD translates into recognising and acting on suspicion.

Why CDD Is the Foundation of Suspicion Detection

The ICA is direct: "CDD information is invaluable in the recognition and determination of suspicion." Money laundering is rarely identified because you know for certain that a specific crime was committed. It is identified because a client's behaviour is inconsistent with what you know about them. That inconsistency is only visible if you have done your CDD properly.

How to Identify Suspicious Activity

Money laundering activity is typically identified in one of three situations:

  1. No legitimate commercial rationale for the relationship or transaction ‚Äî why is this person doing this?
  2. Suspicious client behaviour ‚Äî pressure to follow instructions without providing full answers; reluctance to provide information; evasive or inconsistent responses
  3. Unusual or irregular activity compared with: the client's historical pattern; what is known about their circumstances; the known commercial objectives; comparable clients in similar situations
üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Common Barriers to Reporting Suspicion

The ICA identifies several common reasons employees fail to report suspicious activity ‚Äî even when CDD information is on file. Know these ‚Äî they appear in scenario questions:

  • "I can't point to a specific crime" ‚Äî You don't need to. Suspicion of criminality in the broadest sense is sufficient. Most legislation sets the threshold at "reasonable grounds to suspect."
  • "I can't prove a crime was committed" ‚Äî Proof is not required. Suspicion is the threshold.
  • "No one aspect of the relationship is suspicious" ‚Äî It is very often the combination of factors that creates suspicion. Don't dismiss individual red flags because none alone is conclusive.
  • "The crime occurred before AML laws were introduced" ‚Äî You still have an obligation to report. It is possible to commit the offence of money laundering by knowingly handling property derived from historic crimes.
  • "This is a long-standing client" ‚Äî Existing client relationships pose as great a ML threat as new ones ‚Äî often greater, because complacency has set in.

The Suspicion Formulation Process

The ICA describes suspicion as a gradual process ‚Äî it rarely happens in an instant or from a single event. The typical pathway follows three stages:

üìä The Suspicion Formulation Pathway (ICA)

Comfort ‚Üí Concern ‚Üí Suspicion

An event within a normal client relationship triggers a concern. Once concerned, you should:

  1. Analyse available CDD and business information
  2. Discuss concerns with colleagues and managers
  3. Make discreet enquiries of the client ‚Äî this is not tipping off

Following this, either: concerns are allayed ‚Üí return to comfort; OR concerns remain/deepen ‚Üí suspicion ‚Üí report to the MLRO as quickly as possible.

Objective vs. Subjective Suspicion

The ICA draws an important distinction between two tests of suspicion:

TestWhat It AsksRisk for You
SubjectiveDid this person actually suspect ML was occurring?Lower ‚Äî it is what you genuinely believed
ObjectiveShould a reasonable person in this position have suspected ML, given all the relevant information available?Higher ‚Äî you may be judged on what you should have known, not just what you knew
üî¥ The Objective Test: Why It Matters

The objective test is the higher standard ‚Äî and the one that prosecutors use. It means that ignoring CDD information, failing to follow up on red flags, or simply not engaging with the information in your files can be criminal. The standard is higher for employees of financial services businesses and regulated firms than for those in unregulated sectors. You are expected to be trained to spot what a lay person might miss.

‚úÖ The CDD Module Summary

CDD is not bureaucracy ‚Äî it is how money laundering gets detected. Every element of CDD (identification, verification, understanding the relationship, ongoing monitoring, plausibility testing) exists to help you build a picture of the client that lets you spot when something is wrong. Done properly, it protects your clients, your firm, and the broader financial system.
