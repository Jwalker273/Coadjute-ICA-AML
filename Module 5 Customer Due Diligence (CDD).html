What is Customer Due Diligence?

CDD is the foundation of every firm's AML framework. It is not a one-time identity check ‚Äî it is an ongoing process of knowing your customer well enough to spot when something is wrong.

CDD in Plain English

At its core, CDD is a risk assessment. It is the process by which a business obtains sufficient information about a client to assess the ML/TF risks that relationship poses ‚Äî at the outset, and throughout the life of the relationship.

The ICA is direct: without good CDD, you cannot understand the potential a relationship has for exposing your organisation to the risk of money laundering. CDD is your most effective weapon against being used to launder the proceeds of crime.

You may also hear it referred to as KYC ‚Äî Know Your Customer. KYC is another term for the same concept: understanding the personal and financial circumstances of a client well enough that you can spot when something doesn't fit.

‚ÑπÔ∏è CDD Serves Multiple Purposes

Beyond AML compliance, good CDD protects genuine clients from being suspected of ML; guards against identity theft; protects the firm from reputational and financial risk; assists in detecting fraud; and is a legal obligation in the UK under the Money Laundering Regulations 2017.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The FATF Definition of CDD

FATF defines CDD as comprising four components. Memorise all four ‚Äî they are tested in multiple forms:

  • (a) Identifying the customer and verifying that customer's identity using reliable, independent source documents, data or information
  • (b) Identifying the beneficial owner, and taking reasonable measures to verify the identity of the beneficial owner ‚Äî for legal persons and arrangements, understanding the ownership and control structure
  • (c) Understanding and, as appropriate, obtaining information on the purpose and intended nature of the business relationship
  • (d) Conducting ongoing due diligence on the business relationship and scrutiny of transactions to ensure they are consistent with the institution's knowledge of the customer, their business and risk profile, including, where necessary, the source of funds

When Must CDD Be Applied?

A firm must apply CDD measures in the following four situations ‚Äî and only one needs to be present:

  1. When establishing a business relationship
  2. When carrying out an occasional transaction (even a one-off)
  3. When there are suspicions of ML or TF, regardless of any other trigger
  4. When there are doubts about the veracity or adequacy of documents or information previously obtained for identification or verification purposes
‚ö†Ô∏è ICA Non-Negotiable Rule

"CDD must be completed in all cases before any transactions are completed for any client." There is no minimum transaction threshold, no grace period, no exception for trusted long-standing clients. CDD first ‚Äî then the transaction.

The Three-Tier CDD Framework

Not every client carries the same risk, so CDD intensity varies across three levels. The choice of which level to apply must be documented and defensible:

üü¢ Simplified (SDD)

Reduced measures for genuinely low-risk situations. Still requires basic identification. NOT a full exemption from CDD.

üü£ Standard (CDD)

The default for most clients. Full identification, verification, understanding of the business relationship and ongoing monitoring.

üî¥ Enhanced (EDD)

Mandatory for PEPs, high-risk third countries, and any higher-risk situations. More intensive verification and senior sign-off.

Why CDD Matters at Coadjute

At Coadjute, we perform AML checks on behalf of estate agents and conveyancers. That means the CDD we conduct is the frontline defence for our clients' legal compliance. We are not a passive data-collector ‚Äî we are an active gatekeeper. If CDD is incomplete, inadequate, or not applied at the right time, the professional who instructed us may face regulatory action, fines, or criminal prosecution.

The MLR 2017 applies directly to estate agents and conveyancers as "relevant persons." They ‚Äî and by extension we ‚Äî have a legal duty to conduct CDD before completing any property transaction.

Standard CDD in Practice

Standard CDD is the default. It applies to the vast majority of client relationships and sets the benchmark against which all CDD is measured. Understanding what it requires ‚Äî and how to calibrate it ‚Äî is the core practical skill this module builds.

The Three Core Requirements

Standard CDD requires a firm to do three things:

  1. Identify the client ‚Äî including beneficial owners of any legal entity
  2. Verify that identity ‚Äî using reliable, independent source documents, data or information
  3. Record sufficient additional information ‚Äî to understand the relationship and assist in detecting suspicious activity

That third element is often underweighted in practice. Identification and verification alone are not enough. You also need to understand the client's circumstances well enough that you can spot when something doesn't fit.

What "Additional Information" Means in Practice

For an individual client, the additional information the ICA expects includes:

  • The type of business or employment they are engaged in
  • Their industry or sector
  • Whether they are new or established in that business
  • Their stage of life ‚Äî a 22-year-old buying a ¬£2m property warrants more scrutiny than a 55-year-old executive
  • The counterparties they deal with ‚Äî who they pay, and who pays them
  • The geographical areas in which they operate
  • The expected turnover on the account ‚Äî values, volumes and frequency of money in and out

This information creates a client profile. That profile is what allows you to detect when activity is unusual ‚Äî because "unusual" only has meaning when you have a baseline expectation.

üìã ICA Illustration ‚Äî Same CDD, Very Different Depth

How intensity varies within standard CDD

Client A: A salaried individual working for a multinational company who wants a mortgage. Standard ID verification, payslips, basic income check ‚Äî straightforward. Low risk.

Client B: A director of a private limited company seeking to purchase a ¬£3.5m commercial property via a corporate structure. Requires: company verification, ultimate beneficial ownership chain, source of company funds, confirmation of business purpose, and understanding of the transaction structure. More intensive, but still "standard" CDD if no PEP or high-risk country connection.

Both are applying standard CDD ‚Äî but the depth of enquiry differs enormously. The ICA expects calibration to the risk profile, not a one-size-fits-all form.

Identifying the Beneficial Owner

When the client is a legal entity ‚Äî a company, LLP, trust, or other structure ‚Äî you must look through it to identify the natural person (or persons) who ultimately own or control it. This is the beneficial owner.

The ICA is explicit: understanding legal structure alone is insufficient. You must understand the real ownership and control. Shell companies, nominee shareholders, and offshore structures are all routinely used to obscure beneficial ownership ‚Äî which is precisely why regulators require it to be identified.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Beneficial Owner Definition

For a corporate entity, the beneficial owner is the natural person(s) who ultimately own or control the customer through direct or indirect ownership of a sufficient percentage of shares or voting rights (typically 25%), or through other means of control. Where a trust is involved, beneficial owners include the settlor, the trustees, the beneficiaries, and any other natural person exercising effective control.

In a property transaction, you may need to apply CDD to multiple parties:

‚ö° Quick Check
A buyer is purchasing a property through a BVI-incorporated company. The registered director is a nominee company. The buyer tells you the ultimate owner is "a family trust." What is the correct CDD approach?
Correct. CDD requires looking through any corporate structure to the ultimate beneficial owner ‚Äî the natural person who ultimately owns or controls the entity. A nominee structure combined with an offshore jurisdiction and a trust arrangement are all classic layering techniques. Certificate of incorporation only confirms legal existence, not true ownership.

Simplified Due Diligence (SDD)

SDD is available where a firm determines that a business relationship or transaction presents a genuinely low ML/TF risk. It is a reduction in the depth and frequency of CDD measures ‚Äî not an exemption from them.

What SDD Is ‚Äî and Isn't

A common misconception is that SDD means doing nothing. It does not. SDD means a firm may apply reduced measures ‚Äî for example, less frequent monitoring, accepting a narrower range of verification documents, or inferring the purpose of a relationship from the type of transaction rather than specifically asking.

The crucial point: the decision to apply SDD must itself be documented and justifiable. You cannot simply decide a client "seems low risk." You must have assessed the relationship against a defined set of risk factors and concluded, on that evidence, that the risk is genuinely low.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî FATF on SDD

The FATF Interpretive Notes to Recommendation 10 specify that where SDD is applied, firms may reduce CDD measures, including by: (i) delaying verification of identity until after the business relationship is established, on condition that transactions remain below a defined monetary threshold; (ii) reducing the frequency of customer identification updates; (iii) reducing the degree of ongoing monitoring; and (iv) not collecting specific information or carrying out specific measures to understand the purpose and intended nature of the business relationship, but inferring the purpose and nature from the type of transactions or business relationship established. SDD requires a firm-level risk assessment ‚Äî it is not automatic for any category of client.

When SDD Might Be Appropriate

Examples of situations the ICA identifies as potentially qualifying for SDD include:

  • UK-regulated financial institutions (e.g. banks, building societies)
  • UK-listed companies subject to disclosure obligations
  • UK government bodies and public authorities
  • Products or transactions with a low transaction value and no cash element
  • Pension schemes or child savings products with documented membership criteria
‚ö†Ô∏è Property Sector Caution

SDD is rarely appropriate in residential property transactions. The combination of large cash sums, high value assets, and the sector's historic attractiveness to money launderers means the risk profile almost always demands at minimum standard CDD. Do not assume that because a buyer has a UK-regulated solicitor acting for them, you can apply SDD to the buyer directly ‚Äî that is a different protection, not a substitute for your own CDD.

The Common Mistake: SDD as a Default

The ICA consistently examines on the distinction between SDD as a considered, documented, risk-assessed decision versus SDD as a shortcut for busy or compliant-looking clients. The answer is always the same: SDD is not available merely because a client appears trustworthy or has a professional background. The decision must flow from a risk assessment, not from instinct.

‚ö° Quick Check
A conveyancer instructs Coadjute to conduct AML checks on a buyer. The buyer is a UK-registered solicitor buying a buy-to-let property. The conveyancer says: "Just do simplified CDD ‚Äî they're a regulated professional." Is this correct?
Correct. SDD requires the firm's own risk assessment to determine that this specific relationship presents low ML/TF risk. The decision must be documented and justifiable. Professional occupation is one factor ‚Äî it does not override the need for proper risk assessment.

Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)

Where standard CDD is the default, EDD is the mandatory response to higher-risk. It requires more intensive verification, senior management involvement, and a deeper understanding of the client's source of wealth and source of funds.

When Is EDD Mandatory?

The ICA and the UK MLR 2017 both specify categories where EDD is automatically required. You do not have discretion in these cases ‚Äî EDD must be applied:

  • Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs) ‚Äî including their close family and known associates
  • Customers from, or transactions involving, high-risk third countries ‚Äî as listed by FATF or the UK government
  • Correspondent banking relationships ‚Äî where one bank processes transactions on behalf of another
  • Any situation where the firm determines the risk to be higher based on its own assessment ‚Äî complex structures, unusual transaction patterns, unexplained wealth, etc.
üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The EDD Obligation

The ICA states that in higher-risk situations, firms must: obtain additional information on the customer and the beneficial owner; obtain additional information on the intended nature of the business relationship; obtain information on the source of funds and source of wealth of the customer and the beneficial owner; obtain information on the reasons for the intended or performed transactions; and increase the monitoring of the business relationship ‚Äî including greater scrutiny of transactions.

Source of Funds vs Source of Wealth

These are two distinct concepts that the ICA tests on separately. Understanding the difference is essential:

ConceptWhat it meansWhat it requires
Source of FundsThe specific money being used in this particular transaction ‚Äî where did the ¬£480,000 deposit come from?Bank statements, completion statement from a property sale, gift letter, HMRC records
Source of WealthThe origin of the client's overall wealth ‚Äî how did they accumulate what they have?Employment history, business ownership records, inheritance evidence, audited accounts, probate documentation

For EDD clients, you need both. "The funds came from my savings account" answers the source of funds question for the account ‚Äî but it does not answer the source of wealth question unless you know how the savings accumulated.

Insufficient Answers for EDD Source of Wealth

The ICA provides explicit guidance on answers that are insufficient for an EDD source of wealth enquiry. These are starting points for further questioning, not satisfactory answers:

  • "Savings" ‚Äî says nothing about how the savings were accumulated
  • "Profits from investments" ‚Äî which investments, over what period, in what amounts?
  • "Inheritance" ‚Äî from whom? What was the original source of that person's wealth?
  • "Business dealings" ‚Äî what business, where, with what turnover?
  • "Sale of business" ‚Äî what was the business, what did it generate, where are the sale proceeds documented?
üìã Property Sector EDD Example

A PEP Purchasing via Coadjute's Platform

A buyer is identified as a PEP ‚Äî a former government minister in an Eastern European state with a history of corruption. They are purchasing a ¬£2.8m residential property in London. They state source of funds as "business income from my consulting firm."

EDD response required:

  • Senior management sign-off required before proceeding
  • Name of consulting firm; nature of its work; revenue and profit history
  • Audited accounts or financial statements
  • Explanation of how consulting income supports a ¬£2.8m property purchase
  • Source of the specific funds used ‚Äî bank statements tracing the money from the consulting firm to the purchase funds
  • Background checks on the consulting firm's clients and activities
  • Ongoing enhanced monitoring of the relationship

Accepting "consulting income" without this detail would not meet the EDD standard. The ICA's test is not whether the answer is plausible ‚Äî it is whether it has been independently verified.

‚ö° Quick Check
A buyer subject to EDD states that their source of wealth is "inheritance from my late grandfather." Is this sufficient?
Correct. "Inheritance" is explicitly listed by the ICA as one of several insufficient source of wealth descriptions for EDD purposes. It is a starting point, not an answer. You must ask further questions and obtain independent evidence.

Plausibility Testing

Plausibility testing is the ICA's term for what is, at heart, professional common sense. It means stepping back from the documents and asking: does this client's story actually make sense?

What Is Plausibility Testing?

The ICA defines plausibility testing as "quite simply using common sense when applying CDD." It is the professional obligation to assess whether a client's stated circumstances, financial profile, and explanation of source of funds and wealth are credible ‚Äî given everything you know about them.

CDD is never a tick-box exercise. Even in a routine, low-risk case, each situation should be treated on its merits. Plausibility testing is the mental discipline that prevents CDD from becoming administrative box-ticking.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The ICA Plausibility Test Example

The manual sets out a specific example that appears in various forms in exam questions:

Client profile: 24-year-old single UK national, resident in UAE for 12 months. Wants to open current and savings accounts. Previously banked with a rival in UAE. Occupation: IT Consultant, income ~AED 150,000 ($41,000). Has a property in UAE valued at 5 million Dirhams AND one in Germany valued at ‚Ç¨13 million ‚Äî both rented, no mortgages.

ICA verdict: On the face of it, not plausible.

  • A large amount of assets for his age and stated income
  • How were the properties funded without mortgages on an IT consultant's salary?
  • How long has he had the properties? Were they purchased before employment?
  • What other income sources might exist?

The ICA notes there may be a rational explanation ‚Äî perhaps inherited wealth, an early tech exit, or family support. But: "unless you ask the question you will not know."

The Four Plausibility Testing Questions

The ICA sets out four key questions to work through when applying plausibility testing ‚Äî particularly for EDD clients:

  1. Are you convinced that the funds and wealth can be reasonably explained and established to be legitimate?
  2. Can you independently obtain evidence of the client's source of wealth for higher-risk accounts?
  3. Are you able to establish the relationship between the client and any third party where accounts are funded by a third party?
  4. Do you continue asking for information and persist in seeking clarity wherever circumstances are unclear or account structures are complex?

Property Sector Plausibility Scenarios

Plausibility testing is especially important in property transactions, where large sums change hands rapidly. Some common scenarios Coadjute's team should recognise:

Client StatementPlausibility QuestionWhat to Do
"I saved ¬£450,000 cash from my market stall over 10 years"What is a realistic annual profit from a market stall? Does the maths work? Is there any banking history showing gradual accumulation?Request trading accounts and bank statements showing gradual accumulation. Cash-heavy businesses are high-risk ‚Äî scrutinise closely.
"The funds are a gift from my family in Nigeria"Who is the family member? What is their source of wealth? Are they a PEP? What is their relationship to the buyer?Identify the third-party funder. Apply full CDD to them. Consider EDD if high-risk jurisdiction applies.
"I run a cash restaurant ‚Äî these are trading profits"What is the restaurant's turnover? What year did it open? Do the claimed profits match HMRC records?Request business accounts, HMRC self-assessment records, and bank statements. Cross-reference turnover with the claimed funds.
"I sold my cryptocurrency portfolio last year"What exchange? What coins? What was the purchase price vs. sale price? Are there records?Request exchange records and transaction history. Large crypto gains are possible but must be documented.
"My employer pays me partly in cash"Is this legal? Is there a contractual basis? Does it comply with minimum wage legislation?Request employment contract, payslips, and bank records. Off-the-books payments are a red flag.
üí° The Plausibility Mindset

The ICA does not expect you to be a private investigator or to prove guilt. It expects you to be a professional with common sense who asks questions when a story doesn't add up. If the explanation is legitimate, asking questions causes no harm. If it is not, asking questions may be the thing that prevents your firm from being used to launder the proceeds of crime.

‚ö° Quick Check
A 28-year-old buyer is purchasing a ¬£1.9m property in cash. They state they are a "self-employed trader." No further detail is provided. Applying the ICA's plausibility testing framework, what is the correct response?
Correct. Plausibility testing does not require you to refuse ‚Äî it requires you to ask. Age, transaction size, and vague occupational description together create a profile that warrants further enquiry. The ICA's position is: ask the question. If the answer is satisfactory and documented, you can proceed.

Ongoing Monitoring

CDD does not end when a business relationship is established. Regulation 28 of the MLR 2017 creates a continuing legal obligation to monitor ‚Äî ensuring that what you learn about a client over time remains consistent with what you know about them.

The Legal Basis: Regulation 28 MLR 2017

Regulation 28 requires firms to conduct ongoing monitoring of every business relationship. This has two distinct and mandatory components:

  1. Scrutiny of transactions ‚Äî ensuring that transactions being undertaken are consistent with the firm's knowledge of the customer, their business and risk profile, including, where necessary, the source of funds
  2. Keeping CDD documents, data and information up to date ‚Äî records must be kept current and reflect changes in the customer's circumstances
üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî Reg 28 Two-Part Obligation

The ICA tests on the distinction between the two components of Regulation 28. Both are mandatory. Scrutiny of transactions alone is insufficient ‚Äî you must also keep records current. A firm that monitors activity but never updates its CDD records would not comply with Reg 28.

What Transaction Scrutiny Means in Practice

Transaction scrutiny is not about reviewing every payment manually. It is about having systems and processes that flag transactions that are inconsistent with the known profile of the customer. This means:

  • Understanding what level of activity is normal for each client
  • Noticing when transactions change in size, frequency, counterparty, or jurisdiction
  • Identifying transactions that serve no apparent commercial or logical purpose
  • Escalating concerns to the MLRO when something doesn't fit the profile

Keeping CDD Records Up to Date

The ICA is clear: CDD records must be reviewed and updated when the firm becomes aware of changes in the client's circumstances. Examples include:

  • A change in the client's occupation or business activity
  • A change in the ownership structure of a corporate client
  • The client becoming a PEP (e.g. being appointed to public office)
  • The client's country of residence or business changing to a high-risk jurisdiction
  • Expiry of identity documents
‚ö†Ô∏è The "Set and Forget" Failure

A major compliance failure the ICA highlights is treating CDD as a one-time event at onboarding. A client who was low-risk in 2020 may be high-risk in 2024. A client who was a private individual may now be a government minister. The obligation is ongoing ‚Äî the risk is ongoing ‚Äî and the monitoring must be ongoing.

Risk-Based Monitoring Frequency

How often should ongoing monitoring occur? The ICA's answer is: it depends on the risk. Higher-risk clients require more intensive and frequent monitoring. The ICA suggests frameworks such as:

  • 30-, 60- or 90-day client reviews for higher-risk relationships
  • Annual client reviews for standard-risk relationships, ideally conducted by someone other than the primary relationship manager
  • Event-triggered reviews ‚Äî any significant change in circumstances, transaction pattern, or risk status should prompt an immediate review regardless of the scheduled cycle

CDD in Property: The Ongoing Obligation

For Coadjute's purposes, the most relevant ongoing monitoring obligation arises where we maintain a continuing relationship with an estate agent or conveyancer, and where repeat transactions occur. If a buyer or seller appears multiple times in transactions, the CDD on file must be current and the historical risk profile must be reconsidered in light of each new transaction.

A one-time buyer purchasing a residential property is, in most cases, a transaction relationship that concludes. But where a client purchases multiple properties through the same agent, or where a developer transacts repeatedly, the relationship becomes ongoing ‚Äî and ongoing monitoring obligations apply fully.

‚ö° Quick Check
Regulation 28 of the MLR 2017 requires ongoing monitoring to include: (select the most complete and accurate answer)
Correct. Regulation 28 has two distinct requirements: (a) transaction scrutiny ‚Äî ensuring transactions match the customer's known profile; and (b) keeping CDD records current. Both are mandatory. Neither an annual face-to-face review nor automatic re-verification on a fixed schedule is the standard ‚Äî it is risk-sensitive and responsive to change.

Identifying Suspicious Activity

Good CDD is what makes suspicious activity detectable. Without a baseline understanding of a client's expected behaviour, you cannot recognise when something is wrong. This section covers how to identify red flags, what "suspicion" means legally, and when to act.

What Is a "Red Flag"?

A red flag is an indicator ‚Äî a piece of information, a pattern of behaviour, or a characteristic of a transaction ‚Äî that suggests the possibility of ML or TF. Red flags are not proof of wrongdoing. They are signals that warrant closer examination.

The ICA categorises red flags across several dimensions. In the property sector, the most significant categories are:

CategoryRed Flags
Source of FundsUnexplained cash; funds from a third party with no clear relationship; overseas funds from high-risk jurisdictions; large cash deposits shortly before purchase
Transaction BehaviourUrgency to complete; indifference to price or terms; purchase significantly above market value; rapid resale at a loss; multiple transactions in short succession
Identity & OwnershipComplex offshore corporate structures; nominee arrangements; reluctance to identify beneficial owner; changing ownership structure mid-transaction
Client BehaviourRefusal to provide information; unusual secrecy; changes in story; using multiple advisers for a simple transaction; avoiding face-to-face contact
Property CharacteristicsProperty purchased and immediately rented out at below-market rent; unusual property modifications; property rarely occupied after purchase

The Legal Threshold: What Does "Suspicious" Mean?

The ICA spends considerable time on this question, because the answer matters legally. You do not need to know that money laundering is occurring to have a reporting obligation. The legal threshold is lower: you need reasonable grounds to suspect.

This means a suspicion that is based on objective facts ‚Äî not a feeling, but information that would cause a reasonable person in your position to think something might be wrong. It is deliberately set below certainty, because the law wants concerns reported early, not after ML has been completed and the money has moved on.

üìò ICA Exam Note ‚Äî The Suspicion Threshold

The ICA distinguishes between three levels:

  • Knowledge ‚Äî you know ML is taking place. This is the highest threshold and rarely the trigger.
  • Suspicion ‚Äî you think ML is possible, based on objective grounds. This triggers the internal reporting obligation.
  • Reasonable grounds to suspect ‚Äî an objective standard: would a reasonable, informed professional in your position have reached the same conclusion? This is the standard applied to determine whether a SAR was appropriate.

The ICA also notes: you are not required to suspect a specific predicate offence. Suspicion that something criminal is occurring ‚Äî even if you cannot identify what ‚Äî is sufficient to trigger reporting.

What Happens When a Red Flag Is Identified?

When you encounter a red flag, the response is not to refuse the transaction or challenge the client. It is to escalate internally. Specifically:

  1. Do not act on the transaction ‚Äî do not complete, proceed, or take any further steps until the concern has been assessed
  2. Report internally to the MLRO ‚Äî via your firm's internal reporting procedure. At Coadjute, this means escalating through the designated compliance channel
  3. Do not tip off the client ‚Äî you must not tell the client that a report has been made or that they are under suspicion (this is a criminal offence ‚Äî covered in Module 6)
  4. The MLRO decides ‚Äî whether to make an external SAR to the National Crime Agency (NCA), and whether to consent to the transaction proceeding
‚ö†Ô∏è The "I Wasn't Sure" Defence

A common misconception: "I thought it might be suspicious, but I wasn't certain, so I didn't report." The ICA is clear this is not a valid defence. The reporting obligation arises at the point of suspicion ‚Äî not certainty. If you had reasonable grounds to suspect and did not report, you may have committed a criminal offence under POCA 2002.

‚ö° Quick Check
A staff member suspects unusual activity on a transaction but cannot identify what specific crime has been committed. They decide not to file an internal report because "I can't name the offence." According to the ICA, is this reasoning correct?
Correct. You are not required to identify a specific predicate offence. The ICA is explicit: suspicion of criminality ‚Äî even at a general level ‚Äî is sufficient to trigger the internal reporting obligation. The role of the MLRO, and ultimately the NCA, is to investigate. Your role is to report your concern.
